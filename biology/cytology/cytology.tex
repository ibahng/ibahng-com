\section{Macromolecules}

  \subsection{Macromolecule Basics}

    \begin{core}{Polymers \$ Monomers}

      Large carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids are classified as \textbf{macromolecules} due to their enormous molecular size. These macromolecules exist as chain-like structures known as \textbf{polymers}, which are built from many smaller repeating subunits called \textbf{monomers}.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Dehydration \& Hydrolysis Reactions}

      Cells can both assemble and disassemble polymers through two complementary chemical processes: \textbf{dehydration (condensation) reactions} and \textbf{hydrolysis reactions}. During dehydration reactions, two monomers become covalently linked through the \textbf{removal of a water molecule}, creating a new bond that extends the polymer chain. Conversely, hydrolysis reactions break these covalent bonds by the \textbf{addition of a water molecule}, splitting polymers back into their constituent monomers.

      \bce{\text{(\textit{Dehydration Synthesis})} \ce{A-OH + HO-B -> A-O-B + H2O}}

      \bce{\text{(\textit{Hydrolysis})} \ce{A-O-B + H2O -> A-OH + B-OH}}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Lipids}

    \textbf{Lipids} are unique among the major classes of biological macromolecules in that they do not form true polymers. Unlike carbohydrates, proteins, and nucleic acids, lipids are largely \textbf{hydrophobic} (\textit{water-fearing}) molecules composed primarily of hydrocarbons with nonpolar covalent bonds. The most biologically important lipids include \textbf{fats}, \textbf{phospholipids}, and \textbf{steroids}.

  \subsection{Proteins}

    \textbf{Proteins} account for more than 50\% of the dry mass of most cells and are the most functionally diverse class of biological macromolecules. These versatile polymers are constructed from monomeric building blocks called \textbf{amino acids}.

    \begin{core}{Amino Acids}

      There are 20 standard amino acids commonly incorporated into proteins. Each amino acid shares a common structural core---an amino group and a carboxyl group bonded to a central carbon--but possesses a distinctive side chain (\textit{R group}) that determines its unique chemical properties.

    \begin{core}{Protein Structural Hierarchy}

      Proteins perform a wide range of functions, including catalyzing biochemical reactions (\textbf{enzymes}), providing structural support, facilitating cell signaling, and transporting molecules. A protein is a polymer of amino acids linked by \textbf{peptide bonds}, and its specific function is determined by its precise three-dimensional structure. This structure is organized into four hierarchical levels: primary, secondary, tertiary, and quaternary structure.

      \myfig{0.6}{cytology_figures/pstructures.png}{caption}{fig:fig12}

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item The \textbf{primary} structure represents the unique linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain, determined by the order in which amino acids are covalently bonded during protein synthesis.

        \item The \textbf{secondary} structure refers to localized folding patterns that arise from hydrogen bonding between atoms in the polypeptide backbone. The two most common secondary structures are the \textbf{α-helix}, a right-handed coil, and the \textbf{β-pleated sheet}, formed by adjacent polypeptide strands.

        \item The \textbf{tertiary} structure is the overall three-dimensional shape of a complete polypeptide, stabilized by interactions among amino acid side chains (\textit{R groups}). These interactions include hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and covalent disulfide bridges.

        \item The \textbf{quaternary} structure arises when multiple polypeptide subunits associate to form a functional protein complex. Not all proteins exhibit quaternary structure---only those composed of two or more polypeptide chains.

      \end{enumerate}

      The precise three-dimensional structure of a protein is directly linked to its function, and even small changes in folding can render a protein nonfunctional. This structure-function relationship underlies the remarkable versatility of proteins, making them essential molecular machines for virtually every biological process.

    \end{core}

\section{Cell Overview}

  Cells are the fundamental units of life---every living organism, from the simplest bacterium to the most complex multicellular plant or animal, is composed of one or more cells. By studying cellular structure and function, biologists can understand the molecular basis of how organisms grow, reproduce, respond to stimuli, and interact with their environment.
    
  \subsection{Prokaryotic and Eukaryotic Cells}

    Despite the remarkable diversity of life, all cells share several fundamental features that define them as living units. Every cell is enclosed by a selective barrier called the \textbf{plasma membrane}, which controls the passage of substances into and out of the cell. Within this membrane lies the \textbf{cytosol}, a semifluid, gel-like substance in which cellular components are suspended. All cells also contain \textbf{chromosomes}, DNA-containing structures that carry genetic information. Beyond these universal features, cells are broadly classified into two fundamentally different types: \textbf{prokaryotic} and \textbf{eukaryotic}.

    \begin{core}{Prokaryotic Cells}

      \textbf{Prokaryotic} cells---which include the domains Bacteria and Archaea---are characterized by the absence of a membrane-enclosed nucleus. Instead, their DNA is concentrated in an irregularly shaped region called the \textbf{nucleoid}, which is not separated from the cytosol by a membrane. Prokaryotes also lack other membrane-bound organelles, making them structurally simpler than eukaryotic cells. They are generally smaller in size and most exist as single-celled organisms.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/prokaryotic_cell}{Prokaryotic cell}{fig:fig18}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Eukaryotic Cells}

      \textbf{Eukaryotic} cells---found in animals, plants, fungi, and protists---are distinguished by the presence of a true, membrane-bound nucleus that houses their genetic material. Beyond the nucleus, eukaryotic cells contain an extensive system of membrane-bound organelles, each specialized for specific functions, making them considerably larger and more structurally complex than prokaryotes. Among eukaryotes, \textbf{animal cells} and \textbf{plant cells} represent the two most commonly studied types. While they share many organelles, including mitochondria (\autoref{core:mito}), endoplasmic reticulum (\autoref{core:er}), and Golgi apparatus (\autoref{core:golgi}), plant cells possess unique structures: \textbf{chloroplasts} for photosynthesis (\autoref{core:chl}), a rigid \textbf{cell wall} exterior (\autoref{core:cw}) to the plasma membrane, and a large \textbf{central vacuole} (\autoref{core:vac}) for storage and maintaining turgor pressure.

      \myfig{0.85}{cytology_figures/ap_cell}{Eukaryotic cells}{fig:fig19}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Cell Organelles}

    The following sections examine the major organelles found in cells and their specialized functions.

    \begin{core}[label=core:nuc]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Nucleus})}

      \sbs{0.6}{
        The \textbf{nucleus} houses most of the genetic material (\textit{DNA}) within a protective double membrane called the \textbf{nuclear envelope}. This envelope is perforated by nuclear pores that regulate the passage of molecules between the nucleus and cytoplasm. Within the nucleus lies the \textbf{nucleolus}, a distinct region where ribosomal RNA (\textit{rRNA}) is synthesized and ribosomal subunits are assembled before export to the cytoplasm.

        The DNA within the nucleus is not randomly dispersed but rather organized into discrete structures called \textbf{chromosomes}. Each chromosome consists of a single, extremely long DNA molecule intimately associated with proteins that help package and regulate the DNA.
      }{
        \myfig{}{cytology_figures/nucleus}{Nucleus}{fig:fig21}
      }
  
    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:rib]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Ribosomes})}

      \sbs{0.6}{
        \textbf{Ribosomes} are molecular machines composed of ribosomal RNA (\textit{rRNA}) and associated proteins that catalyze \textbf{protein synthesis}, translating genetic information from messenger RNA into polypeptide chains. Ribosomes exist in two functional populations within the cell.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item \textbf{Free ribosomes} float freely in the cytosol and typically synthesize proteins destined for use within the cytoplasm.

          \item \textbf{Bound ribosomes}, which are attached to the endoplasmic reticulum (CORE CONCEPT REFERENCE) or nuclear envelope, generally produce proteins destined for secretion or membrane insertion.

        \end{itemize}
      }{
        \myfig{0.85}{cytology_figures/ribosome}{Ribosome}{fig:fig23}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:er]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Endoplasmic Reticulum})}

      \sbs{0.5}{
        The \textbf{endoplasmic reticulum (ER)} is an extensive network of interconnected membranes that exists in two distinct forms: smooth ER and rough ER, each specialized for different functions.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item The \textbf{smooth ER} lacks ribosomes and synthesizes lipids, metabolizes carbohydrates, detoxifies drugs and poisons, and stores calcium ions. 

          \item The \textbf{rough ER}, studded with bound ribosomes, produces proteins intended for secretion, incorporation into membranes, or delivery to other organelles.

        \end{itemize}
      }{
        \myfig{0.8}{cytology_figures/er}{Endoplasmic reticulum}{fig:fig24}
      }

      Newly synthesized proteins are packaged into transport vesicles that bud from the rough ER and ferry their cargo to the Golgi apparatus for further processing.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:golgi]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Golgi Apparatus})}

      \sbs{0.6}{
        The \textbf{Golgi apparatus} consists of polarized, membrane-bound sacs called \textbf{cisternae} that modify, sort, and package proteins and lipids received from the ER. Materials enter at the \textbf{cis face} and migrate through the medial stacks, where they undergo enzymatic processing such as \textbf{glycosylation}---the addition or trimming of sugar chains to create functional glycoproteins and glycolipids.

        At the \textbf{trans face}, molecules are sorted into vesicles, which then bud off for three primary pathways: \textbf{exocytosis} for secretion, integration into the \textbf{plasma membrane}, or the formation of \textbf{lysosomes} for intracellular digestion. This regulated trafficking ensures that finished products reach their specific functional destinations.
      }{
        \myfig{0.8}{cytology_figures/ga}{Golgi apparatus}{fig:fig25}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:lyso]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Lysosome})}

      \sbs{0.6}{
        \textbf{Lysosomes} are membrane-bound organelles containing a diverse array of hydrolytic enzymes capable of digesting macromolecules, damaged organelles, and engulfed materials. These enzymes function optimally in the acidic environment actively maintained by proton pumps. This acidic pH is crucial for enzymatic activity and provides a safety mechanism---should the lysosome rupture, the enzymes would be inactive in the neutral pH of the cytosol, protecting the cell from self-digestion.

        Lysosomes play a critical role in recycling the cell's own components through \textbf{autophagy}, a process in which damaged or obsolete organelles are engulfed and degraded, allowing their molecular components to be reused by the cell.
      }{
        \myfig{0.9}{cytology_figures/lysosome}{Lysosome}{fig:fig26}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:vac]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Vacuoles})}

      \textbf{Vacuoles} are membrane-bound organelles derived from the endoplasmic reticulum and Golgi apparatus that serve diverse functions across different cell types.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Food vacuoles} (\textit{also called phagosomes}) form through \textbf{phagocytosis} (CORE CONCEPT REFERENCE). These vacuoles subsequently fuse with lysosomes to break down the engulfed material into usable nutrients.
        
        \item \textbf{Contractile vacuoles} in freshwater protists function as osmoregulatory organelles that actively pump out excess water to maintain osmotic balance.
        
        \item Plant cells contain a large \textbf{central vacuole} that serves multiple essential functions: it stores water, inorganic ions, nutrients, pigments, and metabolic wastes. Critically, the central vacuole maintains \textbf{turgor pressure}---the outward pressure of the vacuolar fluid against the cell wall---which provides rigidity to the cell and supports the plant body, allowing herbaceous plants to stand upright.

      \end{itemize}

      \myfig{0.6}{cytology_figures/vacuole}{Plant cell central vacuole}{fig:fig27}

    \end{core}    

    The nuclear envelope, endoplasmic reticulum, Golgi apparatus, lysosomes, vacuoles, and plasma membrane (\autoref{core:phosbi}) are functionally interconnected components of the \textbf{endomembrane system}.

    These organelles work together through vesicular transport---small membrane-bound sacs bud from one organelle, travel through the cytosol, and fuse with another, delivering proteins and lipids to their destinations. This integrated network coordinates protein synthesis and modification, lipid metabolism, intracellular transport of macromolecules, and cellular secretion, enabling eukaryotic cells to compartmentalize and regulate complex biochemical processes.

    \begin{core}[label=core:mito]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Mitochondria})}

      \sbs{0.6}{
        \textbf{Mitochondria} are the sites of \textbf{cellular respiration} (CORE CONCEPT REFERENCE), the metabolic process that generates ATP by extracting energy from sugars and other organic molecules in the presence of oxygen. 

        These organelles possess a distinctive double-membrane structure: the smooth outer membrane and the highly folded inner membrane, whose folds form projections called \textbf{cristae} that increase surface area for ATP synthesis. The interior compartment---the \textbf{mitochondrial matrix}---contains the enzymes for the \textbf{citric acid cycle} (CORE CONCEPT REFERENCE). Mitochondria are present in nearly all eukaryotic cells, reflecting the universal need for efficient energy production.
      }{
        \myfig{0.9}{cytology_figures/mitochondria}{Mitochondria}{fig:fig28}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:chl]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Chloroplast})}

      \textbf{Chloroplasts}, found in plants and algae, are the sites of \textbf{photosynthesis} (CORE CONCEPT REFERENCE), converting light energy into chemical energy stored in sugars.

      Like mitochondria, chloroplasts possess a double membrane and contain their own DNA and ribosomes. Within the chloroplast lies an internal membrane system of disc-shaped sacs called \textbf{thylakoids}, which stack to form structures called \textbf{grana}. The thylakoid membranes contain chlorophyll and other pigments that capture light energy. Surrounding the thylakoids is the \textbf{stroma}, a fluid-filled space containing enzymes for the \textbf{Calvin cycle} (CORE CONCEPT REFERENCE), which synthesizes sugars from carbon dioxide.

      \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/chloroplast}{Chloroplasts}{fig:fig29}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:cyto]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Cytoskeleton})}

      The \textbf{cytoskeleton} is a dynamic network of protein fibers that maintains cell shape, anchors organelles, enables cellular movement, and facilitates intracellular transport. This structural framework consists of three distinct types of fibers, each with specialized functions.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Microtubules} are hollow tubes composed of the protein \textbf{tubulin} arranged in a cylindrical structure. These largest cytoskeletal fibers serve as tracks along which motor proteins transport organelles and vesicles, form the mitotic spindle that separates chromosomes during cell division (CORE CONCEPT REFERENCE), and constitute the structural core of \textbf{cilia} and \textbf{flagella}. In animal cells, microtubules radiate outward from the \textbf{centrosome}, an organizing center containing a pair of cylindrical structures called \textbf{centrioles}.
        }{
          \myfig{0.9}{cytology_figures/microtubules}{Microtubules}{fig:fig30}
        }

        \item \textbf{Microfilaments}, also called \textbf{actin filaments}, are the thinnest cytoskeletal components, consisting of twisted double chains of the protein \textbf{actin}. These filaments resist tensile forces, maintain cell shape, and enable various forms of cellular movement. In muscle cells, actin filaments interact with the motor protein \textbf{myosin} to generate the sliding forces that produce muscle contraction.

        \myfig{0.7}{cytology_figures/microfilament}{Microfilament}{fig:fig31}

        \item \textbf{Intermediate filaments} are rope-like structures with diameters between those of microtubules and microfilaments. In contrast to the rapidly assembling and disassembling microtubules and microfilaments, intermediate filaments form more \textbf{permanent} structures that provide mechanical stability to cells. They reinforce cell shape and anchor organelles in fixed positions.S

        \myfig{0.65}{cytology_figures/intermediate_filament}{Intermediate filament}{fig:fig32}

      \end{enumerate}

      Together, these three cytoskeletal components form an integrated network that adapts to the cell's changing structural and functional needs throughout its life cycle.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:cw]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Cell Walls})}

      \sbs{0.5}{
        The \textbf{cell wall} is a rigid extracellular structure that surrounds the plasma membrane in plant cells, fungi, and many prokaryotes, but is absent in animal cells. Composed primarily of the polysaccharide \textbf{cellulose} in plants (\textit{and \textbf{chitin} in fungi}), the cell wall provides structural support, protects the cell from mechanical damage and pathogens, and prevents excessive water uptake that would otherwise cause the cell to burst via \textbf{osmotic lysis} (CORE CONCEPT REFERENCE).
      }{
        \myfig{0.75}{cytology_figures/cell_wall}{Cell wall}{fig:fig33}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:ecm]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Extracellular Matrix})}

      \sbs{0.6}{
        Animal cells, lacking the rigid cell walls, instead produce an \textbf{extracellular matrix (ECM)}, a complex, dynamic network of glycoproteins and polysaccharides secreted into the extracellular space surrounding and between cells.

        The ECM is composed primarily of the fibrous structural protein \textbf{collagen}, \textbf{proteoglycans}, and \textbf{fibronectin}, an adhesive glycoprotein that helps organize the matrix. These ECM components bind to transmembrane receptor proteins called \textbf{integrins}, which physically anchor the cell to the matrix and relay signals bidirectionally between the external environment and the cell interior, linking the ECM to the cytoskeleton. The ECM provides essential structural support, facilitates cell adhesion and migration, regulates tissue organization and development, and influences cell behavior, differentiation, and gene expression through various signaling pathways.
      }{
        \myfig{0.9}{cytology_figures/ecm}{Extracellular matrix}{fig:fig34}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:ij]{Cell Organelles (\textit{Intercellular Junctions})}

      In multicellular organisms, cells are organized into tissues, organs, and organ systems, requiring specialized structures for cell-to-cell communication and adhesion. \textbf{Intercellular junctions} are sites of direct physical contact between neighboring cells that serve these functions. Plant and animal cells have evolved distinct types of junctions suited to their cellular architecture.

      In plant cells, channels called \textbf{plasmodesmata} penetrate adjacent cell walls, creating cytoplasmic bridges that allow direct exchange of water, nutrients, and signaling molecules between neighboring cells.

      \myfig{0.45}{cytology_figures/plasmodesmata}{Plasmodesmata}{fig:fig35}

      Animal cells, lacking cell walls, instead rely on specialized intercellular junctions to connect and communicate.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Tight junctions} seal adjacent cells together by fusing their plasma membranes, preventing leakage of substances through the intercellular space. They are especially prominent in epithelial tissues, such as the gut lining, where strict control of molecular passage is essential.

        \item \textbf{Desmosomes} mechanically anchor cells to one another via intermediate filaments, conferring tissue-level structural strength. They are particularly abundant in tissues subject to mechanical stress, such as cardiac muscle and skin.

        \item \textbf{Gap junctions} form direct communication channels between cells via proteins called \textbf{connexins}, permitting the passage of small molecules, ions, and electrical signals. This makes them especially important in coordinating rapid responses.

      \end{itemize}

      \myfig{0.47}{cytology_figures/animal_junctions}{Intercellular junctions of animal cells}{fig:fig36}

    \end{core}

\section{Membrane Structure and Function}

  \subsection{Membrane Basics}

    \begin{core}[label=core:phosbi]{Phospholipid Bilayer}

      \sbs{0.5}{
        The cell membrane is an essential structural and functional component of all cells, serving as a selective barrier that governs the transport of substances across the cellular boundary. Its architecture is defined by the \textbf{phospholipid bilayer}, comprising two opposing layers of phospholipids in which the hydrophilic (\textit{water-attracting}) heads face the aqueous environments on either side, while the hydrophobic (\textit{water-repelling}) tails are sequestered in the interior. This arrangement arises from the \textbf{amphipathic} nature of phospholipids, which possess both \textbf{polar} and \textit{nonpolar} regions within the same molecule.
      }{
        \myfig{0.9}{cytology_figures/bilayer}{Phospholipid bilayer}{fig:fig37}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Fluid Mosaic Model}

      The \textbf{fluid mosaic model} conceptualizes the cell membrane as a dynamic, fluid structure in which a diverse array of proteins are embedded within or associated with the phospholipid bilayer, collectively forming a complex molecular mosaic.

      \myfig{0.85}{cytology_figures/fluid_model}{Fluid mosaic model}{fig:fig38}

      The membrane is characterized as \textbf{fluid} because its constituent phospholipids and proteins are capable of lateral movement within the bilayer---analogous to icebergs drifting across the surface of the sea. This fluidity is attributed to two principal factors.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item The \textbf{unsaturated nature of the hydrocarbon tails} ntroduces steric irregularities that prevent tight packing.

        \item The \textbf{presence of cholesterol} in animal cell membranes modulates phospholipid spacing and maintains membrane flexibility.

      \end{enumerate}

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/fluid_reason}{Membrane fluidity}{fig:fig39}

      The membrane further harbors a functionally diverse complement of proteins, collectively mediating processes that include transmembrane transport, enzymatic catalysis, signal transduction, cell-cell recognition, intercellular joining, and cytoskeleton-ECM attachment.

      \myfig{0.8}{cytology_figures/membrane_functions}{Types of membrane proteins}{fig:fig40}

      In addition to lipids and proteins, \textbf{carbohydrates} constitute a significant class of membrane-associated molecules. These are covalently bonded either to lipids, forming \textbf{glycolipids}, or to proteins, forming \textbf{glycoproteins}, and collectively mediate \textbf{cell-cell recognition and intercellular communication}.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Selective Permeability}

      The cell membrane is characterized by \textbf{selective permeability}---the property by which certain substances are permitted to traverse the membrane while others are excluded. Small, nonpolar molecules such as \ce{O2} and \ce{CO2} diffuse freely across the lipid bilayer, whereas larger polar molecules and ions, by virtue of their hydrophilic nature, require specialized \textbf{transport proteins} to facilitate their passage.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Passive Transport}

    \begin{core}{Passive Transport}

      \textbf{Passive transport} refers to the movement of substances across the membrane driven exclusively by thermodynamic gradients, requiring \textbf{no expenditure of cellular energy}.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Simple Diffusion}

      \textbf{Simple diffiusion} is passive transport that proceeds \textbf{without the involvement of membrane proteins}, whereby substances migrate down their \textbf{concentration gradient}---from regions of higher to lower concentration---until thermodynamic equilibrium is established, as exemplified by the transmembrane movement of \ce{O2} and \ce{CO2}.

      \myfig{0.6}{cytology_figures/simple}{Simple diffusion}{fig:fig41}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Facilitated Diffusion}

      \sbs{0.45}{
        \textbf{Facilitated diffusion} is passive transport that \textbf{requires the involvement of membrane proteins}, whereby specific molecules---such as glucose and ions---traverse the membrane with the assistance of dedicated transport proteins. As with simple diffusion, transport occurs down the concentration gradient.

        Facilitated diffusion is mediated by two principal classes of transport proteins: \textbf{channel proteins} and \textbf{carrier proteins}.
      }{
        \myfig{0.85}{cytology_figures/facilitated}{Channel (\textit{left}) and carrier (\textit{right}) proteins}{fig:fig43}
      }

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Channel proteins} form hydrophilic pores that permit the passage of specific ions or molecules across the membrane. A prominent example is \textbf{ion channels}, which selectively permit the passage of ions such as \ce{Na+} or \ce{K+}, and frequently function as \textbf{gated channels} that open or close in response to stimuli such as chemical signals or changes in electrical potential. These ion channels are explored further in CELLULAR SIGNALING AUTOREF.

        \item \textbf{Carrier proteins} bind to specific substrate molecules and undergo conformational changes to facilitate their translocation across the membrane. A well-characterized example is the glucose transporter, which mediates the uptake of glucose into the cell.

      \end{itemize}

      The principal distinction between these two classes lies in their mechanism of transport: channel proteins form \textbf{open, continuous pores} through which molecules diffuse freely, whereas carrier proteins undergo \textbf{induced conformational changes} to shuttle specific molecules across the membrane. Consequently, carrier proteins exhibit greater selectivity but operate at comparatively lower transport rates than channel proteins.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Osmosis \& Tonicity}

      \textbf{Osmosis}---the passive movement of water across a selectively permeable membrane from regions of lower to higher solute concentration---is exemplified by the \textbf{aquaporin}, a channel protein that facilitates the transmembrane transport of water molecules.

      Osmosis is driven by \textbf{tonicity}, which characterizes the ability of a surrounding solution to induce a net gain or loss of water in a cell. When a solution is \textbf{hypotonic}, \textbf{isotonic}, or \textbf{hypertonic} relative to the cell, it contains a lower, equal, or higher concentration of solutes than the intracellular environment, respectively. As a consequence, cells placed in a hypotonic solution undergo a net influx of water, while those placed in a hypertonic solution experience a net efflux. In either case, the system tends toward \textbf{isotonicity}, reflecting the thermodynamic drive to equilibrate solute concentrations across the membrane.

      A consequent of differential tonicity is that animal cells, which lack a rigid cell wall, are susceptible to \textbf{lysis} in hypotonic solutions, wherein excessive water influx causes the cell to rupture. Plant cells, by contrast, are protected from this outcome by their cell walls, instead becoming \textbf{turgid} under hypotonic conditions---a state of rigidity that confers structural support. In hypertonic solutions, both animal and plant cells experience a net efflux of water and subsequent shrinkage; in plant cells, this manifests as \textbf{plasmolysis}, a phenomenon in which the cell membrane retracts from the cell wall as the cytoplasm contracts.

      \myfig{0.85}{cytology_figures/tonicity}{Tonicity consequences in animal and plant cells}{fig:fig42}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Active Transport}

    \begin{core}{Active Transport}

      \textbf{Active transport} refers to the \textbf{energy-dependent} movement of substances across the membrane, typically driven by the hydrolysis of \textbf{ATP}. Unlike passive transport, active transport enables cells to maintain concentration gradients that deviate from thermodynamic equilibrium, facilitating the translocation of substances \textit{against} their concentration gradient. All proteins implicated in active transport belong to the class of \textbf{carrier proteins}, several prominent examples of which are outlined below.

    \end{core}    

    \begin{core}{Sodium-Potassium Pump}

      The \textbf{sodium-potassium pump} is an ATP-driven carrier protein that translocates 3 \ce{Na+} ions out of the cell and 2 \ce{K+} ions into the cell, both \textbf{against} their respective concentration gradients.

      This pump is integral to establishing and maintaining the \textbf{membrane potential} of animal cells---defined as the voltage difference across the membrane arising from the asymmetric distribution of ions on either side. Analogous to a battery (EXTERNAL REFERENCE TO ELECTROCHEMICAL CELLS), the membrane potential represents a form of stored electrochemical energy that can be harnessed to drive other cellular processes. The combined influence of the chemical driving force (\textit{concentration gradient}) and the electrical driving force (\textit{membrane potential}) constitutes the \textbf{electrochemical gradient}, which governs the net movement of ions across the membrane. \textit{It is worth noting that this electrochemical component is relevant only to charged species; uncharged molecules such as glucose are influenced solely by their concentration gradient.}

      \myfig{0.65}{cytology_figures/na_k_pump}{Sodium-potassium pump}{fig:fig44}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Proton Pump}

      The \textbf{proton pump} represents another well-characterized example of active transport, functioning by translocating \ce{H+} ions (\textit{protons}) across the membrane to establish a proton concentration gradient. This electrochemical gradient serves as a form of stored potential energy that can subsequently be harnessed to drive energetically demanding cellular processes, most notably ATP synthesis in the mitochondria (ETC AUTOREF).

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/proton_pump}{Proton pump}{fig:fig45}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Sucrose-Proton Cotransporter}

      The mechanisms of active transport discussed thus far have involved the direct hydrolysis of ATP to drive the translocation of substances against their concentration gradients. However, a distinct mode of active transport---termed \textbf{cotransport}---couples the thermodynamically favorable "downhill" diffusion of one substance to the "uphill" translocation of another against its concentration gradient, thereby circumventing the direct requirement for ATP.

      A well-characterized example is the \textbf{sucrose-\ce{H+} cotransporter} of plant cells, which harnesses the potential energy stored in the proton gradient established by the proton pump to drive the uptake of sucrose against its concentration gradient, while simultaneously permitting \ce{H+} ions to diffuse down their electrochemical gradient.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/cotransport}{Sucrose-proton cotransporter}{fig:fig46}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Exocytosis \& Endocytosis}

    \begin{core}{Bulk Transport}

      The mechanisms of passive and active transport described above account for the movement of small molecules and ions across the cell membrane. However, larger macromolecules---such as proteins and polysaccharides---cannot be accommodated by these protein-mediated transport mechanisms. Instead, their translocation is achieved through \textbf{bulk transport}, a process by which large particles or macromolecules are conveyed across the membrane enclosed within membrane-bound vesicles. Bulk transport is broadly classified as either \textbf{exocytosis} (\textit{\autoref{core:exocytosis}}) or \textbf{endocytosis} (\textit{\autoref{core:endocytosis}}).

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:exocytosis]{Exocytosis}

      \textbf{Exocytosis} occurs when an intracellular vesicle fuses with the plasma membrane, releasing its contents into the extracellular environment. This mechanism is widely employed for the secretion of proteins processed through the Golgi apparatus---including hormones and enzymes---as well as for the expulsion of cellular waste materials.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/exocytosis}{Exocytosis}{fig:fig47}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:endocytosis]{Endocytosis}

      \textbf{Endocytosis} occurs when the cell takes in macromolecules by forming vesicles from the plasma membrane, the method by which can be categorized into the following types.

      \myfig{0.75}{cytology_figures/endocytosis}{Phagocytosis, pinocytosis, and receptor-mediated endocytosis}{fig:fig48}

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Phagocytosis} (\textit{cellular eating}) involves the engulfment of large particles or entire cells, achieved by the extension of \textbf{pseudopodia} around the target, culminating in the formation of a food vacuole. The food vacuole subsequently fuses with a lysosome to facilitate digestion, as described in \autoref{core:vac}.

        \item \textbf{Pinocytosis} (\textit{cellular drinking}) involves the nonspecific uptake of extracellular fluid and its dissolved solutes through the formation of small membrane-bound vesicles. As this process is indiscriminate, the cell internalizes all solutes present in the surrounding extracellular fluid.

        \item \textbf{Receptor-mediated endocytosis} is a \textbf{highly selective} form of endocytosis in which specific receptor proteins on the cell surface bind target molecules---termed \textbf{ligands}---in the extracellular fluid. Ligand binding triggers inward folding of the plasma membrane, forming a vesicle that delivers the bound ligands into the cell interior. This mechanism enables the selective internalization of specific macromolecules, such as cholesterol and certain hormones.

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

\section{Metabolism}

  \subsection{Catabolism \& Anabolism}

    \begin{core}{Metabolism, Catabolism, and Anabolism}

      \textbf{Metabolism} encompasses the totality of chemical reactions occurring within a living organism to sustain life. A \textbf{metabolic pathway} refers to a defined sequence of enzyme-catalyzed reactions that transforms one or more substrates into a final product.

      \myfig{0.6}{cytology_figures/metabolism}{Metabolic pathway}{fig:fig49}

      Metabolic pathways are broadly classified into anabolism and catabolism.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Anabolic pathways} involve the \textbf{biosynthesis} of complex molecules from simpler precursors, necessitating a net input of energy. \textit{Photosynthesis (\autoref{core:photosynthesis}) exemplifies this category, in which solar energy drives the conversion of carbon dioxide and water into glucose.}

        \bce{\ce{6CO2 + 6H2O + \text{solar energy} -> C6H12O6 + 6O2} \hspace{3mm}(\textit{Photosynthesis})}

        \item \textbf{Catabolic pathways} involve the \textbf{degradation} of complex molecules into simpler constituents, a process accompanied by the release of chemical energy. \textit{Cellular respiration (\autoref{core:cell_respiration}) is a paradigmatic example, in which glucose is broken down to yield ATP.}

        \bce{\ce{C6H12O6 + 6O2 -> 6CO2 + 6H2O + ATP} \hspace{3mm} (\textit{Celllular Respiration})}

      \end{enumerate}

      In general, the energy liberated by catabolic pathways serves to drive energetically unfavorable anabolic reactions---a coupling that underlies the thermodynamic organization of cellular metabolism.

    \end{core}  
    
  \subsection{Energy}

    \begin{core}{Energy}

      \textbf{Energy} is formally defined as the capacity to perform work or induce change within a system. In biological contexts, energy manifests primarily in two fundamental forms: \textbf{kinetic energy}, associated with the motion of matter, and \textbf{potential energy}, representing stored energy by virtue of position or configuration. Two particularly relevant subforms are \textbf{thermal energy}---a manifestation of kinetic energy arising from the random translational motion of atoms and molecules---and \textbf{chemical energy}, a form of potential energy stored within the covalent bonds of molecules and released upon their rearrangement or cleavage.

    \end{core}

    The behavior of energy across all transformations---including those occurring within biological systems---is governed by the \textbf{laws of thermodynamics}, which constitute the fundamental framework for understanding the flow and interconversion of energy between different forms.

    \begin{core}{First Law of Thermodynamics}

      The \textbf{first law of thermodynamics}, also known as the \textbf{law of conservation of energy}, states that the total energy of an isolated system remains constant---energy can neither be created nor destroyed, but only converted from one form to another. 

      \textit{In biological systems, this principle manifests in processes such as the conversion of chemical energy stored in nutrient molecules into the kinetic energy underlying muscle contraction, wherein the total energetic content of the system is conserved throughout the transformation.}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Second Law of Thermodynamics}

      The \textbf{second law of thermodynamics} states that in any energy transfer or transformation, the \textbf{total entropy of an isolated system increases over time}. Entropy is formally defined as a measure of the dispersal of energy or the degree of molecular disorder within a system. 

      \textit{In biological systems, this law manifests as the inherent inefficiency of energy transformations: a fraction of the energy involved in any conversion is inevitably dissipated as heat, thereby increasing the entropy of the surroundings---even in cases where local biological order is transiently maintained.}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Gibbs Free Energy}

      To quantify the energetic favorability of chemical reactions, we employ the concept of \textbf{Gibbs free energy (G)}, a thermodynamic potential that accounts for the total energy changes of a system at constant temperature and pressure. The change in Gibbs free energy ($\Delta G$) associated with a given reaction serves as a criterion for \textbf{spontaneity}.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item When $\Delta G < 0$, the reaction proceeds \textbf{spontaneously} and is classified as an \textbf{exergonic reaction}. Such reactions release free energy, which may be harnessed to perform work on the surroundings.

        \myfig{0.6}{cytology_figures/exergonic}{Exergonic reaction free energy diagram}{fig:fig51}

        \item Conversely, when $\Delta G > 0$, the reaction is thermodynamically \textbf{non-spontaneous} and requires a net input of free energy to proceed. These are classified as \textbf{endergonic reactions}, as they absorb free energy from their surroundings and store it in the chemical bonds of the products (\textit{anabolic pathways}).

        \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/endergonic}{Endergonic reaction free energy diagram}{fig:fig52}

      \end{itemize}

    \end{core}

    \textit{The discussion free energy and entropy here is intentionally simplified to prioritize conceptual accessibility over rigor; a more formal and complete discussion is provided in \href{../../chemistry/thermodynamics/thermodynamics.pdf}{Thermodynamics}.}

  \subsection{Energy Coupling \& ATP}

    \begin{core}{Types of Work}

      Returning to metabolism in the context of the cell, cellular activity can be broadly categorised into three distinct types of work.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Chemical work} involves driving endergonic reactions that would not proceed spontaneously, such as the synthesis of macromolecules from their constituent monomers.

        \item \textbf{Transport work} involves the active movement of substances across membranes against their electrochemical gradients.

        \item \textbf{Mechanical work} encompasses physically dynamic processes, such as the beating of cilia or the contraction of muscle fibres.

      \end{itemize}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Energy Coupling}

      The cell accomplishes energetically unfavourable processes by coupling them to exergonic reactions that release free energy---a principle known as \textbf{energy coupling}. This coupling is primarily mediated by \textbf{adenosine triphosphate (ATP)}, which serves as the cell's principal energy currency.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/atp}{Adenosine triphosphate (ATP)}{fig:fig54}

      The hydrolysis of ATP to \textbf{adenosine diphosphate (ADP)} and inorganic phosphate ($P_i$) is an exergonic reaction that liberates free energy, which can subsequently be harnessed to drive endergonic processes within the cell.

      \bce{\ce{ATP + H2O -> ADP + P_i} \hspace{5mm} $\Delta G_{\text{ATP Hydrolysis}} = -7.3 \, kcal \, mol^{-1}$}

      Reactions that are endergonic in isolation can be rendered thermodynamically favourable by coupling them to ATP hydrolysis. This is commonly achieved through \textbf{phosphorylation}, whereby a phosphate group is transferred from ATP to a reactant, generating a phosphorylated intermediate of elevated reactivity.

      To illustrate, consider the synthesis of glutamine from glutamic acid (GluA) and ammonia (\ce{NH3})---a reaction that is endergonic when uncoupled.

      \bce{\ce{GluA + NH3 -> Glutamine} \hspace{5mm} $ΔG_{\text{Glu}} = + 3.4 \, kcal \, mol^{-1}$}

      Upon the introduction of ATP, glutamic acid is first phosphorylated to yield a high-energy intermediate. This activated species subsequently reacts with ammonia to produce glutamine. By coupling the endergonic synthesis step to ATP hydrolysis, the overall reaction is rendered exergonic.

      \bce{\ce{GluA + ATP -> GluA-P + ADP ->[NH3] Glutamine + ADP + P_i}}

      Summing the two constituent reactions---glutamine synthesis and ATP hydrolysis---yields a net negative $\Delta G$, confirming that the coupled reaction proceeds spontaneously under these conditions.
      \[
        ΔG_{\text{ATP Hydrolysis}} + ΔG_{\text{Glu}} = -7.3 \, kcal \, mol^{-1} + 3.4 \, kcal \, mol^{-1} = -3.9 \, kcal \, mol^{-1}
      \]

    \end{core}

    Since ATP is continuously consumed to power cellular processes, its regeneration from ADP and \ce{P_i} is essential for sustained cellular function. As ATP hydrolysis is exergonic, \textbf{the reverse reaction---the synthesis of ATP from ADP and \ce{P_i}---is necessarily endergonic}, requiring a continuous input of free energy. In most eukaryotic cells, this energy is supplied primarily through \textbf{cellular respiration}, explored in detail in \autoref{core:cell_respiration}.

    \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/atp_cycle}{ATP hydrolysis and synthesis cycle}{fig:fig58}

  \subsection{Enzyme Mechanisms}

    \begin{core}{Enzyme \& Substrates}

      The reactant upon which an enzyme acts is termed its \textbf{substrate}. Upon binding to its substrate---or substrates, in reactions involving multiple reactants---the enzyme forms an \textbf{enzyme--substrate complex}, within which the catalytic conversion of substrate to product is facilitated.

      \bce{\ce{Enzyme + Substrate -> Enzyme-Substrate Complex -> Enzyme + Product}}

      Substrate specificity is governed by the geometry and chemical properties of the enzyme's \textbf{active site}---the region to which the substrate binds. The active site establishes a distinctive microenvironment that promotes the conversion of substrate to product. Enzymes exhibit \textbf{high specificity}, with each enzyme typically catalysing a single reaction and acting on one substrate (or a narrow class of structurally related substrates).

      Upon substrate binding, weak non-covalent interactions---including hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, and van der Waals forces---induce a conformational change in the enzyme. This adjustment optimises the interaction between the active site and the substrate, stabilizing the transition state and enhancing catalytic efficiency. This phenomenon is known as \textbf{induced fit}.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/enzyme_substrate}{Enzyme--substract complex cycle}{fig:fig61}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Optimal Enzyme Conditions}

      Each enzyme operates most efficiently within a characteristic range of environmental conditions, including temperature, pH, and ionic strength, collectively referred to as its \textbf{optimal conditions}. In humans, most enzymes function optimally at temperatures of 35--40$^\circ$C and at pH values between 6 and 8, though notable exceptions exist. 

      \textit{Pepsin, a protease active in the stomach, for instance, is adapted to function under highly acidic conditions.} 

      When exposed to conditions outside their optimal range, enzymes may undergo \textbf{denaturation}---the loss of their functional three-dimensional conformation---resulting in diminished or abolished catalytic activity.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Cofactors}

      Many enzymes require the assistance of nonprotein molecules to carry out catalysis; these are collectively termed \textbf{cofactors}. Cofactors may be inorganic---such as metal ions including iron or zinc---or organic, in which case they are designated \textbf{coenzymes}. The nutritional importance of many vitamins derives from their role as coenzymes or as biosynthetic precursors from which coenzymes are derived.

      \myfig{0.4}{cytology_figures/cofactor}{Cofactors}{fig:fig62}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Inhibition}

      Enzymatic activity can be reduced or abolished by molecules known as \textbf{inhibitors}. Enzyme inhibition is broadly classified into the following two categories.

      \myfig{0.7}{cytology_figures/inhibition}{Competitive (\textit{left}) and noncompetitive (\textit{right}) inhibition}{fig:fig63}

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Competitive inhibitors} are molecules that bear structural similarity to the substrate and compete directly for occupancy of the enzyme's active site. By binding to the active site, they preclude substrate binding and thereby suppress product formation.

        \item \textbf{Noncompetitive inhibitors} bind to a site distinct from the active site---termed the \textbf{allosteric site}. This binding induces a conformational change in the enzyme that diminishes or abolishes catalytic activity, even when the substrate remains capable of binding.

      \end{itemize}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Allosteric Regulation}

      Most inhibitors are \textbf{reversible}, associating with the enzyme through noncovalent interactions and dissociating upon removal of the inhibitor, thereby restoring normal catalytic function. \textbf{Irreversible inhibitors}, by contrast, form covalent bonds with the enzyme or otherwise permanently abolish its activity. \textit{Many biological toxins and poisons exert their effects through irreversible enzyme inhibition.}

      Unregulated and simultaneous activity across all metabolic pathways would result in profound metabolic imbalance. Consequently, enzyme activity is subject to precise \textbf{regulation} in response to the cell's metabolic state and environmental conditions. A principal mechanism of such regulation is \textbf{allosteric regulation}, in which the binding of a regulatory molecule at one site on the enzyme modulates its activity at a spatially distinct site, either \textbf{activating} or \textbf{inhibiting} catalysis.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/allosteric}{Allosteric regulation}{fig:fig65}

      A related phenomenon, known as \textbf{cooperativity}, occurs in multisubunit enzymes whereby substrate binding at one active site enhances catalytic activity at the remaining active sites within the same enzyme complex.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/cooperativity}{Cooperativity}{fig:fig66}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Feedback Inhibition}

      A particularly important mode of metabolic regulation is \textbf{feedback inhibition}, in which the end product of a biosynthetic pathway inhibits an enzyme acting earlier in that same pathway. This self-regulating mechanism maintains product concentrations within an appropriate range, preventing both overproduction and underproduction relative to the cell's requirements.

      A well-characterised example is the biosynthesis of isoleucine, in which the accumulation of the end product allosterically inhibits an enzyme catalysing an early step in its own synthetic pathway.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/feedback}{Feedback inhibition}{fig:fig67}

    \end{core}

\section{Cellular Respiration}

  \subsection{Respiration Basics}

    \begin{core}[label=core:cell_respiration]{Cellular Respiration}

      \textbf{Cellular respiration} encompasses the set of metabolic processes by which cells extract chemical energy from organic molecules and transduce it into the synthesis of ATP. These processes are broadly categorised into the following categories.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Aerobic respiration} is the most energetically efficient catabolic pathway available to most organisms, in which molecular oxygen serves as the terminal electron acceptor during the stepwise oxidation of organic fuel molecules.

        \myfig{0.7}{cytology_figures/aerobic}{Aerobic respiration}{fig:fig68}

        \item \textbf{Anaerobic respiration} follows identically to aerobic respiration, however it uses an inorganic molecule (\textit{such as sulfate or nitrate}) as the terminal electron acceptor rather than oxygen.

        \sbs{0.45}{
          \item \textbf{Fermentation} is the partial catabolism of sugars or other organic molecules that proceeds in the absence of oxygen and without the involvement of an electron transport chain.
        }{
          \myfig{1}{cytology_figures/fermentation}{Fermentation}{fig:fig69}
        }

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Reduction-Oxidation Reactions}

      The central mechanism by which cellular respiration extracts energy from organic molecules involves \textbf{redox (reduction--oxidation) reactions}---chemical processes in which \ce{e^-}s are transferred between molecular species. The \textbf{loss of \ce{e^-}s} by a substance is termed \textbf{oxidation}, while the \textbf{gain of \ce{e^-}s} is termed \textbf{reduction}. The following non-biological example involving sodium and chlorine serves as a useful illustration.

      \myfig{0.4}{cytology_figures/nacl}{Reduction-oxidation reaction of sodium and chlorine}{fig:fig70}

      The same redox principles apply in the biological context of cellular respiration, as depicted below.

      \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/redox}{Reduction-oxidation reaction of cellular respiration}{fig:fig71}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{NADH \& FADH2}

      Electron transfer in cellular respiration is mediated by the coenzyme \textbf{\ce{NAD+}} (\textit{oxidised form}) and its reduced counterpart \textbf{\ce{NADH}} (\textit{nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide}). Acting in concert with \textbf{dehydrogenase enzymes}, \ce{NAD+} accepts \ce{e^-}s from substrate molecules---typically from hydroxyl groups of the form \ce{H-C-OH}---oxidizing the substrate to a carbonyl (\ce{C=O}) while itself being reduced to \ce{NADH}, with release of \ce{H+}.

      \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/nad}{Reduction of \ce{NAD+} to \ce{NADH}}{fig:fig72}

      A second important \ce{e^-} carrier is \textbf{FAD} (\textit{flavin adenine dinucleotide}), which functions analogously to \ce{NAD+} as an electron acceptor. Its reduced form, \ce{FADH2}, plays an equally significant role throughout the respiration pathway.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Glycolysis, Pyruvate Oxidation, Citric Acid Cycle, and Oxidative Phosphorylation}
    
    \begin{core}{Glycolysis}

      \sbs{0.4}{
        The process of aerobic respiration begins with \textbf{glycolysis}---from the Greek for "\textit{sugar splitting}"--- which is the metabolic pathway in which a single glucose molecule (\textit{a 6-carbon sugar}) is cleaved into two 3-carbon molecules of \textbf{pyruvate}. The pathway is divided into two phases.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item In the \textbf{energy investment phase}, 2 ATP are consumed to phosphorylate glucose and prime it for cleavage.

          \item In the subsequent \textbf{energy payoff phase}, 4 ATP and 2 NADH are generated, along with 2 pyruvate and 2 \ce{H2O} molecules. 

        \end{itemize}

        \textit{Although each phase comprises multiple enzymatic steps, their mechanistic details lie beyond the current scope of these notes.}
      }{
        \myfig{0.87}{cytology_figures/glycolysis}{Glycolysis}{fig:fig73}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Pyruvate Oxidation}

      \sbs{0.46}{
        Following glycolysis, each pyruvate molecule is transported into the \textbf{mitochondrial matrix}---the compartment enclosed by the inner mitochondrial membrane---where it undergoes \textbf{pyruvate oxidation}. This reaction, catalysed by the multienzyme complex \textbf{pyruvate dehydrogenase}, converts pyruvate into the two-carbon compound \textbf{acetyl-CoA}, releasing one carbon per pyruvate as \ce{CO2} and generating 2 additional NADH per glucose molecule.
      }{
        \myfig{0.95}{cytology_figures/pyruvate_de}{Pyruvate oxidation}{fig:fig74}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Citric Acid (Krebs) Cycle}

      Acetyl-CoA subsequently enters the \textbf{citric acid cycle} (\textit{also known as the \textbf{Krebs cycle}}), a series of enzyme-mediated reactions occurring in the mitochondrial matrix. Each turn of the cycle fully oxidises the acetyl group of acetyl-CoA, releasing its two carbons as \ce{CO2} and generating additional \ce{NADH} and \ce{FADH2}. One molecule of ATP (\textit{or the energetically equivalent GTP}) is also produced per acetyl-CoA, corresponding to 2 ATP per glucose molecule.

      \myfig{0.6}{cytology_figures/krebs}{Citric acid (\textit{Krebs}) cycle}{fig:fig75}

      As illustrated in \autoref{fig:fig75}, the pathway regenerates its initial reactant, \textit{oxaloacetate}, thereby completing the \textit{cycle} and enabling continuous operation.

    \end{core}

    Summing the outputs of the three stages described, a single glucose molecule yields the following:

    \begin{itemize}

      \item Glycolysis $\rightarrow$ 2 ATP, 2 NADH

      \item Pyruvate Oxidation $\rightarrow$ 2 NADH (\textit{one per pyruvate})

      \item Citric Acid Cycle $\rightarrow$ 6 NADH, 2 \ce{FADH2}, 2 ATP

    \end{itemize}

    This amounts to a cumulative yield of 10 NADH, 2 \ce{FADH2}, and 4 ATP. However, the overall ATP yield of cellular respiration is approximately 30--32 ATP per glucose---the bulk of which is generated in the final stage.

    \begin{core}[label=core:ox_phos]{Oxidative Phosphorylation}

      \textbf{Oxidative phosphorylation} is the final stage of cellular respiration, in which the electron carriers \ce{NADH} and \ce{FADH2} donate their electrons to the \textbf{electron transport chain (ETC)}.

      \myfig{0.95}{cytology_figures/ox_phos}{Oxidative phosphorylation}{fig:fig76}

      Within the mitochondrial inner membrane, \ce{NADH} and \ce{FADH2} are oxidised to \ce{NAD+} and \ce{FAD} respectively, releasing \ce{e^-}s and protons (\ce{H^+}). The \ce{e^-}s traverse a series of protein complexes (Complexes I--IV), descending to progressively lower energy states before being transferred to \ce{O2}---the terminal electron acceptor---which combines with the \ce{e^-}s and \ce{H^+}s to yield \ce{H2O} as a byproduct.

      The energy liberated during electron transfer is harnessed to pump \ce{H^+} from the mitochondrial matrix into the intermembrane space, establishing an electrochemical gradient known as the \textbf{proton-motive force}. \textbf{ATP synthase} exploits this gradient, coupling the thermodynamically favourable diffusion of \ce{H^+} back into the matrix to the phosphorylation of ADP, thereby generating ATP.

      It is through this mechanism that the majority of ATP is produced, bringing the total cellular yield to approximately 30--32 ATP per glucose molecule.

    \end{core}

    The chemical energy stored in glucose is thus transduced through a sequence of intermediate forms, with the ultimate energy capture occurring via the phosphorylation of ADP to ATP.
    \[
      \text{glucose} \rightarrow \text{NADH} \rightarrow \text{ETC} \rightarrow \text{proton-motive force} \rightarrow \text{ATP}
    \]

  \subsection{Anaerobic Respiration \& Fermentation}
    
    Having examined aerobic respiration, we now turn to energy-harvesting pathways that operate in the absence of oxygen, broadly categorised as \textbf{anaerobic respiration} and \textbf{fermentation}.

    \begin{core}{Anaerobic Respiration}

      \textbf{Anaerobic respiration} is employed by certain prokaryotes and proceeds by a mechanism analogous to aerobic respiration, with the critical distinction that an inorganic molecule other than oxygen---such as nitrate (\ce{NO3^-}) or sulfate (\ce{SO4^{2-}})---serves as the terminal \ce{e^-} acceptor. Crucially, the ETC is retained in anaerobic respiration, which constitutes the principal feature distinguishing it from fermentation.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Fermentation}

      \textbf{Fermentation} is the more biologically widespread of the two anaerobic pathways. It involves the partial catabolism of sugars or other organic fuels in the absence of both oxygen and the ETC. The pathway consists of glycolysis followed by subsidiary reactions whose primary function is to regenerate \ce{NAD+} by transferring \ce{e^-}s from \ce{NADH} to pyruvate or pyruvate-derived intermediates---thereby sustaining continued glycolytic activity.

      The sole ATP yield is the net 2 ATP generated during glycolysis, rendering fermentation substantially less energetically efficient than aerobic respiration. Two principal variants are recognised: \textbf{alcohol fermentation} and \textbf{lactic acid fermentation}.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item In \textbf{alcohol fermentation}, pyruvate is converted to ethanol with the concomitant oxidation of \ce{NADH} to \ce{NAD+} and release of \ce{CO2}. \textit{Yeast represents the most well-known organism performing this pathway, and has been exploited by humans for millennia in brewing and baking---the \ce{CO2} produced by baker's yeast during fermentation is responsible for the leavening of bread dough.}

        \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/alc}{Alcohol fermentation}{fig:fig77}

        \item In \textbf{lactic acid fermentation}, pyruvate is instead reduced to lactate, again with the concomitant oxidation of \ce{NADH} to \ce{NAD+}. \textit{This pathway is employed by human muscle cells under conditions of oxygen limitation, such as during sustained intense exercise.}

        \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/lac}{Lactic acid fermentation}{fig:fig78}

      \end{itemize}

    \end{core}

    Fermentation, anaerobic respiration, and aerobic respiration collectively constitute the three principal ATP-generating pathways by which cells harvest chemical energy from organic fuels. All three share a common origin in glycolysis, which oxidises glucose and other substrates to pyruvate with a net yield of 2 ATP, and all three employ \ce{NAD+} as the primary oxidising agent during this initial stage. Their key distinctions lie in the magnitude of total ATP produced and in the mechanism by which \ce{NADH} is subsequently reoxidised to \ce{NAD+} to sustain continued metabolic flux.

\section{Photosynthesis}

  \subsection{Photosynthesis Basics}

    \begin{core}[label=core:photosynthesis]{Photosynthesis}

      \textbf{Photosynthesis} is the process by which light energy is captured and converted into chemical energy stored in organic molecules, serving as the foundation of nearly all life on Earth, either directly or indirectly.

      Analogous to the role of the mitochondria in cellular respiration (\textit{\autoref{core:cell_respiration}}), photosynthesis is localized to the \textbf{chloroplasts}. The overall process, though mechanistically complex, can be represented by the following net equation. Notably, photosynthesis is broadly the reverse of cellular respiration, distinguished primarily by the form of energy involved: whereas cellular respiration harnesses \textbf{chemical energy}, photosynthesis is driven by \textbf{light energy}.

      \bce{\ce{6CO2 + 12H2O + \text{Light energy} -> C6H12O6 + 6O2 + 6H2O}}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Autotrophs \& Heterotrophs}

      Within the biosphere, photosynthesis is performed exclusively by \textbf{autotrophs}---organisms capable of synthesizing organic compounds from inorganic precursors and thus independent of other organisms for their energy supply. \textbf{Heterotrophs}, by contrast, lack this biosynthetic capacity and must obtain energy by consuming organic matter derived from autotrophs or other heterotrophs.

      \textit{It follows that the energy available to heterotrophs can, in virtually all cases, be traced back to photosynthetic activity in autotrophs.}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Light Reactions \& Calvin Cycle}
  
    Photosynthesis occurs in two distinct stages: the \textbf{light reactions} and the \textbf{Calvin cycle}. Let us first examine the light reactions.

    \begin{core}{Light Reactions}

      The \textbf{light reactions} are responsible for the conversion of solar energy into chemical energy, and occur within the \textbf{thylakoid membrane}. A foundational understanding of light energy, wavelength, photons, and absorption (\textit{\autoref{qua-sect:light}}) is prerequisite to this discussion.

      The light reactions are mediated by photosynthetic pigments localized within the chloroplast, most notably \textbf{chlorophyll}. Chlorophyll absorbs incident light energy and, in doing so, promotes its \ce{e^-}s to higher energy states. It is classified into two forms, \textbf{chlorophyll a} and \textbf{chlorophyll b}, with \textbf{carotenoids} serving as accessory pigments. Of these, chlorophyll a is the principal light-capturing pigment that participates directly in the light reactions. While each pigment exhibits a distinct absorption spectrum, their overall spectral profiles are broadly similar.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/absorption}{Absorption spectrum of chlorophyll a, chlorophyll b, and carotenoids}{fig:fig80}

      As illustrated above, wavelengths corresponding to green light are absorbed least efficiently and are instead reflected or transmitted by the pigment molecules. This accounts for the characteristically green appearance of leaves and, in some cases, yellow---but seldom blue, purple, or red.

      Upon excitation to a higher potential energy level, an electron is thermodynamically unstable and will eventually return to its ground state, dissipating the absorbed energy as heat or fluorescent light.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/excited}{Energy dissipation of an excited electron}{fig:fig81}

      In chloroplasts, however, this energy is captured and directed through specialized molecular assemblies known as \textbf{photosystems}. Each photosystem comprises a \textbf{reaction-center complex}---containing a pair of chlorophyll a molecules and a \textbf{primary \ce{e^-} acceptor}---surrounded by an antenna network of \textbf{light-harvesting complexes}.

      \myfig{0.4}{cytology_figures/photosystem}{Photosystem}{fig:fig82}

      Two distinct photosystems operate in series: \textbf{Photosystem II (PS II)} and \textbf{Photosystem I (PS I)}. Acting in concert, they transduce light energy into chemical energy in the form of ATP and \ce{NADPH}.

      Here is a step-by-step description of the light reactions mediated by these photosystems.

      \myfig{0.9}{cytology_figures/light_r}{Light reactions}{fig:fig83}

      \begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]

        \item A photon absorbed by a pigment molecule within the light-harvesting complex of PS II excites one of its electrons to a higher energy level. Rather than decaying back to the ground state, the excitation energy is transferred resonantly to an adjacent pigment molecule, which in turn excites one of its own electrons. This sequential energy transfer propagates through the antenna complex until it converges on the chlorophyll a pair at the reaction-center complex.

        \item The excited electron from the reaction-center chlorophyll a pair is transferred to the primary \ce{e^-} acceptor. To replenish the oxidized reaction center, \ce{e^-}s are extracted from the oxidative splitting of \ce{H2O}, a reaction catalyzed by an associated enzyme complex that yields \ce{O2} and \ce{H^+} as byproducts. The liberated protons contribute to the establishment of an electrochemical gradient across the thylakoid membrane.

        \item The energized \ce{e^-} is subsequently passed from the primary acceptor of PS II through an \textbf{electron transport chain (ETC)}, analogous to that of oxidative phosphorylation in cellular respiration (\textit{\autoref{core:ox_phos}}). As \ce{e^-}s traverse this series of redox carriers toward the PS I reaction center, the free energy released is coupled to the active transport of \ce{H^+} from the stroma into the thylakoid lumen, further amplifying the proton gradient established in the previous step during the oxidative splitting of \ce{H2O}.

        The resulting electrochemical gradient drives ATP synthesis through \textbf{chemiosmosis}, as protons flow down their concentration gradient through ATP synthase, powered by the proton-motive force.

        \item Light energy is absorbed by the PS I antenna complex and funneled to its reaction-center chlorophyll a pair via the same resonance transfer mechanism described above, exciting an \ce{e^-} that is passed to the PS I primary acceptor. Unlike PS II, water splitting is not required to restore the oxidized reaction center, as the electron deficit is fulfilled by electrons arriving from the PS II ETC.

        \item The photoexcited electrons from PS I are transferred through a second set of \ce{e^-} carriers and ultimately delivered to \ce{NADP+} reductase, which reduces \ce{NADP+} to \ce{NADPH}. This reaction consumes stromal \ce{H^+}, further reinforcing the proton gradient across the thylakoid membrane.

      \end{enumerate}

      Here is a more comprehensive figure illustrating the light reactions within the thylakoid membrane, highlighting the chemiosmotic generation of ATP.

      \myfig{1}{cytology_figures/light_r_complete}{Light reactions within the thylakoid membrane}{fig:fig84}

      Under certain conditions, photoexcited electrons may follow an alternative route known as \textbf{cyclic electron flow}, which engages PS I exclusively, bypassing PS II entirely.

      \myfig{0.85}{cytology_figures/light_r_cyclic}{Cyclic electron flow}{fig:fig85}

      In summary, the light reactions transduce solar energy into the chemical energy carriers ATP and \ce{NADPH}, which are subsequently consumed by the \textbf{Calvin cycle}. Concomitantly, the oxidative splitting of \ce{H2O} sustains the proton gradient and provides a continuous supply of electrons to PS II.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Calvin Cycle}

      The \textbf{Calvin cycle} takes place in the stroma and harnesses the chemical energy stored in ATP and \ce{NADPH} to drive the reductive fixation of \ce{CO2} into sugar. Like the citric acid cycle, it operates as a regenerative cycle: the carbon acceptor molecule is continuously replenished as substrates enter and products exit. The Calvin cycle proceeds through the following phases.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Phase 1 --- Carbon Fixation:} Three molecules of \ce{CO2} are each covalently attached to a molecule of the five-carbon acceptor ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate (RuBP), a reaction catalyzed by the enzyme \textbf{RuBisCO}. Each resulting six-carbon intermediate is highly unstable and rapidly cleaves into two molecules of 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PGA), yielding six 3-PGA molecules in total.

        \item \textbf{Phase 2 --- Reduction:} Each 3-PGA molecule is phosphorylated by ATP to yield 1,3-bisphosphoglycerate, which is subsequently reduced by \ce{NADPH} to produce glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate (G3P). Of the six G3P molecules generated, one is exported from the cycle as the net carbohydrate output, while the remaining five are retained for the regeneration phase.

        \item \textbf{Phase 3 --- Regeneration of RuBP:} The five retained G3P molecules undergo a series of carbon skeleton rearrangements to regenerate the three RuBP acceptor molecules, a process that requires the additional expenditure of three ATP molecules.

      \end{enumerate}

      In total, the Calvin cycle consumes 9 ATP and 6 \ce{NADPH} per net G3P molecule produced, generating 9 ADP and 6 \ce{NADP+} in the process. These spent energy carriers are returned to the light reactions, where they are re-energized and recycled for use in subsequent turns of the cycle. 

      \myfig{0.8}{cytology_figures/calvin_cycle}{Calvin cycle}{fig:fig86}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Photorespiration and Plant Adaptations}
    
    Over millions of years, plants have evolved sophisticated mechanisms to meet the challenges imposed by terrestrial life. A particularly significant challenge is the trade-off between sustaining photosynthesis and limiting excessive water loss. The \ce{CO2} required for carbon fixation enters the leaf through specialized pores called \textbf{stomata}, which simultaneously serve as the primary sites of \textbf{transpiration}.

    On hot, dry days, many plants partially or fully close their stomata to minimize water loss. This, however, restricts \ce{CO2} influx and promotes the accumulation of \ce{O2} within the leaf's intercellular air spaces. Under such conditions, RuBisCO---the enzyme responsible for carbon fixation---binds \ce{O2} in lieu of \ce{CO2}, initiating a metabolically costly process known as \textbf{photorespiration}.

    \begin{core}{C3 Plants}

      C3 plants---named for the three-carbon molecule 3-phosphoglycerate, the first stable product of carbon fixation --- exhibit reduced photosynthetic output under hot, dry conditions. As stomatal closure depletes intracellular \ce{CO2}, \ce{O2} increasingly competes with \ce{CO2} for the active site of RuBisCO, diverting carbon flux toward photorespiration. This process yields a two-carbon compound, phosphoglycolate, which is exported from the chloroplast and metabolized through a series of reactions that consume ATP without producing net sugar. 

      Photorespiration is therefore \textbf{energetically wasteful}, rendering C3 plants particularly susceptible to chronic heat and water stress. The evolutionary pressure imposed by these conditions has driven the emergence of alternative carbon fixation strategies in other plant lineages.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{C4 Plants}

      \textbf{C4 plants} have evolved a spatial carbon-concentrating mechanism that suppresses photorespiration. Under hot, dry conditions, C4 plants only partially close their stomata, striking a balance between water conservation and \ce{CO2} acquisition. The \ce{CO2} that enters is first fixed in the \textbf{mesophyll cells} by \textbf{PEP carboxylase}, which combines it with phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP), a three-carbon acceptor, to yield a four-carbon organic acid. Unlike free \ce{CO2}, this covalently bound intermediate cannot diffuse back out of the cell, effectively trapping the carbon. The four-carbon compound is subsequently transported to the \textbf{bundle-sheath cells}, where it is decarboxylated to release \ce{CO2} directly at the site of RuBisCO activity, while PEP is regenerated and returned to the mesophyll. This spatial compartmentalization maintains a locally elevated \ce{CO2} concentration around RuBisCO, strongly favoring carboxylation over oxygenation and thus minimizing photorespiration.

      \myfig{0.95}{cytology_figures/c4}{C4 pathway}{fig:fig87}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Crassulacean Acid Metabolism (CAM) Plants}

      \textbf{Crassulacean acid metabolism (CAM)} represents a temporal, rather than spatial, solution to the photorespiration problem. CAM plants open their stomata exclusively at night, when cooler temperatures reduce the evaporative demand, and keep them closed during the day to minimize water loss. During the nocturnal period, \ce{CO2} is fixed by PEP carboxylase into four-carbon organic acids, which are stored in vacuoles. In the daytime, when the stomata are sealed, the light reactions proceed normally, generating ATP and \ce{NADPH}. Concurrently, the stored organic acids are decarboxylated to release \ce{CO2}, which is fed directly into the Calvin cycle within the chloroplasts. This temporal separation of \ce{CO2} acquisition and carbon fixation allows CAM plants to sustain sugar production even under conditions of prolonged stomatal closure.

      The following figure summarizes the key distinctions between $C_4$ and CAM carbon fixation strategies.

      \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/c4_cam}{C4 (\textit{left}) and CAM (\textit{right}) plant pathways}{fig:fig88}

    \end{core}

\section{Cellular Signaling}

  \begin{core}{Cellular Signalling}

    Cellular signalling is a fundamental biological process that enables intercellular communication through the interaction of signalling molecules (\textbf{ligands}) with their cognate receptors. This sophisticated mechanism allows cells to coordinate their activities and respond to environmental stimuli.

  \end{core}

  \subsection{Signalling Types}

    \begin{core}{Signalling Types}

      Cell communication can be broadly classified into three categories based on the distance of signal transmission.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Juxtacrine signalling} is a form of \textbf{direct cell-cell} communication that requires physical contact between adjacent cells, mediated either by membrane-bound ligands interacting with neighbouring cell receptors or through \textbf{gap junctions} (\textit{\autoref{core:ij}}) that permit the direct passage of signals between cells.

        \sbs{0.4}{
          \item \textbf{Paracrine signalling} involves \textbf{local} intercellular communication, in which signalling molecules diffuse across short distances to act upon neighbouring target cells within the immediate microenvironment. The spatial range of paracrine signals is inherently limited by diffusion kinetics and rapid degradation or uptake of signalling molecules, ensuring that only cells in close proximity to the signal source are affected.
        }{
          \myfig{0.95}{cytology_figures/paracrine}{Paracrine signalling}{fig:fig90}
        }

        \sbs{0.45}{
          \item \textbf{Endocrine signalling} (\textit{also termed \textbf{hormonal signalling}}) facilitates \textbf{long-distance} intercellular communication through the systemic circulation. Specialized \textbf{endocrine cells} synthesize and secrete hormones into the bloodstream, which transports these chemical messengers to anatomically distant target cells. Only cells expressing the appropriate cognate receptors are capable of detecting and responding to these hormonal signals, thereby ensuring specificity in systemic communication.
        }{
          \myfig{0.95}{cytology_figures/endocrine}{Endocrine (\textit{hormonal}) signaling}{fig:fig91}
        }

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Signal Reception, Transduction, and Response}
    
    \subsubsection{Signal Reception}

      \begin{core}{Signal Reception}

        \sbs{0.4}{
          \textbf{Signal reception} constitutes the initial step of cellular signalling, wherein target cells detect extracellular signalling molecules through specific receptor proteins. These receptors are strategically positioned either at the \textbf{cell surface} or \textbf{within the cell} as intracellular receptors---which are located primarily in the cytoplasm or nucleous and are activated by signalling molecules possessing sufficient hydrophobicity or small molecular size to traverse the plasma membrane's lipid bilayer.
        }{
          \myfig{1}{cytology_figures/intracellular}{Intracellular signal reception}{fig:fig95}
        }

      \end{core}

      \begin{core}[label=core:receptor_types]{Major Cell-Surface Receptor Types}

        Cell-surface receptors can be classified into three principal categories based on their structural characteristics and signal transduction mechanisms.

        \begin{enumerate}

          \item \textbf{G Protein–Coupled Receptors (GPCRs)} are seven-transmembrane domain receptors that undergo ligand-induced conformational changes, thereby activating associated heterotrimeric G proteins. The activated G protein subsequently modulates downstream effector proteins, initiating intracellular signalling cascades.

          \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/gpcr}{G protein-coupled receptors}{fig:fig92}

          \item \textbf{Receptor Tyrosine Kinases (RTKs)} are single-pass transmembrane receptors that, upon ligand binding, undergo receptor dimerization and subsequent autophosphorylation at specific intracellular tyrosine residues. These phosphotyrosine residues function as docking sites for cytoplasmic signalling proteins, propagating downstream signalling cascades.

          \myfig{0.6}{cytology_figures/rtk}{Receptor tyrosine kinases}{fig:fig93}

          \item \textbf{Ligand-Gated Ion Channel Receptors} are transmembrane proteins that undergo ligand-induced conformational changes, resulting in the opening or closing of an integral ion channel. This conformational transition permits the selective flux of specific ions across the plasma membrane, rapidly altering the cell's membrane potential.

          \myfig{0.6}{cytology_figures/ion_channel}{Ligand-gated ion channel receptors}{fig:fig94}

        \end{enumerate}

      \end{core}

    \subsubsection{Signal Transduction}

      \begin{core}{Signal Transduction}

        \textbf{Signal transduction} is the process by which cells convert signals into intracellular responses. Although signal transduction may occasionally occur through a single-step mechanism, it more commonly involves a \textbf{sequential series of molecular interactions} between proteins, collectively termed a \textbf{signal transduction pathway}, which functions analogously to a molecular relay system to amplify and integrate cellular signals.

      \end{core}

      \begin{core}{Phosphorylation Cascades}

        A fundamental mechanism underlying signal transduction pathways is reversible protein phosphorylation, which forms the basis of \textbf{phosphorylation cascades}. In these cascades, a protein kinase catalyzes the phosphorylation of a substrate protein, which, once activated, phosphorylates a subsequent target protein in a sequential manner. Enzymes that catalyze the transfer of phosphate groups from ATP to protein substrates are termed \textbf{protein kinases}, as exemplified by receptor tyrosine kinases (\textit{\autoref{core:receptor_types}}).

        \myfig{0.6}{cytology_figures/phos_cas}{Phosphorylation cascade}{fig:fig96}

      \end{core}

      \begin{core}{Second Messengers}

        While the signalling cascades predominantly feature protein-protein interactions, signal transduction pathways frequently incorporate small, nonprotein, water-soluble molecules or ions termed \textbf{second messengers}. These diffusible intracellular mediators serve to amplify and propagate signals throughout the cell. The two most ubiquitous second messengers in eukaryotic cells are \textbf{cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP)} and \textbf{calcium ions (\ce{Ca2+})}.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item \textbf{Cyclic AMP (cAMP)} is synthesized from ATP by the membrane-associated enzyme \textbf{adenylyl cyclase}, which is canonically activated downstream of GPCR signalling (\textit{\autoref{core:receptor_types}}) through G proteins. Elevated intracellular cAMP concentrations activate \textbf{protein kinase A (PKA)}, a serine/threonine kinase that phosphorylates specific substrate proteins, thereby eliciting diverse cellular responses (\textit{\autoref{fig:fig97}})

          \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/camp}{Cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) pathway}{fig:fig97}

          \item \textbf{Calcium ions (\ce{Ca2+})} constitute an even more versatile second messenger than cAMP, participating in a broader array of cellular processes. Cells maintain a steep \ce{Ca2+} concentration gradient, with cytosolic levels approximately 10,000-fold lower than extracellular concentrations and ER luminal stores. This gradient is actively maintained by ATP-dependent \ce{Ca2+} pumps that sequester \ce{Ca2+} into the ER and extrude it from the cell. Consequently, even modest absolute increases in cytosolic \ce{Ca2+} represent substantial relative changes, enabling sensitive signal detection.

          \myfig{0.65}{cytology_figures/ca_ion}{\ce{Ca2+} pathway}{fig:fig98}

          Upon activation of specific signalling pathways, cytosolic \ce{Ca2+} concentrations increase, predominantly through release from ER stores. This mechanism frequently involves two lipid-derived second messengers---\textbf{diacylglycerol (DAG)} and \textbf{inositol 1,4,5-trisphosphate (IP$_3$)}---which are generated via hydrolysis of the membrane phospholipid phosphatidylinositol 4,5-bisphosphate (PIP$_2$) by \textbf{phospholipase C (PLC)}, typically activated downstream of GPCRs. IP$_3$ diffuses through the cytosol and binds to IP$_3$ receptors on the ER membrane, inducing \ce{Ca2+} release into the cytoplasm.

        \end{itemize}

      \end{core}

    \subsubsection{Signal Response}

      \begin{core}{Cellular Response}

        \textbf{Cellular response} represents the terminal phase of signal transduction, wherein integrated signalling information elicits specific biological outcomes. These responses can be broadly classified into two distinct categories.
          
        \myfig{0.45}{cytology_figures/nuclear_cytoplasmic}{Nuclear (\textit{left}) and cytoplasmic (\textit{right}) responses}{fig:fig99}

        \begin{itemize}

          \item \textbf{Nuclear responses} involve signal-dependent modulation of gene transcription through the activation or repression of specific \textbf{transcription factors} (\textit{\autoref{core:transcription}}). These genomic responses ultimately alter cellular protein composition through protein synthesis (\textit{\autoref{sec:gene_expression}}).

          \item \textbf{Cytoplasmic responses} modulate the activity, localization, or stability of pre-existing proteins through post-translational modifications, bypassing transcriptional regulation. These non-genomic responses enable rapid cellular adaptation by directly affecting the functional state of cytoplasmic and membrane-associated proteins.

        \end{itemize}

      \end{core}

  \subsection{Signal Regulation}
    
    Cellular signalling operates as a \textbf{dynamic} and \textbf{highly regulated} process rather than a simple binary switch. The magnitude and duration of cellular responses are subject to multiple levels of control through distinct regulatory mechanisms.

    \begin{core}{Signal Amplification}

      Signalling cascades function to amplify cellular responses through sequential enzymatic steps, wherein each activated component catalyzes the activation of multiple downstream molecules. This multiplicative effect at each catalytic step enables exponential \textbf{signal amplification}. For instance, a single activated adenylyl cyclase molecule can generate numerous cAMP molecules prior to its inactivation. Consequently, multi-tiered enzymatic cascades can achieve substantial amplification of the initial signal, with the overall gain determined by the product of amplification factors at each successive step.

      \myfig{0.6}{cytology_figures/amplification}{Signal amplication in cAMP pathway}{fig:fig100}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Specificity and Coordination}

      Cellular responses to identical extracellular signals exhibit remarkable \textbf{specificity} due to \textbf{differential protein expression profiles across cell types}. Each cell type expresses a unique repertoire of receptors, signalling intermediates, and effector proteins, enabling diverse responses to the same ligand. Consequently, a given signalling molecule may elicit a robust response in one cell type, no response in another, or qualitatively distinct responses in a third. This cellular specificity is determined by the particular complement of signalling machinery expressed within each cell, allowing for coordinated yet context-dependent physiological responses.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/specificity1}{Signal specificity}{fig:fig101}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Scaffolding Proteins}

      Signal transduction efficiency is substantially enhanced by \textbf{scaffolding proteins}---large adaptor molecules that simultaneously bind multiple signalling components, organizing them into spatially defined functional complexes. By colocalizing pathway components, scaffolding proteins circumvent the kinetic limitations of diffusion, thereby accelerating signal propagation while simultaneously increasing signalling specificity through prevention of inappropriate cross-talk between parallel pathways. In certain cases, scaffolding proteins establish stable, pre-assembled signalling modules that enable rapid pathway activation upon receptor engagement.

      \myfig{0.45}{cytology_figures/scaffolding}{Scaffolding protein}{fig:fig102}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Signal Termination}

      Signal transduction pathways are subject to active \textbf{termination} mechanisms that prevent constitutive activation and enable cellular responsiveness to subsequent stimuli. This responsiveness critically depends on the \textbf{reversibility} of signal-induced molecular modifications. Multiple enzymatic mechanisms mediate pathway deactivation: phosphodiesterases hydrolyze cAMP to 5'-AMP, thereby depleting second messenger pools; the intrinsic GTPase activity of G proteins catalyzes GTP hydrolysis to GDP, returning G proteins to their inactive state; and protein phosphatases reverse kinase-mediated phosphorylation events, restoring signalling components to their basal state.

    \end{core}  

    Cellular responses are typically initiated when receptor occupancy---which correlates with ligand-receptor complex formation---exceeds a threshold level sufficient to activate downstream signalling cascades. Importantly, most signalling systems exhibit graded, dose-dependent responses rather than all-or-nothing behavior, enabling cells to produce proportional outputs that scale with the magnitude of signal strength.

  \subsection{Apoptosis}

    \begin{core}{Apoptosis}

      Dysregulation of signalling pathways---through mutation, infection, or damage---can result in aberrant pathway activation or loss of normal regulatory control. Such dysregulation is particularly prevalent in malignant and virally infected cells, where constitutive pathway activation may promote uncontrolled proliferation or other pathological phenotypes. When cells sustain irreparable damage, reach the terminus of their functional lifespan, or pose a threat to homeostasis, they may be eliminated through \textbf{apoptosis}, a regulated form of programmed cell death.

      \textbf{Apoptosis} is a genetically programmed and \textbf{tightly regulated} process of cellular self-destruction that maintains tissue homeostasis by eliminating damaged, infected, senescent, or superfluous cells. The apoptotic program involves systematic dismantling of cellular architecture.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item Endonucleases cleave chromosomal DNA into nucleosomal fragments.

        \item Proteases degrade cytoskeletal and organellar protein.

      \item The cell undergoes characteristic morphological changes including cytoplasmic condensation, nuclear fragmentation (\textit{karyorrhexis}), and plasma membrane blebbing.
      \end{itemize}
      
      Cellular contents are subsequently packaged into membrane-enclosed apoptotic bodies that are recognized and phagocytosed by neighboring cells or professional phagocytes, enabling efficient clearance without eliciting \textbf{inflammation} (EXTERNAL REFERENCE TO IMMUNOLOGY INFLAMMATION). This orderly process contrasts sharply with \textbf{necrosis}, an uncontrolled form of cell death characterized by plasma membrane rupture, release of intracellular contents, and consequent inflammatory responses that can damage surrounding tissue.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Apoptosis History \& Pathway}

      The molecular mechanisms of apoptosis were first elucidated through pioneering genetic studies in the nematode \textit{Caenorhabditis elegans}. In this model organism, the anti-apoptotic protein Ced-9 constitutively inhibits the pro-apoptotic factors Ced-4 and Ced-3 under normal physiological conditions. Upon receipt of an apoptotic stimulus via cell-surface death receptors, downstream signal transduction pathways inactivate Ced-9, thereby relieving its inhibitory constraint on Ced-4 and Ced-3. This derepression initiates a proteolytic cascade mediated by \textbf{caspases}---a family of proteases---that systematically cleave structural and regulatory proteins while activating others responsible for internucleosomal DNA fragmentation, ultimately executing the apoptotic program.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/apoptosis}{Apoptosis pathway in nematode (\textit{Caenorhabditis elegans})}{fig:fig103}

      In mammals, including humans, apoptosis is mediated through multiple convergent pathways that collectively utilize approximately 15 distinct caspases. A principal pathway, termed the intrinsic or mitochondrial pathway, involves Bcl-2 family proteins that regulate \textbf{mitochondrial outer membrane permeabilization (MOMP)}. Pro-apoptotic Bcl-2 family members oligomerize to form pores in the mitochondrial outer membrane, triggering the release of intermembrane space proteins, including cytochrome \textit{c}. Once released into the cytosol, cytochrome \textit{c} binds to Apaf-1, forming the apoptosome complex that activates initiator caspase-9, which subsequently cleaves and activates executioner caspases (\textit{caspase-3, -6, -7}) to propagate the apoptotic cascade.
      \[
        \text{Bcl-2} \rightarrow \text{Cytochrome \textit{c}} \rightarrow \text{Apaf-1} \rightarrow \text{caspase-9} \rightarrow \text{Apoptotic Cascade}
      \]

      These mammalian apoptotic pathways employ proteins structurally and functionally homologous to the \textit{C. elegans} core apoptotic machinery: Bcl-2 family proteins are homologous to Ced-9, Apaf-1 corresponds to Ced-4, and mammalian caspases are homologous to Ced-3. This remarkable conservation underscores the fundamental importance of programmed cell death across metazoan evolution.

    \end{core}

\section{Cell Division}

  \subsection{DNA Organization and Replication}

    \begin{core}{Cell Division}

      The capacity for self-replication constitutes a fundamental defining characteristic of living systems. Like all biological processes, reproduction is ultimately rooted in cellular mechanisms. The continuity of life across generations depends critically on the faithful reproduction of cells through \textbf{cell division}.

      Prior to cell division, cells must undergo growth and complete duplication of their cellular constituents to ensure that each daughter cell receives a complete complement of necessary materials. Among the components requiring faithful replication, the genetic material (\textit{DNA}) is paramount. To comprehend the mechanics of DNA replication and segregation, we must first examine the organizational architecture of genetic material within eukaryotic cells.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Chromosomes \& Chromatids}

      In eukaryotic cells, DNA exists as extended double-helical molecules that associate with \textbf{histone} proteins to form highly compacted nucleoprotein structures termed \textbf{chromosomes}. Most sexually reproducing species are \textbf{diploid}, meaning that each somatic cell harbors two complete sets of chromosomes---one inherited maternally and one paternally---resulting in an even total chromosome number. In humans, somatic cells contain 46 chromosomes, organized as 23 homologous pairs (\textit{23 paternal and 23 maternal chromosomes}).

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/genome}{Human karyotype before replication}{fig:fig104}

      Prior to DNA replication, a human somatic cell contains 46 chromosomes, each comprising a single DNA molecule as shown in \autoref{fig:fig104}. Following replication during the S phase (\textit{\autoref{core:m_phases}}), the chromosome count remains 46; however, each chromosome now consists of two identical \textbf{sister chromatids} joined together, yielding a total of 92 DNA molecules as shown in \autoref{fig:fig105}. Sister chromatids are held together along their entire length by multi-subunit protein complexes called \textbf{cohesins}, a physical association termed \textbf{sister chromatid cohesion}. Each replicated chromosome possesses a single \textbf{centromere}---a specialized chromosomal region enriched in repetitive DNA sequences where sister chromatids exhibit their tightest physical association and where kinetochore proteins assemble.

      \myfig{0.5}{cytology_figures/dup_chromo}{Human karyotype after replication}{fig:fig105}

      \textit{Precise terminology is essential to avoid confusion. Chromosome number is operationally defined by the \textbf{number of centromeres} present in the cell. Chromatid number, conversely, refers to the total number of individual DNA molecules (visualized as discrete chromatin "arms").}

      \textit{Prior to DNA replication, a human somatic cell contains 46 chromosomes and 46 chromatids (a 1:1 ratio). Following replication, the chromosome number remains 46 (as only 46 centromeres are present), but the chromatid number doubles to 92, organized as 46 pairs of sister chromatids joined at their respective centromeres.}

      During mitotic anaphase, sister chromatid cohesion is dissolved, allowing the chromatids to segregate to opposite poles of the dividing cell. Upon separation, each chromatid is reclassified as an independent chromosome, restoring the 1:1 chromosome-to-chromatid ratio in each daughter cell.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Mitosis \& The Cell Cycle}
   
    \begin{core}{Cell Cycle}

      \textbf{The cell cycle} comprises the ordered sequence of events through which a cell grows, duplicates its genetic material, and divides to produce two daughter cells. Mitosis represents only one component of this cycle, which is partitioned into four discrete phases: \textbf{Gap 1 (G1)}, \textbf{DNA synthesis (S)}, \textbf{Gap 2 (G2)}, and \textbf{mitosis and cytokinesis (M)}.

      \textbf{Interphase} is a collective term encompassing all non-mitotic phases (\textit{G1, S, and G2}). Consequently, the cell cycle can be conceptualized as an alternating progression between interphase and M phase.

      Throughout all three interphase stages, cells undergo growth through biosynthesis of proteins and biogenesis of cytoplasmic organelles, including mitochondria and endoplasmic reticulum. Chromosomal DNA replication, an absolute prerequisite for subsequent cell division, occurs exclusively during S phase.

      The temporal organization of the cell cycle can therefore be summarized as follows: initial growth and preparation (\textit{G1}), continued growth concurrent with chromosomal DNA replication (\textit{S}), final growth and preparation for mitosis (\textit{G2}), followed by nuclear and cytoplasmic division (\textit{M}). The resulting daughter cells may subsequently re-enter G1 to initiate a new cycle.

      \myfig{0.65}{cytology_figures/cell_cycle}{The cell cycle}{fig:fig106}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Mitosis}

      Nuclear division, termed \textbf{mitosis}, segregates replicated chromosomes into two daughter nuclei and is immediately followed by \textbf{cytokinesis}, the physical partitioning of the cytoplasm to complete cellular division.

      \textit{Mitosis must be distinguished from \textbf{meiosis}, a specialized form of nuclear division that generates \textbf{haploid} gametes (spermatozoa in males and ova in females) through two successive rounds of chromosome segregation. Meiotic division is examined in detail in \autoref{gen-core:meiosis}.}

    \end{core}
    
    \begin{core}[label=core:m_phases]{Mitotic Phases}

      Mitosis is conventionally subdivided into the following five sequential phases based on characteristic chromosomal and cytoskeletal dynamics.

      \begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item During \textbf{prophase}, dispersed interphase chromatin condenses into discrete, microscopically visible chromosomes, each comprising two identical sister chromatids joined by cohesin complexes at the centromere. Concurrently, the \textbf{mitotic spindle} begins to assemble from two \textbf{centrosomes}, which migrate to opposite cellular poles. Although cells typically harbor a single centrosome during interphase, centrosome duplication occurs during S/G2 phase to provide the two poles required for bipolar spindle formation. The \textbf{mitotic spindle} is a highly dynamic macromolecular machine composed of microtubules and associated motor proteins, regulatory factors, and structural components. Radial arrays of shorter microtubules emanating from each centrosome are termed \textbf{asters}.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{cytology_figures/prophase}{Prophase}{fig:fig107}
        }

        \textit{Note: For descriptive simplicity, we have employed the term "chromosomes" throughout the preceding discussion. Strictly speaking, during interphase, DNA exists in a decondensed state as chromatin fibers dispersed throughout the nucleus, only condensing into discrete chromosomal structures during prophase.}

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item \textbf{Prometaphase} is characterized by nuclear envelope breakdown, which permits spindle microtubules emanating from each centrosome to access the chromosomes. A subset of these microtubules attach to \textbf{kinetochores}---large multiprotein complexes that assemble at the centromere of each sister chromatid---forming \textbf{kinetochore microtubules} that mediate chromosome motility. Other microtubules, termed \textbf{nonkinetochore microtubules} or \textbf{polar microtubules}, extend from opposite poles and join at the spindle midzone, where they contribute to spindle elongation and maintenance of spindle bipolarity.
        }{
          \myfig{0.65}{cytology_figures/prometaphase}{Prometaphase}{fig:fig108}
        }

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item By \textbf{metaphase}, the centrosomes have completed their migration to opposite cellular poles, and all chromosomes have achieved kinetochore-microtubule attachments, resulting in their alignment along the \textbf{metaphase plate}---a conceptual plane equidistant from both spindle poles. 
        }{
          \myfig{0.72}{cytology_figures/metaphase}{Metaphase}{fig:fig109}
        }

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item \textbf{Anaphase} is initiated by proteolytic cleavage of cohesin complexes by the protease \textbf{separase}, which dissolves sister chromatid cohesion and permits chromatid separation. Upon separation, each chromatid is reclassified as an independent daughter chromosome. Poleward chromosome movement (\textit{anaphase A}) is driven by kinetochore-microtubule depolymerization and motor protein activity, while concurrent spindle elongation (\textit{anaphase B}) further separates the chromosome masses.
        }{
          \myfig{0.72}{cytology_figures/anaphase}{Anaphase}{fig:fig110}
        }

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item During \textbf{telophase}, two daughter nuclei form as nuclear envelope membranes reassemble around each segregated chromosome complement. Chromosomes decondense, reverting to the dispersed chromatin state characteristic of interphase, and mitotic spindle microtubules undergo depolymerization. 

          \textbf{Cytokinesis}, which typically initiates during late anaphase and continues through telophase, achieves cytoplasmic partitioning. In animal cells, cytokinesis is mediated by a contractile ring composed of actin and myosin filaments that assembles at the cell equator, forming a \textbf{cleavage furrow} that progressively constricts to physically separate the two daughter cells.
        }{
          \myfig{0.7}{cytology_figures/telophase}{Telophase \& cytokinesis}{fig:fig111}
        }

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}  

  \subsection{Cell Cycle Control and Checkpoints}
    
    Having established the mechanistic basis of mitosis, we now examine the regulatory networks governing cell cycle progression. The ordered temporal sequence of cell cycle events is orchestrated by a \textbf{cell cycle control system}---a cyclically operating regulatory network that triggers and coordinates key cell cycle transitions.

    \begin{core}{Checkpoints}

      \sbs{0.5}{
        A \textbf{checkpoint} is a regulatory mechanism that monitors critical cell cycle events and integrates signals to determine whether the cell proceeds or arrests. Three major checkpoints occur at the G1/S transition, G2/M transition, and during M phase (\textit{the spindle assembly checkpoint}).

        The core molecular machinery governing checkpoint control consists of \textbf{cyclin-dependent kinases (Cdks)}---serine/threonine protein kinases---and their regulatory subunits, \textbf{cyclins}. Cdks require association with a cyclin partner for catalytic activity, hence their nomenclature. Active cyclin-Cdk heterodimers are collectively termed \textbf{maturation-promoting factors (MPFs)}, reflecting their function in promoting cell cycle phase transitions.
      }{
        \myfig{1}{cytology_figures/checkpoint}{Cell cycle checkpoints}{fig:fig112}
      }

      For instance, cyclins that accumulate during G2 phase (\textit{e.g., cyclin B in vertebrates}) bind to Cdk proteins (\textit{Cdk1}), generating an active MPF complex that phosphorylates numerous proteins involved in nuclear envelope breakdown, chromosome condensation, and spindle assembly, thereby triggering mitosis.

      \myfig{0.47}{cytology_figures/cdk}{G2 Cdk cycle}{fig:fig113}

      For most cell types, the G1 checkpoint represents the primary decision point governing cell cycle commitment. Cells that successfully pass this checkpoint typically proceed irreversibly through S, G2, and M phases to complete division. Conversely, cells lacking appropriate mitogenic signals, nutrients, or exhibiting DNA damage may exit the cell cycle and enter \textbf{G0}, a quiescent state characterized by indefinite cell cycle arrest.

    \end{core}

    The frequency of cell division exhibits pronounced tissue-specific variation. For example, epithelial cells in the epidermis and hair follicles undergo continuous proliferation throughout organismal lifespan to maintain tissue homeostasis. In contrast, hepatocytes (\textit{liver cells}) retain proliferative competence but normally reside in G0, re-entering the cell cycle only upon tissue injury or stimulation.

    \begin{core}{Growth Factors}

      Cell cycle entry and progression are regulated by extracellular \textbf{growth factors}---secreted polypeptides that bind cell-surface receptors and activate signaling cascades promoting proliferation. Many primary mammalian cells exhibit strict growth factor dependence, arresting without appropriate mitogenic signals. For example, \textbf{platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF)}, released from activated platelets during wound healing, stimulates fibroblast proliferation in connective tissue.

    \end{core}
    
    \begin{core}{Cancer}

      While most cells with irreparable damage activate apoptosis, certain cells acquire mutations that disable apoptotic pathways and checkpoint controls, permitting continued proliferation despite genomic instability. This breakdown of cell cycle regulation constitutes a hallmark of \textbf{cancer}. Cancer cells exhibit autonomy from normal growth controls, proliferating without mitogenic signals and evading contact inhibition. When cultured with adequate nutrients, cancer cells display unlimited replicative potential---termed \textbf{transformation}---unlike normal somatic cells, which undergo senescence after finite divisions.

      \textit{For a comprehensive discussion of oncogenic mechanisms and cancer biology, see \href{../oncology/oncology.pdf}{Oncology}.}

    \end{core}
 
\section{Gene Expression}
  \label{sec:gene_expression}

  Gene expression constitutes the multi-step process through which heritable genetic information (\textit{DNA}) is decoded to produce functional cellular components.

  \subsection{Transcription}

    \begin{core}{RNA Polymerase}
 
      \textbf{RNA polymerase} catalyzes the synthesis of RNA transcripts by reading the template DNA strand in the $3' \to 5'$ direction while synthesizing the complementary RNA strand in the $5' \to 3'$ direction (\textit{the downstream direction}).

      \textit{In the context of general protein transcription, the relevent RNA polymerase is RNA polymerase II. RNA polymerase I, on the other hand, primarily participates in the synthesis of ribosomal RNA (rRNA).}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:transcription]{Synthesis of RNA Transcript}

      Transcription proceeds through the following phases, each characterized by specific molecular events and regulatory mechanisms.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Initiation}

        \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/initiation}{Transcription nitiation}{fig:fig115} 

        Transcription initiation in eukaryotes requires the assembly of a multi-protein complex at the \textbf{promoter region}. A critical recognition element within this region is the \textbf{TATA box} (\textit{\autoref{fig:fig116}}), a highly conserved DNA sequence located approximately $-25$ to $-30$ nucleotides upstream of the transcription start site. This sequence is recognized by \textbf{transcription factors}, a diverse collection of proteins that facilitate RNA polymerase recruitment and positioning.

        \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/tata}{TATA box}{fig:fig116}
        
        The complete assembly of RNA polymerase II and all requisite transcription factors at the promoter constitutes the \textbf{transcription initiation complex} (\textit{also termed the pre-initiation complex, or PIC}). Formation of this complex positions RNA polymerase at the \textbf{transcription start site} (\textit{$+1$ position}), the precise nucleotide where RNA synthesis commences.

        \item \textbf{Elongation}

        \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/elongation}{Transcription elongation}{fig:fig117}
        
        During elongation, RNA polymerase translocates along the template DNA strand, catalyzing the addition of ribonucleotides complementary to the DNA template sequence. As the polymerase advances, it continuously unwinds the double helix ahead of the transcription bubble while rewinding the duplex behind it.         

        \item \textbf{Termination}

        The \textbf{terminator} is a DNA sequence located downstream of the coding region that signals transcription termination. Upon transcription of this sequence, RNA polymerase ceases synthesis and dissociates from the DNA template, releasing the completed RNA transcript.

        \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/termination}{Transcription termination}{fig:fig118}
        
        Transcription termination mechanisms differ markedly between prokaryotes and eukaryotes, reflecting fundamental differences in cellular organization and RNA processing requirements.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item \textbf{Prokaryotic termination}: In bacteria, transcription terminates when RNA polymerase encounters a terminator sequence in the DNA template, destabilizing the RNA-DNA hybrid and causing the transcript to release. The resulting bacterial mRNA is immediately translation-competent, requiring no further processing.

          \item \textbf{Eukaryotic termination}: In eukaryotes, RNA polymerase II termination is coupled to $3'$-end processing of the pre-mRNA. The polymerase transcribes through a \textbf{polyadenylation signal sequence (AAUAAA)} in the DNA, which serves as a recognition element for cleavage and $3'$-end processing factors. These proteins bind to the AAUAAA sequence in the nascent pre-mRNA as it emerges from the polymerase and endonucleolytic cleavage occurs $10-35$ nucleotides downstream of the AAUAAA signal.

        \end{itemize}

      \end{enumerate}
        
    \end{core}

  \subsection{RNA Modification}

    \begin{core}{RNA Processing}

      In eukaryotes, primary transcripts termed \textbf{pre-mRNA} (\textit{precursor mRNA}) undergo extensive modifications before export to the cytoplasm for \textbf{translation} (\textit{\autoref{core:translation}}). This \textbf{RNA processing} is essential because eukaryotic genes contain both coding and non-coding sequences (\textit{\autoref{core:rna_splicing}}) that must be distinguished and appropriately processed to generate mature, translation-competent mRNA. 

    \end{core}
    
    \begin{core}{Alteration of mRNA Ends}
      
      Both termini of eukaryotic pre-mRNA undergo enzymatic modification shortly after transcription initiation, yielding a mature mRNA with distinctive structural features at each end.

      \myfig{0.9}{cytology_figures/mrna_ends}{5'cap (\textit{left}) and poly-A tail (\textit{right})}{fig:fig119} 

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{$5'$ cap}: A modified guanosine nucleotide is added to the $5'$ end of the nascent transcript via an unusual $5'-5'$ triphosphate linkage. This capping occurs when the transcript is only $20-40$ nucleotides long.

        \item \textbf{Poly-A tail}: Following transcription termination and cleavage at the polyadenylation signal (AAUAAA), an enzyme adds approximately $50-250$ adenine nucleotides to the $3'$ end of the pre-mRNA.

      \end{itemize}
      
      The resulting mature mRNA structure comprises the following elements in order.

      \begin{center}

        5' cap --- $5'$ UTR --- start codon (AUG) --- coding sequence --- stop codon --- $3'$ UTR --- poly-A tail

      \end{center}

      The $5'$ cap, poly-A tail, and untranslated regions (UTR) contribute to mRNA stability and function through several mechanisms: promoting nuclear export of mature transcripts, conferring resistance to exonucleolytic degradation, and facilitating ribosome recruitment during cap-dependent translation initiation.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:rna_splicing]{RNA Splicing}
      
      \textbf{RNA splicing} is the process by which non-coding sequences are excised from pre-mRNA and the remaining coding sequences are ligated to form mature mRNA, catalyzed by a protein complex called the \textbf{spliceosome}.

      \myfig{0.9}{cytology_figures/intron_exon}{Introns \& exons}{fig:fig120} 

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Introns} (\textit{intervening sequences}) are non-coding segments that are transcribed but subsequently removed during RNA processing. These sequences can be extensive, sometimes constituting $>90\%$ of a gene's length.

        \item \textbf{Exons} (\textit{expressed sequences}) are the coding segments that are retained in mature mRNA and ultimately translated into protein. 

      \end{itemize} 
             
      \textit{Remarkably, the spliceosome's catalytic activity resides in its RNA components rather than its proteins. These \textbf{ribozymes} demonstrate RNA's enzymatic capacity, a discovery that fundamentally altered our understanding of catalysis and supported the RNA world hypothesis.}

      While constitutive splicing removes all introns and retains all exons in a predetermined pattern, \textbf{alternative splicing} enables a single gene to encode multiple distinct protein isoforms by varying which exons are retained or excluded during RNA processing. This mechanism dramatically expands proteomic diversity.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Domains}

      \sbs{0.4}{
        \textbf{Protein domains} are discrete structural and functional units within a polypeptide chain, typically $50-200$ amino acids in length, that fold independently and often retain function when isolated from the full protein.

        \textit{The exon-intron architecture of eukaryotic genes often mirrors protein domain organization, with individual exons or exon groups encoding discrete domains.}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Translation}

    \begin{core}[label=core:translation]{Translation}

      \textbf{Translation} is the process by which the nucleotide sequence of mRNA is decoded to direct the synthesis of a polypeptide chain with a specific amino acid sequence. Translation occurs on \textbf{ribosomes} (\textit{\autoref{core:ribosome_structure}}), which coordinate the interactions between mRNA, transfer RNAs (\textit{tRNAs; \autoref{core:trna}}), and various translation factors to catalyze peptide bond formation.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Codons}
      
      The genetic code defines the correspondence between nucleotide triplets in mRNA and amino acids in proteins. Each three-nucleotide sequence, termed a \textbf{codon}, specifies a particular amino acid or translation termination signal. With four possible nucleotides (\textit{A, U, G, C}) at each of three positions, 64 possible codons exist. Since only 20 standard amino acids are incorporated into proteins, the genetic code is \textbf{degenerate} (\textit{or redundant})---most amino acids are specified by multiple codons.

    \begin{core}[label=core:trna]{Transfer RNA}

      \sbs{0.65}{
        \textbf{Transfer RNAs (tRNAs)} are small RNA molecules (\textit{~75-95 nucleotides}) that serve as adaptors, physically linking codons in mRNA to the corresponding amino acids. Each tRNA molecule exhibits a characteristic cloverleaf secondary structure that folds into an L-shaped three-dimensional conformation with two critical functional regions.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item The \textbf{amino acid attachment site} is the $3'$ terminus of all tRNAs that terminates in the sequence CCA, where the amino acid is covalently attached to the $3'$-OH of the terminal adenosine via an ester bond.

          \item The \textbf{anticodon} is a three-nucleotide sequence located in the anticodon loop that base-pairs with the complementary codon in mRNA through Watson-Crick and \textbf{wobble base pairing}. The anticodon determines which codon(s) a given tRNA recognizes.

          \textit{The third codon position exhibits relaxed base-pairing specificity, a phenomenon termed \textbf{wobble} that permits a single tRNA to recognize multiple synonymous codons differing in the third position. This flexibility explains why cells require fewer than 61 distinct tRNAs despite 61 sense codons---humans possess only ~45 tRNA genes.}

        \end{itemize}
      }{
        \myfig{0.95}{cytology_figures/trna}{Transfer RNA structure}{fig:fig122} 
      }
      
      \textbf{Aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases} ensure translation fidelity by correctly matching tRNAs with their cognate amino acids. This family of 20 enzymes (\textit{one per amino acid}) catalyzes aminoacylation, enabling tRNA reuse following amino acid release during protein synthesis (\textit{\autoref{core:polypeptide_synthesis}}.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:ribosome_structure]{Ribosome Structure}

      \sbs{0.55}{
        \textbf{Ribosomes} are large ribonucleoprotein complexes (\textit{molecular machines}) composed of small and large subunits that catalyze polypeptide synthesis, with each subunit comprising proteins and \textbf{ribosomal RNAs (rRNAs)}. Three tRNA-binding sites with distinct functions coordinate translation.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item The \textbf{A site} (\textit{aminoacyl-tRNA site}) binds incoming aminoacyl-tRNA carrying the next amino acid to be incorporated. Codon recognition and initial binding occur here.

        \end{itemize}
      }{
        \myfig{0.87}{cytology_figures/ribosome_structure}{Ribosome structure}{fig:fig123}
      }

      \begin{itemize}

        \item The \textbf{P site} (\textit{peptidyl-tRNA site}) holds the tRNA attached to the growing polypeptide chain. During each elongation cycle, the peptide is transferred from the P-site tRNA to the amino acid on the A-site tRNA.

        \item The \textbf{E site} (\textit{exit site}) binds deacylated tRNA (\textit{tRNA without amino acid}) immediately before its release from the ribosome.

      \end{itemize}
      
      During translation, tRNAs move sequentially through these sites: $A \rightarrow P \rightarrow E$, with each translocation advancing the ribosome three nucleotides along the mRNA.

    \end{core}    

    \begin{core}[label=core:polypeptide_synthesis]{Polypeptide Synthesis}

      Like transcription (\textit{\autoref{core:transcription}}), \textbf{translation} proceeds through the same three phases, each orchestrated by specific protein factors and powered by GTP hydrolysis.
      
      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Initiation}

        \myfig{0.65}{cytology_figures/translation_initiation}{Translation initiation}{fig:fig124}

        Translation initiation establishes the correct \textbf{reading frame} and positions the ribosome at the start codon through mechanisms that differ fundamentally between prokaryotes and eukaryotes.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item \textbf{Prokaryotic initiation}: The small ribosomal subunit binds directly to the \textbf{Shine-Dalgarno sequence} (\textit{ribosome binding site}) in the mRNA, located just upstream of the AUG start codon. The initiator tRNA can associate either before or after mRNA binding, providing flexibility in assembly order.
          
          \item \textbf{Eukaryotic initiation}: The small ribosomal subunit, pre-bound with initiator tRNA, recognizes the $5'$ cap of the mRNA and scans downstream until encountering the AUG start codon, typically the first AUG in favorable context (\textbf{Kozak sequence}).

        \end{itemize}

        Upon start codon recognition, the large ribosomal subunit joins the complex to form the \textbf{translation initiation complex}. Throughout this process, \textbf{initiation factors}---proteins that coordinate assembly of ribosomal subunits, mRNA, and initiator tRNA---facilitate these events through GTP-dependent mechanisms.
 
        \item \textbf{Elongation}

        Elongation is a cyclical process that adds amino acids to the growing polypeptide one residue at a time. Each elongation cycle comprises three steps coordinated by \textbf{elongation factors} (\textit{not shown in \autoref{fig:fig125}}.

        \begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]

          \item \textbf{Codon recognition}: The anticodon of an incoming aminoacyl-tRNA pairs with the complementary mRNA codon in the A site.

          \item \textbf{Peptide bond formation}: An rRNA molecule of the large ribosomal subunit catalyzes peptide bond formation between the carboxyl terminus of the growing polypeptide in the P site and the amino group of the incoming amino acid in the A site. This reaction transfers the polypeptide from the P-site tRNA to the A-site tRNA, leaving a deacylated tRNA in the P site.

          \item \textbf{Translocation}: The ribosome translocates the peptidyl-tRNA from the A site to the P site while simultaneously moving the deacylated tRNA from the P site to the E site, where it dissociates. This movement advances the mRNA exactly three nucleotides, placing the next codon in the vacant A site.

        \end{enumerate}

        \myfig{0.9}{cytology_figures/translation_elongation}{Translation elongation}{fig:fig125}

        \item \textbf{Termination}

        \myfig{0.9}{cytology_figures/translation_termination}{Translation termination}{fig:fig126}
        
        Translation terminates when the A site encounters a stop codon (\textit{UAA, UAG, or UGA}), for which no corresponding aminoacyl-tRNA exists. Instead, \textbf{release factors}---proteins that structurally mimic tRNAs---recognize stop codons and bind the A site, triggering the hydrolytic breakdown process of the translation process by other protein factors.

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Signal Peptides}

      Eukaryotic cells direct nascent polypeptides to specific subcellular destinations through \textbf{signal peptides}—short amino acid sequences that specify localization to the cytosol, nucleus, mitochondria, endoplasmic reticulum (ER), or other organelles.

      \myfig{0.95}{cytology_figures/signal_peptide}{Protein targeting to the endoplasmic reticulum by signal peptides and signal recognition particles}{fig:fig127}

      The \textbf{signal recognition particle (SRP)}, a ribonucleoprotein complex, recognizes ER-targeting signal peptides and escorts the ribosome-nascent chain complex to the ER membrane, where the polypeptide is translocated into the lumen or integrated into the membrane.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Polyribosomes}

      \textbf{Polyribosomes} (\textit{or \textbf{polysomes}}) are clusters of multiple ribosomes simultaneously translating a single mRNA molecule. Because ribosomes are relatively small compared to mRNA length, multiple ribosomes can bind and translate an mRNA concurrently, each synthesizing an independent polypeptide copy.

      \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/polyribosome}{Polyribosome}{fig:fig128}
      
      Polyribosome formation dramatically amplifies protein production from a single mRNA template---with ten ribosomes per mRNA, protein synthesis rates increase tenfold compared to monoribosome translation. This mechanism enables cells to rapidly respond to changing protein demands without proportionally increasing mRNA levels.
      
      In prokaryotes, the lack of nuclear compartmentalization permits \textbf{coupled transcription-translation}: ribosomes bind nascent mRNA and initiate translation while RNA polymerase continues transcribing the downstream gene sequence. 

      \myfig{0.55}{cytology_figures/coupled_transtrans}{Coupled transcription-translation}{fig:fig129}

    \end{core}
  
  \subsection{Operons}

    % ORIGINAL: Bacteria that can conserve resources and energy have an advantage, meaning it produces proteins for a specific case only when needed and doesn't when not needed.
    
    Bacterial cells optimize resource allocation by regulating gene expression in response to environmental conditions. Synthesizing enzymes constitutively (continuously) wastes energy and biosynthetic precursors when their substrates are absent or products are abundant. Consequently, bacteria have evolved sophisticated regulatory mechanisms that couple gene expression to metabolic need, producing proteins only when required and repressing synthesis when unnecessary. This adaptive gene regulation confers significant competitive advantages in fluctuating environments.

    \begin{core}{Operon Structure}

      % ORIGINAL: Operator: the on and off switch of a segment of DNA; this is positioned within the promoter
      % this controls the access of RNA polymerase to these genes
      % Operon: the entire operator, promotor, gene constitution
      
      Bacterial genes encoding enzymes for related metabolic functions are often clustered and coordinately regulated through an organizational unit called an \textbf{operon}. This arrangement enables simultaneous control of multiple genes through a single regulatory mechanism.
      
      An operon comprises several key elements:
      
      \textbf{Promoter}: The RNA polymerase binding site where transcription initiates, located upstream ($5'$) of the coding sequences.
      
      \textbf{Operator}: A regulatory DNA sequence, typically overlapping or immediately adjacent to the promoter, that serves as a binding site for repressor proteins. The operator functions as a molecular switch—when occupied by a repressor, it blocks RNA polymerase access to the promoter, preventing transcription of downstream genes. When unoccupied, RNA polymerase can bind the promoter and transcribe the structural genes.
      
      \textbf{Structural genes}: Multiple protein-coding sequences arranged sequentially that are transcribed as a single polycistronic mRNA (an mRNA encoding multiple proteins). Ribosomes can initiate translation at multiple start codons within this single transcript, producing separate proteins from one mRNA molecule.
      
      The complete regulatory unit—promoter, operator, and structural genes—constitutes the operon. This arrangement enables coordinated regulation: a single regulatory protein controlling operator occupancy can simultaneously activate or repress all genes in the operon.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Repressor-Mediated Regulation}

      % ORIGINAL: Repressor: a protein that binds to the operator, preventing RNA polymerse from transcribing
      % This molecule is coded by its corresponding REGULATORY GENE
      % Corepressor: a different protein from the repressor that binds to the repressor to activate it (in this case the lone repressor is defaultly inactive)
      % Inducer: a different protein from the repressor that binds to the repressor to deactivate it (in this case the lone repressor is defaultly active)
      
      \textbf{Repressor proteins} are trans-acting regulatory factors that bind operators and prevent transcription initiation. Each repressor is encoded by a separate \textbf{regulatory gene}, typically located elsewhere in the genome. The regulatory gene possesses its own promoter and is transcribed independently, producing repressor protein constitutively at low levels.
      
      Repressor activity is modulated by small molecules that bind allosterically, inducing conformational changes that alter DNA-binding affinity:
      
      \textbf{Negative inducible operons}: The repressor is inherently active (can bind operator) but is inactivated by binding an \textbf{inducer} molecule. Inducer binding causes a conformational change that prevents operator binding, derepressing the operon. The \textit{lac} operon exemplifies this mechanism—lactose (or allolactose, its isomer) serves as inducer. In lactose's absence, the repressor blocks transcription; lactose presence inactivates the repressor, enabling expression of lactose-metabolizing enzymes.
      
      \textbf{Negative repressible operons}: The repressor is inherently inactive (cannot bind operator) but is activated by binding a \textbf{corepressor} molecule. Corepressor binding induces a conformational change enabling operator binding, repressing the operon. The \textit{trp} operon exemplifies this mechanism—tryptophan serves as corepressor. When tryptophan is abundant, it activates the repressor to block synthesis of tryptophan biosynthetic enzymes (feedback repression). When tryptophan is scarce, the inactive repressor cannot bind the operator, permitting enzyme synthesis.
      
      These mechanisms exemplify \textbf{negative regulation}—the regulatory protein (repressor) inhibits transcription. Binding of the effector molecule (inducer or corepressor) reverses the default state.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Activator-Mediated Regulation}

      % ORIGINAL: Positive gene regulation
      % cAMP: a small organic molecule that accumulates when glucose is scarce
      % when camp binds to a protein called CRP, it binds to the DNA and stimulates transcription of a gene
      % this is called an ACTIVATOR
      
      Bacterial operons can also be controlled through \textbf{positive regulation}, where a regulatory protein enhances rather than inhibits transcription. \textbf{Activator proteins} bind DNA sequences near promoters and stimulate RNA polymerase recruitment or activity.
      
      The \textit{lac} operon provides a well-characterized example. Even when derepressed (inducer present, repressor inactive), the \textit{lac} promoter is relatively weak and produces low transcript levels. Efficient transcription requires an activator: the catabolite activator protein (CAP, also called CRP for cAMP receptor protein).
      
      \textbf{Cyclic AMP (cAMP)} is a small signaling molecule whose concentration reflects cellular glucose availability. When glucose is scarce, adenylyl cyclase synthesizes cAMP from ATP, elevating intracellular cAMP levels. When glucose is abundant, cAMP levels drop. This inverse relationship enables cAMP to signal glucose scarcity.
      
      cAMP binds CAP/CRP allosterically, inducing a conformational change that enables CAP-cAMP complex binding to a specific site upstream of the \textit{lac} promoter. DNA-bound CAP-cAMP physically interacts with RNA polymerase, facilitating polymerase recruitment and open complex formation, thereby stimulating transcription initiation. When glucose is abundant (cAMP low), CAP cannot bind DNA, and \textit{lac} transcription remains low even if lactose is present.
      
      This dual control—negative regulation by the lac repressor (lactose sensing) and positive regulation by CAP-cAMP (glucose sensing)—creates a sophisticated logic gate: \textit{lac} operon expression is high only when lactose is present AND glucose is absent. This ensures that bacteria preferentially metabolize glucose but can switch to lactose when necessary, optimizing carbon source utilization.

    \end{core}  

  \subsection{Mutations}

    \begin{core}{Mutations}

      % ORIGINAL: Mutations: a small error in the DNA that lead to good or bad effects on the individual
      
      \textbf{Mutations} are heritable changes in DNA sequence that arise through errors in DNA replication, failures of DNA repair mechanisms, or damage induced by environmental factors. Mutations constitute the ultimate source of genetic variation, providing raw material for evolution. However, most mutations are neutral or deleterious; only rarely do mutations confer advantageous phenotypes. The phenotypic consequences of mutations range from undetectable to lethal, depending on the affected gene, the nature of the sequence change, and environmental context.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Point Mutations}

      % ORIGINAL: Small scale mutations (Point mutations): the differing ONE nucleotide
      % SUBSTITUTIONS, INSERTIONS AND DELETIONS content
      
      \textbf{Point mutations} affect single nucleotides or small numbers of nucleotides and are the most common type of mutation. Two major categories exist: substitutions and indels (insertions/deletions).
      
      \textbf{Nucleotide Substitutions}
      
      Substitutions replace one nucleotide with another. When occurring in coding sequences, substitutions generate three possible outcomes:
      
      (1) \textbf{Silent (synonymous) mutations}: The substitution alters the codon but not the encoded amino acid due to genetic code degeneracy. For example, both GAA and GAG encode glutamate; substitution between them is silent. Silent mutations generally lack phenotypic consequences, though they may affect mRNA stability, splicing, or translation efficiency through codon usage effects.
      
      (2) \textbf{Missense (nonsynonymous) mutations}: The substitution changes the codon to specify a different amino acid. Phenotypic effects vary widely depending on the biochemical similarity between original and substituted amino acids and the structural/functional importance of the affected position. Conservative substitutions (e.g., leucine → isoleucine, both hydrophobic) may be tolerated, while non-conservative substitutions (e.g., glutamate → valine, charged to hydrophobic) are more likely to disrupt protein function. The sickle cell anemia mutation (GAG → GTG, glutamate → valine at position 6 of β-globin) exemplifies a missense mutation with severe consequences.
      
      (3) \textbf{Nonsense mutations}: The substitution converts an amino acid codon to a stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA), causing premature translation termination. The resulting truncated protein typically lacks function and may be rapidly degraded. Nonsense mutations generally produce severe phenotypes, particularly when occurring early in the coding sequence. The Duchenne muscular dystrophy mutations frequently include nonsense mutations in the dystrophin gene.
      
      \textbf{Insertions and Deletions (Indels)}
      
      Insertions add nucleotides to the DNA sequence, while deletions remove nucleotides. When the number of inserted or deleted nucleotides is not divisible by three, indels cause \textbf{frameshift mutations}: the translational reading frame downstream of the mutation is altered, changing every subsequent codon. Frameshifts typically produce nonfunctional proteins for two reasons: (1) the entire amino acid sequence downstream is scrambled; (2) frameshifts frequently introduce premature stop codons, causing truncation.
      
      Frameshifts generally have severe phenotypic consequences, often more severe than missense or nonsense mutations. Insertions or deletions of three nucleotides (or multiples thereof) avoid frameshifts, instead adding or removing amino acids—these may be tolerated if occurring in non-critical regions.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Mutagens}

      % ORIGINAL: Mutagen: physical or chemical agents that interact with DNA in ways that cause mutations (UV, X ways, burnt food)
      
      \textbf{Mutagens} are physical or chemical agents that increase mutation rates above spontaneous background levels by damaging DNA or interfering with replication/repair processes. Understanding mutagens is critical for assessing environmental health risks and cancer etiology.
      
      \textbf{Physical mutagens}:
      
      \textbf{Ultraviolet (UV) radiation} induces formation of pyrimidine dimers (typically thymine dimers) where adjacent pyrimidines on the same strand form covalent bonds, distorting DNA structure. If unrepaired, these lesions cause replication errors. UV exposure is the primary cause of skin cancer.
      
      \textbf{Ionizing radiation} (X-rays, gamma rays, cosmic rays) possesses sufficient energy to eject electrons from atoms, generating reactive ions. Ionizing radiation causes diverse DNA damage: base modifications, single- and double-strand breaks, and DNA-protein crosslinks. Double-strand breaks are particularly hazardous, as they can lead to chromosomal rearrangements or loss. Ionizing radiation exposure increases cancer risk proportionally to dose.
      
      \textbf{Chemical mutagens}:
      
      \textbf{Base analogs} (e.g., 5-bromouracil) structurally resemble normal bases and are incorporated during replication but exhibit altered base-pairing properties, causing substitutions.
      
      \textbf{Alkylating agents} (e.g., ethyl methanesulfonate, nitrogen mustards) add alkyl groups to DNA bases, particularly guanine, altering base-pairing specificity and causing mispairing during replication.
      
      \textbf{Intercalating agents} (e.g., ethidium bromide, acridine dyes) insert between stacked base pairs, distorting the double helix and causing frameshift mutations during replication.
      
      \textbf{Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)} (found in tobacco smoke, charred foods, combustion products) are metabolically activated to reactive epoxides that form bulky adducts with DNA bases, particularly guanine. These adducts block replication and cause mutations if bypassed by error-prone polymerases. PAHs are major contributors to lung cancer and other malignancies.
      
      Cells possess elaborate DNA repair systems (base excision repair, nucleotide excision repair, mismatch repair, homologous recombination) that counteract mutagen-induced damage. Defects in repair pathways dramatically increase mutation rates and cancer susceptibility, as exemplified by xeroderma pigmentosum (defective nucleotide excision repair) and Lynch syndrome (defective mismatch repair).

    \end{core}
