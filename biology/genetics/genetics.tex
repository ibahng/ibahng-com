\section{Meiosis and Sexual Life Cycles}

  \subsection{Sexual Life Cycles}

    The transmission of heritable traits from one generation to the next constitutes \textbf{heredity}, a process through which genetic information is passed across lineages. Offspring exhibit inherited similarities yet also demonstrate \textbf{variation}, reflecting differences in the specific genetic information transmitted. The discipline of \textbf{genetics} encompasses the study of both hereditary mechanisms and variation. Genetic information is transmitted via \textbf{genes}, discrete DNA segments that encode specific functional products and serve as the fundamental units of heredity.

    \begin{core}{Organization of Chromosomes}

      \sbs{0.5}{
        Human \textbf{somatic cells} (\textit{non-reproductive cells}) are characterized by their possession of two complete chromosome sets---one of maternal origin and one of paternal origin---yielding a total of 46 chromosomes. Such cells, containing two chromosome sets (\textit{2n}), are designated \textbf{diploid}. \textit{It should be noted that the diploid chromosome number exhibits considerable variation across species.} The chromosomal composition of a human somatic cell can be visualized through a \textbf{karyotype}, as illustrated to the right.
      }{
        \myfig{0.7}{genetics_figures/karyotype}{Human karyotype}{fig:fig1}
      }

      As demonstrated \autoref{fig:fig1}, paired chromosomes share identical length, centromere position, and gene arrangement. These matched pairs are termed \textbf{homologous chromosomes}. Although both members of a homologous pair carry genes governing the same inherited traits, the specific allelic variants may differ between these \textbf{homologs}. This pattern of homology characterizes 22 of the 23 chromosome pairs in humans. The twenty-third pair comprises the \textbf{sex chromosomes} (\textit{\autoref{core:sex_chromosomes}}), which typically manifest as XX in females and XY in males, while the remaining 22 pairs are collectively designated \textbf{autosomes}.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:sexual_asexual]{Sexual vs Asexual Reproduction}

      \sbs{0.58}{
        \textbf{Sexual reproduction} proceeds through the transmission of genetic material via specialized reproductive cells termed \textbf{gametes}. These gametes are \textbf{haploid} (\textit{n}), containing a single chromosome set, and arise through the specialized cell division process of \textbf{meiosis} (\textit{\autoref{core:meiosis}}). Offspring formation occurs when male and female gametes---sperm and egg, respectively---undergo fusion through \textbf{fertilization}, thereby reconstituting the diploid (\textit{2n}) chromosome complement.

        This reproductive mode contrasts fundamentally with \textbf{asexual reproduction}, wherein offspring derive from a single parent organism through \textbf{mitosis} (\textit{\autoref{cyt-core:mitosis}}), yielding genetically identical \textbf{clones}.
      }{
        \myfig{1}{genetics_figures/sexual_life_cycle}{Human sexual life cycle}{fig:fig2}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Sexual Life Cycles}

      The temporal sequence of reproductive events described in \autoref{core:sexual_asexual} constitutes a \textbf{sexual life cycle}. Although the human life cycle exemplifies this pattern, considerable diversity in sexual life cycle organization exists among organisms.

      \sbs{0.5}{
        Plants and certain algae exhibit \textbf{alternation of generations}, a life cycle characterized by multicellular diploid and haploid stages. The diploid generation, termed the \textbf{sporophyte}, undergoes meiosis to generate haploid \textbf{spores}. In contrast to gametes, spores do not undergo fusion but rather proliferate mitotically to establish a multicellular haploid \textbf{gametophyte}. Subsequently, the gametophyte produces gametes through mitosis. Fertilization of these gametes yields a diploid zygote that develops into the succeeding sporophyte generation. This cyclical pattern---wherein sporophytes generate gametophytes, which in turn produce sporophytes---constitutes the \textit{alternation between generations}.
      }{
        \myfig{0.75}{genetics_figures/alt_cycle1}{Alternation of generations sexual life cycle}{fig:fig3}
      }

      \sbs{0.5}{
        Most fungi and certain protists, including some algal species, demonstrate a life cycle in which \textbf{the diploid phase is restricted to the unicellular zygote stage}. Following gametic fusion to form a diploid zygote, \textbf{meiosis proceeds immediately}, generating haploid cells rather than permitting development of a multicellular diploid organism. These haploid cells subsequently undergo mitotic divisions, establishing either unicellular populations or multicellular haploid organisms. Gamete production then occurs mitotically within the haploid organism, thereby completing the reproductive cycle.
      }{
        \myfig{0.75}{genetics_figures/alt_cycle2}{Life cycle with zygotic meiosis; \textit{characteristic of most fungi and some protists}}{fig:fig4}
      }

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Meiosis}

    \begin{core}[label=core:meiosis]{Meiosis}

      \textbf{Meiosis} constitutes the specialized cell division process through which \textbf{germ cells} within the gonads---ovaries in females and testes in males---generate gametes. Similar to \textbf{mitosis} (\textit{\autoref{cyt-core:mitosis}}), meiosis is preceded by an interphase period that encompasses S phase, during which chromosomal replication occurs. Meiosis diverges from mitosis, however, in comprising two successive division events---meiosis I (\textit{\autoref{core:meiosis_1}}) and meiosis II (\textit{\autoref{core:meiosis_2}})---that collectively yield four haploid daughter cells.

      Each meiotic division encompasses the canonical phases of prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase, with cytokinesis ensuing. These phases are distinguished by Roman numeral designations (\textit{I or II}) to denote their occurrence within either meiosis I or meiosis II.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:meiosis_1]{Meiosis I}

      \begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item \textbf{Prophase I:} Centrosome separation initiates spindle apparatus assembly, concurrent with nuclear envelope disintegration and kinetochore formation. During this phase, \textbf{crossing over} (\textit{\autoref{core:crossing_over}}) occurs generating cytologically visible structures termed \textbf{chiasmata}.
        }{
          \myfig{0.65}{genetics_figures/m1_1}{Prophase I}{fig:prophase1}
        }

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item \textbf{Metaphase I:} \textbf{Homologous chromosome} pairs align at the metaphase plate, with each pair orienting independently of all other pairs, thereby facilitating \textbf{independent assortment}.
        }{
          \myfig{0.65}{genetics_figures/m1_2}{Metaphase I}{fig:metaphase1}
        }

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item \textbf{Anaphase I:} Cleavage of cohesin proteins along chromosome arms permits homologous chromosome separation, while sister chromatids maintain attachment at their centromeric regions.
        }{
          \myfig{0.6}{genetics_figures/m1_3}{Anaphase I}{fig:anaphase1}
        }

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item \textbf{Telophase I \& Cytokinesis:} Cleavage furrow formation in animal cells partitions the cytoplasm, yielding two daughter cells, each possessing a haploid complement of duplicated chromosomes.
        }{
          \myfig{0.55}{genetics_figures/m1_4}{Telophase I}{fig:telophase1}
        }

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:meiosis_2]{Meiosis II}

      \begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item \textbf{Prophase II:} Spindle apparatus assembly proceeds, nuclear envelope disintegration occurs if reformation had ensued following meiosis I, and kinetochores establish connections with spindle microtubules.
        }{
          \myfig{0.65}{genetics_figures/m2_1}{Prophase II}{fig:prophase2}
        }

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item \textbf{Metaphase II:} Chromosomes achieve metaphase plate alignment. Due to crossing over in meiosis I, the two sister chromatids of each chromosome are no longer genetically identical. Sister chromatid kinetochores form attachments to microtubules emanating from opposing spindle poles.
        }{
          \myfig{0.65}{genetics_figures/m2_2}{Metaphase II}{fig:metaphase2}
        }

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item \textbf{Anaphase II:} Cleavage of centromeric cohesin proteins liberates sister chromatids, enabling their poleward migration. Each segregated chromatid now constitutes an individual chromosome.
        }{
          \myfig{0.65}{genetics_figures/m2_3}{Anaphase II}{fig:anaphase2}
        }

        \sbs{0.65}{
          \item \textbf{Telophase II \& Cytokinesis:} Nuclear envelope reformation encompasses each chromosome set, which initiates decondensation. Cytokinesis subsequently ensues.
        }{
          \myfig{0.65}{genetics_figures/m2_4}{Telophase II}{fig:telophase2}
        }

      \end{enumerate}

      \textit{Note that the events depicted above occur in both daughter cells generated during meiosis I.}

    \end{core}

    The complete meiotic process yields four daughter cells, each harboring a haploid complement of unduplicated chromosomes that are genetically distinct both from one another and from the parental cell.

    \begin{core}[label=core:crossing_over]{Crossing Over}

      A fundamental distinction between meiosis and mitosis resides in the occurrence of \textbf{crossing over} during prophase I of meiosis. This process entails the reciprocal exchange of homologous DNA segments between nonsister chromatids of homologous chromosome pairs.

      \myfig{0.55}{genetics_figures/crossing_over}{Crossing over}{fig:fig7}

      The cytologically visible sites of crossing over are designated \textbf{chiasmata}. Proper chromosomal alignment and segregation during meiosis I typically requires at least one crossover event per homologous pair, as chiasmata function to maintain homolog association until their disjunction during anaphase I.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Genetic Variation}

      Meiosis and sexual reproduction generate substantial genetic variation through three distinct mechanisms.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Independent assortment of chromosomes} describes the random orientation of \textbf{homologous chromosome} pairs at the metaphase plate during \textbf{metaphase I} of meiosis.

        \myfig{0.7}{genetics_figures/metaphase1}{Independent assortment of chromosomes}{fig:fig8}

        Each homologous pair may orient such that either the maternal or paternal homolog faces a given spindle pole. Consequently, a 50\% probability exists that any daughter cell resulting from meiosis I will receive the maternal chromosome of a given pair, with an equivalent probability for paternal chromosome inheritance.

        Given that each of the 23 homologous chromosome pairs in humans assorts independently, this mechanism alone generates $2^{23}$ (\textit{exceeding 8 million}) possible combinations of maternal and paternal chromosomes within a single gamete---a figure that precedes consideration of the subsequent two mechanisms.

        \item The \textbf{random nature of fertilization} amplifies genetic variation. Because any of the $2^{23}$ possible chromosomal combinations in a male gamete may unite with any of the $2^{23}$ combinations in a female gamete, the resultant zygote may exhibit $2^{46}$ (\textit{exceeding 70 trillion}) possible configurations of maternal and paternal chromosomes.

        \item \textbf{Crossing over} during prophase I of meiosis I generates \textbf{recombinant chromosomes} that exhibit genetic configurations distinct from the parental maternal and paternal homologs. Given that crossing over may occur at numerous positions along each homologous pair and may transpire multiple times per pair, the spectrum of possible genetic combinations expands dramatically.

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

\section{Mendelian Genetics}

  \subsection{Inheritance and Mendel's Peas}

    \begin{core}{Mendel's Experiments}

      Beginning around 1857, Gregor Mendel conducted systematic experiments on plant species in Brno, focusing on traits exhibiting discrete alternative forms. His experimental design employed \textbf{true-breeding} plants---lines producing identical offspring through repeated self-pollination---as the \textbf{P generation} (\textit{parental generation}). \textbf{Hybridization} between contrasting true-breeding varieties yielded the \textbf{F1 generation}, which upon self-pollination produced the \textbf{F2 generation}. Analysis of phenotypic ratios across these generations enabled Mendel to deduce the fundamental principles of inheritance.

      \myfig{0.8}{genetics_figures/mendel_flowers}{Mendel's experiments examining flower color}{fig:fig9}

      The prevailing \textit{blending hypothesis} predicted that parental traits would blend uniformly in offspring, producing intermediate phenotypes that would persist across generations. Mendel's crosses of true-breeding purple- and white-flowered plants contradicted this model: F1 offspring displayed exclusively purple flowers rather than an intermediate color, and white flowers reappeared in the F2 generation at an approximate 3:1 ratio (\textit{purple:white}). These results established that hereditary factors are transmitted as discrete units (\textit{now recognized as genes}), in which \textbf{dominant} alleles mask the phenotypic expression of \textbf{recessive} alleles.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Mendel's Model of Inheritance}

      Mendel's model of inheritance rests on four foundational principles.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item Alternative versions of genes (\textit{alleles}) account for variations in inherited characters.

        \item For each character, an organism inherits two \textbf{alleles} of a gene, one from each parent.

        \item When alleles at a given locus differ, the \textbf{dominant} allele (\textit{denoted by uppercase notation, e.g., P}) determines the phenotype, whereas the \textbf{recessive} allele (\textit{lowercase notation, e.g., p}) has no phenotypic effect in the presence of the dominant allele.

        \item The two alleles for a heritable character segregate during gamete formation, such that each gamete receives only one allele (\textit{the law of segregation; \autoref{core:segregation}}).

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:pheno_geno]{Phenotypes \& Genotypes}

      Organisms possessing two identical alleles for a gene are \textbf{homozygous} (\textit{PP or pp}), whereas those with two different alleles are \textbf{heterozygous} (\textit{Pp}).

      Because different allelic combinations can produce identical observable traits, appearance does not necessarily reveal genetic composition. We therefore distinguish between an organism's \textbf{phenotype} (\textit{observable traits}) and its \textbf{genotype} (\textit{genetic makeup}).


      \sbs{0.48}{
        Using these definitions: \textit{PP} represents a homozygous dominant genotype expressing the dominant phenotype; \textit{Pp} represents a heterozygous genotype also expressing the dominant phenotype; and \textit{pp} represents a homozygous recessive genotype expressing the recessive phenotype. Importantly, identical phenotypes need not correspond to identical genotypes.

        Organisms displaying a dominant phenotype may be either homozygous dominant (\textit{PP}) or heterozygous (\textit{Pp}). To determine the genotype, a \textbf{testcross} is performed by crossing the unknown individual with a homozygous recessive individual (\textit{pp}). If any offspring exhibit the recessive phenotype, the unknown parent must be heterozygous (\textit{Pp}). If all offspring exhibit the dominant phenotype, the unknown parent is most likely homozygous dominant (\textit{PP}).
      }{
        \myfig{1}{genetics_figures/testcross}{Test cross}{fig:fig11}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:segregation]{Law of Segregation}

      \sbs{0.6}{
        The \textbf{law of segregation} states that the two alleles for each gene separate during gamete formation, such that each gamete receives only one allele.
        
        The law of segregation can be illustrated using a \textbf{Punnett square} (\textit{\autoref{fig:punnett_example}}), which displays all possible allele combinations in offspring given parental \textbf{genotypes} (\textit{\autoref{core:pheno_geno}}). Each cell in the grid represents an equally likely fertilization event, and the relative frequencies of different genotypes can be predicted from the number of cells showing each combination. 

        The predictive power of the law of segregation rests on probability. While the Punnett square shows all possible outcomes, each individual offspring results from a single random fertilization. 
      }{

        }{Punnett square showing cross between heterozygous father (\textit{Rr}) and homozygous dominant mother (\textit{RR})}{fig:punnett_example}{0.8}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Law of Independent Assortment \& Dihybrid Crosses}

      Mendel's \textbf{law of independent assortment} states that alleles of different genes assort independently during gamete formation.

      \myfig{0.82}{genetics_figures/dihybrid}{Dihybrid cross examining seed color and shape}{fig:fig12}

      In order to derive this law, Mendel also examined two characters simultaneously, such as seed color (\textit{yellow/Y or green/y}) and seed shape (\textit{round/R or wrinkled/r}). Crossing true-breeding parents differing in both traits (\textit{YYRR × yyrr}) produced F1 \textbf{dihybrids} heterozygous for both characters (\textit{YyRr}).

      If genes were inherited together (\textit{dependent assortment}), F2 offspring would exhibit a 3:1 phenotypic ratio. However, independent assortment produces the characteristic 9:3:3:1 ratio observed in \textbf{dihybrid crosses}. Mendel's experimental results supported independent assortment.

    \end{core}
  
  \subsection{Complex Inheritance Patterns}

    While Mendel established foundational principles for simple traits, many heritable characteristics exhibit more complex patterns, and the genotype-phenotype relationship is often not straightforward.

    \begin{core}{Single Gene Abnormal Inheritance Patterns}

      We first consider non-Mendelian patterns involving a single gene.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Complete dominance:} In Mendel's classic pea crosses, F1 offspring always resembled one parental variety because one allele exhibited complete dominance over the other.

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Incomplete dominance:} For some genes, neither allele is completely dominant, and F1 hybrids exhibit an intermediate phenotype. For example, crossing true-breeding red and white flowers produces F1 offspring with pink flowers.
        }{
          \myfig{0.9}{genetics_figures/incomplete_dominance}{Incomplete dominance of flower color}{fig:fig13}
        }

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Codominance:} Both alleles independently affect the phenotype in distinguishable ways. For example, the human MN blood group is determined by codominant alleles encoding M and N surface molecules on red blood cells. Homozygous individuals express only one molecule type, whereas heterozygous individuals express both.
        }{
          \myfig{0.7}{genetics_figures/codominance}{Codominance of flower color}{fig:fig14}
        }

        \textbf{Whether a gene displays incomplete dominance or codominance depends on the level of phenotypic analysis.} The heterozygous pink flower in \autoref{fig:fig13}, for instance, appears uniformly pink macroscopically (\textit{incomplete dominance}), yet molecular analysis may reveal a mixture of red and white pigment molecules (\textit{codominance}). Classification therefore depends on observational scale.

        \sbs{0.35}{
          \item \textbf{Multiple Alleles:} Most genes exist in more than two allelic forms within a population. The human ABO blood group, for instance, is determined by three alleles: $I^A$, $I^B$, and $i$, with each individual possessing any two of these.
        }{
          \myfig{0.9}{genetics_figures/multiple_alleles}{Multiple alleles of human blood groups}{fig:fig15}
        }

        \item \textbf{Pleiotropy:} Single genes may influence multiple phenotypic traits. For instance, the gene determining pea flower color also affects seed coat color (\textit{gray or white}).

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}   

    \begin{core}{Multi-Gene Abnormal Inheritance Patterns}

      We now consider non-Mendelian patterns involving multiple genes.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \sbs{0.4}{
          \item \textbf{Epistasis:} The phenotypic expression of one gene modifies the expression of another gene at a different \textbf{locus} (\textit{chromosomal location}). In Labrador retrievers, coat color is determined by two genes: one locus controls pigment type (\textit{black B is dominant to brown b}), while a second locus controls pigment deposition (\textit{dominant E allows deposition}). However, homozygous recessive individuals (\textit{ee}) have yellow coats regardless of genotype at the pigment-type locus.
        }{
          \myfig{0.9}{genetics_figures/epistasis}{Epistasis of Labrador retriever coat color}{fig:fig16}
        }

        \item \textbf{Polygenic Inheritance:} Many characteristics (\textit{e.g., skin color, height}) exhibit continuous variation rather than discrete alternatives. Such \textbf{quantitative characters} result from the additive effects of multiple genes. Below is a simplified model of skin color determined by three genes.

        \myfig{0.58}{genetics_figures/polygenic_inheritance}{Polygenic inheritance of skin color}{fig:fig17}

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Multifactorial Characteristics}

      While genotype provides the foundation for phenotype, certain characteristics (\textit{such as skin color}) are also influenced by environmental factors (\textit{e.g., sun exposure}). Such traits are termed \textbf{multifactorial}, reflecting the combined influence of multiple genetic and environmental factors on phenotypic expression.

    \end{core}
 
  \subsection{Use Cases for Mendelian Patterns of Inheritance}

    \begin{core}{Pedigrees}

      \sbs{0.4}{
        In lieu of controlled breeding experiments (\textit{infeasible for ethical reasons}), human geneticists analyze inheritance patterns using \textbf{pedigrees}, which are family trees that trace the transmission of a particular trait across generations.
      }{
        \myfig{0.95}{genetics_figures/pedigree}{Pedigree analysis of widow's peak (\textit{recessive trait})}{fig:fig18}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Pedigree Analysis of Disorders}

      Pedigree analysis is particularly valuable for studying disease-causing alleles. In \textbf{recessive disorders}, heterozygous individuals (\textit{Aa}) exhibit normal phenotypes because one functional allele produces sufficient protein. These \textbf{carriers} can transmit the recessive allele to offspring, though only homozygous recessive individuals (\textit{aa}) express the disorder. Most affected individuals are born to phenotypically normal carrier parents, and lethal recessive alleles remain rare because affected individuals typically do not reproduce.
      
      In \textbf{dominant disorders}, heterozygous individuals are phenotypically affected. \textbf{Achondroplasia}, a form of dwarfism caused by a dominant allele, persists in populations because the condition does not prevent reproduction; unaffected individuals are homozygous recessive. Dominant lethal alleles are generally rare because affected individuals die before reproducing. However, such alleles can persist if symptoms manifest after reproductive age, as in \textbf{Huntington's disease}, a neurodegenerative disorder with typical onset in mid-adulthood.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Multifactorial Diseases}

      While the preceding discussion focused on single-locus Mendelian disorders, many common diseases have a \textbf{multifactorial} basis involving both genetic predisposition and environmental influences. Susceptibility to cardiovascular disease or stroke, for instance, depends on both genotype and lifestyle factors (\textit{smoking, exercise, etc.}).

    \end{core}

\section{The Chromosomal Basis of Inheritance}
    
  \subsection{Sex-Linked Genes}

    When Mendel described the laws of inheritance in 1866, chromosomes had not yet been identified as the carriers of genetic information. It was not until 1902 that scientists began to formulate the \textbf{chromosome theory of inheritance}. This theory states that Mendelian genes occupy specific loci (sites) on chromosomes, and that the behavior of chromosomes during meiosis—specifically segregation and independent assortment—accounts for the inheritance patterns Mendel observed.

    \begin{core}{Hunt's Fruit Fly Experiment}

      The first strong evidence linking a specific gene to a specific chromosome came in the early 1900s from the work of Thomas Hunt Morgan. Morgan studied a species of fruit fly, \textit{Drosophila melanogaster}, focusing on inheritance of eye color.

      \myfig{0.5}{genetics_figures/fly_eye_color}{caption}{fig:fig19}

      In wild populations, the most common eye color is red, referred to as the \textbf{wild-type} phenotype and denoted as $w^+$. After two years of breeding flies, Morgan discovered a single male with white eyes instead of red. This trait was designated the \textbf{mutant phenotype} and denoted as $w$.

      When Morgan crossed his white-eyed male fly with red-eyed females, all of the F1 offspring had red eyes, indicating that the wild-type allele is dominant over the white-eye allele. When the F1 flies were interbred, the F2 generation displayed an overall 3:1 red-to-white phenotypic ratio. However, a closer look revealed that the white-eye phenotype appeared only in males: approximately half of the males were white-eyed and half were red-eyed, while all females had red eyes. This sex-specific pattern led Morgan to conclude that the eye-color gene is located on the X chromosome.

      Because all F1 offspring were phenotypically wild type, the F1 males (XY) must have carried a wild-type allele on their single X chromosome. The F1 females, however, must have been heterozygous, carrying one wild-type allele and one mutant allele. The appearance of white-eyed males in the F2 generation confirms this: sons inherit their single X chromosome exclusively from their mother. Therefore, any white-eyed male must have received the mutant allele from a heterozygous F1 female, demonstrating that she was a carrier of the recessive white-eye allele.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:sex_chromosomes]{Sex Chromosomes}

      Humans and other mammals possess two sex chromosomes, X and Y. Females typically inherit two X chromosomes (XX), whereas males inherit one X and one Y (XY). The Y chromosome is considerably smaller than the X chromosome, and only short regions at their ends are homologous. These homologous regions allow the X and Y chromosomes to pair and properly segregate during meiosis in males.

      During gamete formation, the sex chromosomes separate so that every egg carries a single X chromosome, while sperm carry either an X or a Y chromosome. Fertilization by an X-bearing sperm produces an XX zygote (female), whereas fertilization by a Y-bearing sperm produces an XY zygote (male). As a result, sex determination in mammals is typically an approximately 1:1 probability. However, the mammalian X-Y system represents only one of several mechanisms of sex determination found in nature.

      \myfig{0.5}{genetics_figures/alt_systems}{caption}{fig:fig20}

    \end{core}    

    \begin{core}{Sex-Linked Genes}

      A gene located on a sex chromosome is called a \textbf{sex-linked gene}. Genes found on the X chromosome are termed \textbf{X-linked}, whereas those on the Y chromosome are \textbf{Y-linked}. The human X chromosome contains approximately 1,100 genes, while the Y chromosome contains about 78 genes that encode roughly 25 distinct proteins, many of which are involved in testis development and sperm production. Because the Y chromosome is transmitted largely unchanged from father to son and carries relatively few genes, comparatively few traits and disorders are inherited in a strictly Y-linked manner.

      X-linked genes follow the inheritance pattern first observed by Morgan in his fruit fly experiments. A father passes his single X chromosome—and therefore any X-linked alleles—exclusively to his daughters, not to his sons. Mothers, who possess two X chromosomes, can pass X-linked alleles to both sons and daughters.

      \myfig{1}{genetics_figures/sex_chromo}{caption}{fig:fig21}

      The phenotypic expression of an X-linked trait in females depends on whether the allele is dominant or recessive. Because males possess only one X chromosome, the terms homozygous and heterozygous do not apply to them. Instead, males are described as hemizygous for X-linked genes and will express whatever allele is present on the single X chromosome inherited from their mother.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Barr Bodies}

      Although female mammals inherit two X chromosomes, one is largely inactivated early in embryonic development. This process ensures that males and females have a similar dosage of most X-linked gene products. The inactivated X chromosome condenses into a \textbf{Barr body} at the nuclear periphery, and most of its genes are transcriptionally silent. In the germline cells that give rise to eggs, however, the inactive X is reactivated prior to meiosis so that each egg ultimately contains one active X chromosome.

      As a result of X inactivation, females are genetic \textbf{mosaics}, composed of two populations of cells: those in which the maternal X chromosome is active and those in which the paternal X chromosome is active. Once an X chromosome is inactivated in a particular cell, all of its mitotic descendants maintain the same inactive X. Consequently, a heterozygous female for an X-linked trait will typically have a mixture of cells expressing one allele and cells expressing the alternate allele.

      \myfig{0.52}{genetics_figures/barr_body}{caption}{fig:fig22}

      This phenomenon is also observed in women who are heterozygous for an X-linked mutation that prevents the development of sweat glands, resulting in distinct patches of normal skin interspersed with patches lacking sweat glands.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Linked Genes}

    \begin{core}{Linked Genes}

      Genes located near one another on the same chromosome tend to be inherited together during genetic crosses; such genes are described as \textbf{genetically linked} and are referred to as \textbf{linked genes}. Consider the following experiment in which true-breeding flies that are double wild type are crossed with true-breeding flies that are double mutant.

      \myfig{0.85}{genetics_figures/linked_flies}{caption}{fig:fig23}

      If the two genes are located on different chromosomes, independent assortment predicts a 1:1:1:1 phenotypic ratio in the testcross progeny. If the genes are located on the same chromosome and the parental alleles are always inherited together (complete linkage), the expected ratio would be 1:1:0:0, with only parental phenotypes observed. However, the actual data from Morgan's experiment yielded a ratio of 965:944:206:185---an intermediate result between these two expectations. We now understand that this deviation is due to \textbf{crossing over}, although the precise mechanism was not yet known at the time.

    \end{core}    

    \begin{core}{Parental Types vs Recombinants}

      Before proceeding, it is helpful to clarify key terminology. \textbf{Genetic recombination} refers to the production of offspring with combinations of traits that differ from those present in either parent of the P generation. The trait combinations that match the original parental combinations are called \textbf{parental types}, whereas those that differ are called \textbf{recombinant types}, or simply \textbf{recombinants}.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Linkage Maps}

      Crossing over occurs randomly along homologous chromosomes during prophase I of meiosis. The probability of a crossover occurring between two genes increases with the physical distance separating them. Therefore, the farther apart two genes are on the same chromosome, the greater the likelihood that a crossover will occur between them, resulting in a higher recombination frequency. This frequency approaches 50\%, the value expected for genes that assort independently (as if located on different chromosomes).

      Using recombination frequencies, one of Morgan's student, Alfred H. Sturtevant, constructed the first genetic maps by assigning relative positions to genes on the same chromosome. These maps, based on recombination data observed in the fruit fly species, are known as \textbf{linkage maps}.

      \myfig{0.7}{genetics_figures/linkage_map}{caption}{fig:fig24}

      Thus, genetic linkage is not a strictly binary property that classifies a pair of genes as simply "linked" or "not linked." Rather, linkage exists along a continuum and is determined by the physical distance between genes on a chromosome: the closer two genes are, the more tightly linked they are, and the lower their recombination frequency.

    \end{core}
  
  \subsection{Chromosomal Disorders}

    So far, we have focused on relatively small-scale genetic changes involving individual genes. However, large-scale chromosomal alterations can also significantly affect an organism's phenotype and may result in miscarriage or developmental disorders.

    \begin{core}{Abnormal Chromosome Counts}

      First, consider \textit{abnormal chromosome numbers}. These typically arise from an occasional error called \textbf{nondisjunction}, in which homologous chromosomes fail to separate properly during meiosis I or sister chromatids fail to separate during meiosis II. As a result, some gametes receive an abnormal number of chromosomes.

      \myfig{0.65}{genetics_figures/nondisjunction}{caption}{fig:fig25}

      If an abnormal gamete fuses with a normal one during fertilization, the resulting zygote will have an abnormal chromosome number—a condition known as \textbf{aneuploidy}. The loss of a single chromosome produces a \textbf{monosomic} zygote ($2n - 1$), whereas the gain of an extra chromosome produces a \textbf{trisomic} zygote ($2n + 1$). Once formed, this chromosomal abnormality is propagated to all somatic cells of the developing embryo through mitotic divisions.

      In contrast, some organisms possess more than two complete chromosome \textbf{sets} (not merely individual extra chromosomes) in their somatic cells. This condition is called \textbf{polyploidy} and includes forms such as \textit{triploidy} ($3n$) and \textit{tetraploidy} ($4n$).    

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Abnormal Chromosome Structure}

      In addition to changes in chromosome number, errors during meiosis or exposure to damaging agents such as radiation can \textbf{alter chromosome structure}. These structural abnormalities fall into four major categories:

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Deletion} occurs when a chromosomal fragment is lost, leaving the affected chromosome missing certain genes.

        \myfig{0.5}{genetics_figures/deletion}{caption}{fig:fig26}

        \item \textbf{Duplication} occurs when a chromosomal fragment becomes attached as an extra segment to a sister chromatid or to a non-sister chromatid, resulting in repeated gene sequences.

        \myfig{0.5}{genetics_figures/duplication}{caption}{fig:fig27}

        \item \textbf{Inversion} occurs when a chromosomal fragment breaks off and reattaches to the original chromosome in the reverse orientation, thereby reversing the gene order within that segment.

        \myfig{0.5}{genetics_figures/inversion}{caption}{fig:fig28}

        \item \textbf{Translocation} occurs when a chromosomal fragment breaks off and attaches to a nonhomologous chromosome. In some cases, two nonhomologous chromosomes exchange segments, a process known as a reciprocal translocation.

        \myfig{0.5}{genetics_figures/translocation}{caption}{fig:fig29}

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Notable Chromosomal Abnormalities}

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Down syndrome}, also known as \textit{trisomy 21}, is a chromosomal disorder caused by the presence of an extra copy of chromosome 21.

        \myfig{0.5}{genetics_figures/down_syndrome}{caption}{fig:fig30}

        Aneuploid conditions involving the sex chromosomes tend to disrupt genetic balance less severely than those involving autosomes. This is partly because the Y chromosome contains relatively few genes, and additional X chromosomes are largely inactivated as Barr bodies, reducing their impact on gene expression.

        \item \textbf{Klinefelter syndrome} is a chromosomal disorder in which individuals possess an extra X chromosome (XXY). Affected individuals are phenotypically male and may exhibit features such as small testes, reduced testosterone levels, taller-than-average height, decreased muscle mass, sparse facial and body hair, and gynecomastia (enlarged breast tissue). Many individuals are infertile.

        Females with \textbf{trisomy X} (XXX) are generally healthy and often have no unusual physical features other than slightly above-average height. However, there is an increased risk of learning disabilities and mild developmental delays.

        \item \textbf{Turner syndrome} (monosomy X, XO) is the only known viable full monosomy in humans. Individuals with Turner syndrome are phenotypically female but are typically sterile due to underdeveloped ovaries and incomplete sexual maturation.

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}
  
  \subsection{Exceptions to Mendelian Inheritance}

    In the previous section, we examined deviations from typical chromosomal inheritance patterns. However, there are also two naturally occurring exceptions to classical Mendelian inheritance: \textbf{genomic imprinting} and \textbf{inheritance of organelle genes}.

    \begin{core}{Genomic Imprinting}

      \textbf{Genomic imprinting} refers to a phenomenon in which the phenotypic effect of an allele depends on whether it was inherited from the mother or the father. For a given imprinted gene, either the maternal or the paternal allele is consistently silenced across individuals of a species, while the other allele is expressed. This parent-specific expression is typically regulated by epigenetic modifications such as DNA methylation established during gamete formation. An example of genomic imprinting is the \textit{insulin-like growth factor 2} (\textit{Igf2}) gene in mice, in which only the paternal allele is expressed.

      \myfig{0.6}{genetics_figures/imprinting}{caption}{fig:fig31}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Inheritance of Organelle Genes}

      \textbf{Inheritance of organelle genes} refers to the transmission of genes located outside the nucleus, specifically the small circular DNA molecules found in mitochondria (and in chloroplasts in plants).

      In most animals—and in plants with respect to chloroplast DNA—a zygote receives nearly all of its cytoplasm and organelles from the egg. The sperm typically contributes little more than a haploid set of nuclear chromosomes. As a result, mitochondrial DNA (and chloroplast DNA in plants) is usually inherited exclusively from the mother, a pattern known as \textit{maternal inheritance}.    

    \end{core}
