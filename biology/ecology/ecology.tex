\section{Ecology Overview}

  \begin{core}{Ecology}

    \textbf{Ecology} is the scientific study of the interactions between organisms and their environment---consisting of both biotic (\textit{living}) and abiotic (\textit{nonliving}) components.

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Climate}

    \textbf{Climate} refers to the long-term prevailing weather conditions in a given area and is primarily controlled by four key factors---temperature, precipitation, sunlight, and wind patterns---which collectively determine the types of ecosystems that can exist in a region.

    The following are factors that substantially influence regional climate.

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item \textbf{Seasonality}: Earth's axial tilt and its annual orbital path around the sun create pronounced seasonal cycles in day length, solar radiation intensity, and temperature. This seasonality drives many biological phenomena including migration, hibernation, and reproductive cycles.

      \sbs{0.5}{
        \item \textbf{Bodies of Water}: Ocean currents significantly influence climate along continental coastlines by heating or cooling overlying air masses that pass across the neighboring land. Currents such as the Gulf Stream transport warm water northward, heating adjacent air masses that then pass across land. Similarly, cold currents cool coastal air, creating distinct microclimates.
      }{
        \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/gulf_stream}{The gulf stream}{fig:fig1}
      }

      \sbs{0.5}{
        \item \textbf{Mountains}: Like large bodies of water, mountains influence airflow patterns. As air masses encounter mountain ranges, they are forced upward, leading to precipitation on windward slopes and rain shadow effects on leeward sides. Mountains also create altitudinal zonation of climate zones.
      }{
        \myfig{0.75}{ecology_figures/mountains}{Windward slope (\textit{left}) and leeward side (\textit{right})}{fig:fig2}
      }

    \end{enumerate}

  \end{core}

  \subsection{Terrestrial Ecosystems}

    \textbf{Terrestrial biomes} are major ecological divisions characterized by distinct climatic conditions and associated vegetation. While plant species distribution is largely determined by climate---particularly temperature and precipitation---terrestrial biomes are typically named for their distinctive climatic features and predominant vegetation types.

    \begin{core}{Terrestrial Biome Types}

      \begin{enumerate}

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Tropical Forest}: Found near the equator with high temperatures and precipitation year-round. Characterized by dense, vertically stratified vegetation and high species diversity.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/tropical_forest}{Tropical forest}{fig:fig3}
        }

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Desert}: Arid regions with low precipitation, extreme daily temperature fluctuations, and sparse vegetation adapted to water conservation.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/desert}{Desert}{fig:fig4}
        }

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Savanna}: Tropical or subtropical grasslands with scattered trees, characterized by alternating wet and dry seasons.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/savanna}{Savanna}{fig:fig5}
        }

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Chaparral}: Mediterranean climate with mild, wet winters and hot, dry summers. Characterized by dense, evergreen shrubs and low diversity.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/chaparral}{Chaparral}{fig:fig6}
        }

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Temperate Grassland}: Mid-latitude regions with deep, fertile soils and seasonal precipitation. Dominated by grasses and few trees.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/temperate_grassland}{Temperate grassland}{fig:fig7}
        }

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Northern Coniferous Forest (Taiga)}: Wide bands across North America and Eurasia with cold climates and evergreen coniferous trees.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/northern_coniferous_forest}{Northern coniferous forest}{fig:fig8}
        }

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Temperate Broadleaf Forest}: Mid-latitude forests with deciduous trees that lose leaves seasonally and moderate precipitation.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/temperate_broadleaf_forest}{Temperate broadleaf forest}{fig:fig9}
        }

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Tundra}: Arctic or alpine regions with extremely cold temperatures, short growing seasons, and low vegetation (mosses, lichens, sedges).
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/tundra}{Tundra}{fig:fig10}
        }

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Aquatic Ecosystems}

    Aquatic ecosystems encompass all water-based environments, covering approximately 70\% of Earth's surface. These ecosystems are fundamentally shaped by physical and chemical properties of water, including light penetration, temperature stratification, salinity, and nutrient availability. Unlike terrestrial ecosystems where climate primarily determines biome distribution, aquatic ecosystems are organized around water depth, flow, and chemistry.

    \begin{core}[label=core:azones]{Aquatic Zones}

      Aquatic environments are characterized by vertical stratification---both physical and chemical---that creates distinct zones with different environmental conditions.


      \begin{enumerate}

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Photic Zone}: The shallow-surface layer where sufficient light penetrates to support photosynthesis.

          \item \textbf{Aphotic Zone}: The deeper, dark region beneath the photic zone where light is insufficient for photosynthesis.

          \item \textbf{Pelagic Zone}: The open-water region extending from the surface to the ocean floor, encompassing both photic and aphotic zones.

          \item \textbf{Abyssal Zone}: The deep ocean region typically between 2,000 and 6,000 meters, characterized by extreme cold, high pressure, and complete darkness.

          \item \textbf{Benthic Zone}: The bottom layer of aquatic environments (\textit{deep and shallow}), inhabited by organisms collectively known as \textbf{benthos}. Organic \textbf{detritus} (\textit{dead organic material}) from upper layers provides energy for benthic organisms.
        }{
          \myfig{0.95}{ecology_figures/aquatic_zones}{Aquatic zones}{fig:fig11}
        }

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Thermocline and Seasonal Turnovers}

      The stratification of aquatic zones (\autoref{core:azones}) is not static---it is largely driven by temperature gradients within the water column. These gradients are defined by the \textbf{thermocline}, a narrow zone where temperature changes rapidly with depth, separating the warm surface layer (\textit{epilimnion}) from the colder deep waters (\textit{hypolimnion}).

      \myfig{1}{ecology_figures/thermocline}{Thermocline and seasonal turnover}{fig:fig12}

      This thermal stratification shifts with the seasons through a process known as \textbf{seasonal turnover}---a semi-annual mixing event occurring in spring and autumn. In summer, warm surface water overlies colder deep water; in winter, this pattern inverts as surface water cools. During turnover, these layers mix, redistributing nutrients, oxygen, and heat throughout the water column.

    \end{core}

    \textbf{Aquatic biomes} are defined by their water-based environments and are broadly categorized into \textbf{freshwater} and \textbf{marine} biomes, characterized by physical and chemical properties such as salinity, water flow, depth, and light availability.

    \begin{core}{Aquatic Biome Types}

      \begin{enumerate}

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Lakes}: Standing bodies of freshwater, ranging from \textbf{oligotrophic} (\textit{nutrient-poor, oxygen-rich}) to \textbf{eutrophic} (\textit{nutrient-rich, oxygen-depleted}).
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/lake}{Lakes}{fig:fig13}
        }

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Wetlands}: Temporarily or permanently inundated areas with hydric soils. Include marshes, swamps, and bogs. Characterized by emergent vegetation adapted to saturated soils.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/wetland}{Wetlands}{fig:fig14}
        }

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Streams and Rivers}: Flowing water systems moving from headwaters to mouths. Physical structure changes along the gradient from steep, turbulent headwaters to wider, slower lower reaches.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/stream_river}{Streams and rivers}{fig:fig15}
        }

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Estuaries}: Partially enclosed coastal areas where freshwater mixes with seawater. Highly productive due to nutrient input from both sources. Support specialized communities adapted to variable salinity.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/estuaries}{Estuaries}{fig:fig16}
        }

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Intertidal Zones}: Areas between high and low tide marks. Experience regular exposure to air and submersion in seawater. Characterized by zonation patterns relating to tidal height and emersion duration.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/intertidal}{Intertidal zones}{fig:fig17}
        }

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Oceanic Pelagic Zones}: Open ocean waters extending from the surface to the deep sea. Primary production by phytoplankton supports diverse marine food webs.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/pelagic}{Oceanic pelagic zones}{fig:fig18}
        }

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Coral Reefs}: Shallow tropical marine ecosystems built by carbonate-secreting corals. Among the most biodiverse marine communities, supported by symbiotic zooxanthellae.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/coral_reefs}{Coral reefs}{fig:fig19}
        }

        \sbs{0.55}{
          \item \textbf{Marine Benthic Zone}: The ocean floor from the continental shelf to the deep sea. Infauna (burrowing organisms) and epifauna (organisms living on surfaces) inhabit this zone.
        }{
          \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/benthic}{Marine Benthic zone}{fig:fig20}
        }

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}
  
\section{Population Ecology}

  \begin{core}{Population}

    A \textbf{population} is a group of individuals of a \textbf{single} species occupying the same general area and potentially interacting with one another. Populations are fundamental units of ecological organization, and their dynamics determine the fate of species and the structure of \textbf{communities} (\autoref{core:commeco}).

  \end{core} 

  \begin{core}{Density and Dispersion}

    \textbf{Population density} refers to the number of individuals per unit area or volume, providing a measure of how crowded a population is. \textbf{Population dispersion} describes the spatial pattern of individuals.

    While counting every individual in a population is sometimes possible, it is usually too difficult in practice. Instead, ecologists use sampling techniques to estimate population size and density. By finding the \textbf{average density} within randomly selected plots, researchers can use this local data to reliably \textbf{estimate} the total population across a much larger area.

    \begin{enumerate}

      \sbs{0.55}{
        \item \textbf{Clumped Dispersion}: Individuals aggregate in patches, often around resource availability. Starfish grouping together where food is abundant display this pattern.
      }{
        \myfig{0.9}{ecology_figures/clumped}{Clumped dispersion}{fig:fig21}
      }

      \sbs{0.55}{
        \item \textbf{Uniform Dispersion}: Individuals are evenly spaced, typically resulting from competition for resources or territorial behavior. Penguins nesting in colonies exemplify this pattern.
      }{
        \myfig{0.9}{ecology_figures/uniform}{Uniform dispersion}{fig:fig22}
      }

      \sbs{0.55}{
        \item \textbf{Random Dispersion}: Individuals are distributed without respect to one another, occurring when resources are randomly distributed and competition is minimal.
      }{
        \myfig{0.9}{ecology_figures/random}{Random dispersion}{fig:fig23}
      }

    \end{enumerate}

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Demography}

    \textbf{Demography} is the study of statistical characteristics of populations and how these characteristics change over time. Factors increasing population size include \textbf{birth rates} (\textit{natality}) and \textbf{immigration} (\textit{arrival of individuals from elsewhere}). Factors decreasing population size include \textbf{death rates} (\textit{mortality}) and \textbf{emigration} (\textit{departure of individuals}). The demographic characteristics of a population are assessed through the following methods.

    \begin{enumerate}

      \sbs{0.55}{
        \item \textbf{Survivorship curves} plot the proportion or number of individuals from a cohort surviving at each age, typically falling into three categories: \textbf{Type I} (\textit{high survival until old age}), \textbf{Type II} (\textit{constant mortality rate}), and \textbf{Type III} (\textit{high mortality early in life}).
      }{
        \myfig{0.9}{ecology_figures/survivorship_curve}{Idealized survivorship curves}{fig:fig24}
      }

      \item \textbf{Age structure} represents the relative number of individuals at each age, influencing a population's growth potential. Age structure diagram shapes can help investigators assess whether a population is \textbf{rapidly growing} (\textit{pyramid-shaped}), \textbf{slowly growing} (\textit{block-shaped}), or \textbf{declining} (\textit{inverted pyramid}).

      \myfig{0.92}{ecology_figures/age_structure}{Age structure diagram for Zambia (\textit{left}), United States (\textit{middle}), and Italy (\textit{right})}{fig:fig25}

      \item \textbf{Life tables} summarize the age-specific survival and reproductive rates within a population, providing critical data for understanding population dynamics.

  \subsection{Population Growth}

    \begin{core}{Exponential Growth}

      \textbf{Exponential growth} describes population increase in an \textbf{idealized, unlimited environment} where resources are abundant and population growth is constrained only by intrinsic biological factors (\textit{birth rate, generation time, etc.}). Under exponential growth, the population increases by a constant proportion each time period, producing a characteristic J-shaped curve when plotted against time.

    \begin{core}{Logistic Growth}

      \textbf{Logistic growth} describes population growth as the population approaches its environment's \textbf{carrying capacity} (\textit{denoted as K})---the maximum population size that the environment can sustain indefinitely. As resources become limited, growth rate decreases, producing an S-shaped (\textit{sigmoid}) curve.

  \subsection{Reproduction}

    \begin{core}{Life History}
      
      Natural selection favors traits that maximize an organism's reproductive success. However, resources allocated to reproduction cannot be used for survival, creating fundamental trade-offs between reproductive effort and survival. These trade-offs manifest in various life history strategies.

      A \textbf{life history} encompasses the traits that determine an organism's schedule of reproduction and survival, including age at first reproduction, frequency of reproduction, number of offspring, and level of parental investment. These traits collectively give rise to two broadly recognized reproductive strategies observed across nature.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Semelparity} represents a "big-bang" reproductive strategy, in which an organism reproduces once and then dies. This strategy is favored when reproductive opportunities are unpredictable but resource availability is high during the single reproductive event (\textit{e.g.,PPacific salmon}).

        \item \textbf{Iteroparity} represents repeated reproduction throughout an organism's lifetime. This strategy is favored when reproductive opportunities are predictable and individuals can survive to reproduce multiple times (\textit{e.g., humans, cats}).

      \end{itemize}

    \end{core}
    
  \subsection{Population Regulation and Dynamics}

    \begin{core}{Density-Dependent Population Regulation}

      \textbf{Density-dependent regulation} occurs when population growth is regulated by factors whose effects intensify as population density increases, such as the following.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Competition}: As population density increases, individuals compete for limited resources including food, space, and mates.

        \item \textbf{Disease}: Higher population densities facilitate pathogen (\autoref{core:pathogen}) transmission.

        \item \textbf{Territoriality}: Space limitations force individuals to compete for territory.

        \item \textbf{Intrinsic factors}: Physiological stress responses to crowding.

        \item \textbf{Toxic waste accumulation}: Accumulation of metabolic waste products in confined environments.

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Density-Independent Population Regulation}

      \textbf{Density-independent regulation} occurs when environmental factors affect population size regardless of density. These factors typically include forces such as \textbf{natural disasters}, \textbf{extreme weather conditions}, and \textbf{seasonal climate variations}.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Population Dynamics}

      \textbf{Population dynamics} describe fluctuations in population size over time and space, resulting from the interplay of birth rates, death rates, immigration, and emigration under the influence of \textbf{abiotic} (\textit{environmental variability}) and \textbf{biotic} (\textit{species interactions}) factors. These dynamics are closely tied to the distribution of a species, which reflect both ecological factors and evolutionary history.

      A key mechanism shaping r is \textbf{dispersal}---the movement of individuals or gametes away from their area of origin---which enables populations to expand their range and colonize new areas.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Metapopulation and Demographic Transition}

      A \textbf{metapopulation} consists of multiple spatially separated populations of the same species linked by dispersal, occupying patches of suitable habitat within a matrix of unsuitable habitat. Humans represent a prominent example, with distinct population centers interconnected through migration. Over time, as these populations develop and industrialize, they tend to undergo \textbf{demographic transition}---a historical pattern in which societies shift from high birth and death rates toward lower, more stable rates.

    \end{core}

\section{Community Ecology}

  \begin{core}[label=core:commeco]{Community}

    A \textbf{community} is a collection of individuals across \textbf{multiple} species occupying the same general area. \textbf{Community ecology} is the study of interactions between different species within a geographic area. These interactions shape community structure, diversity, and function.

  \end{core}

  \subsection{Species Interactions}

      Interspecies interactions can be categorized by their effects on the relevant species: positive (+), negative (-), or neutral (0).

    \begin{core}{Competition (-/-)}

      \textbf{Competition} occurs when individuals of different species each utilize the same limiting resource, reducing the survival and reproduction of both. This interaction is characterized by the following traits.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Competitive Exclusion}: Complete competitors cannot coexist indefinitely; the species with even a slight reproductive advantage will eventually eliminate the inferior competitor from the local area.

        \item \textbf{Ecological Niche}: The specific set of biotic and abiotic resources an organism uses in its environment, including food, habitat, and temporal patterns of activity.

        \item \textbf{Resource Partitioning}: Evolution of differences in niche dimensions among similar species whose distributions overlap, enabling coexistence.

        \item \textbf{Character Displacement}: Divergence in phenotypic traits between sympatric (\textit{overlapping}) populations compared to allopatric (\textit{non-overlapping}) populations, driven by competitive selection pressures.

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Exploitation (+/-)}

      \textbf{Exploitation} describes interactions where one species benefits at the expense of the other. Both predator and prey have evolved adaptations in this evolutionary arms race. The following are different types of exploitative interactions.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Predation}: One species (\textit{predator}) hunts, kills, and consumes another species (\textit{prey}). The following are adaptations prey have evolved over generations to increase their survivability against predators.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item \textbf{Mechanical Defense}: Physical structures that deter predation, including spines, thorns, shells, and tough integuments (\textit{e.g., porcupine quills}).

          \item \textbf{Chemical Defense}: The production of toxic, repellent, or unpalatable compounds that discourage predator attack (\textit{e.g., skunk musk}).

          \sbs{0.5}{
            \item \textbf{Aposematic Coloration}: Warning coloration in dangerous species that signals unpalatability to potential predators.
          }{
            \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/aposematic}{Aposematic coloration}{fig:aposematic}
          }

          \sbs{0.5}{
            \item \textbf{Cryptic Coloration}: Camouflage that allows prey to avoid detection from predators.
          }{
            \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/cryptic}{Cryptic coloration}{fig:cryptic}
          }

          \sbs{0.5}{
            \item \textbf{Batesian Mimicry}: A harmless species evolves appearance resembling a harmful species to gain protection.
          }{
            \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/batesian}{Batesian mimicry}{fig:batesian}
          }

          \sbs{0.5}{
            \item \textbf{Müllerian Mimicry}: Two or more harmful species evolve similar warning coloration, reinforcing predator avoidance learning.
          }{
            \myfig{0.8}{ecology_figures/mullerian}{Müllerian mimicry}{fig:mullerian}
          }

        \end{itemize}

        It is important to note that predators, too, develop adaptations that enhance their hunting efficiency. For instance, certain octopus species employ mimicry to approach prey undetected, while terrestrial predators such as the cheetah have evolved powerful musculature and skeletal structure to pursue and overtake fast-moving prey.

        \item \textbf{Herbivory}: An herbivore consumes plant material. Plants have evolved defensive adaptations including thorns, spines, toxins, and secondary compounds. Herbivores have evolved countermeasures including chemical detection mechanisms and specialized digestive systems.

        \item \textbf{Parasitism}: A parasite derives nourishment from a host organism without immediately killing it.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item \textbf{Endoparasites}: Parasites that live inside the host body (\textit{e.g., tapeworms}).

          \item \textbf{Ectoparasites}: Parasites that feed on external surfaces (\textit{e.g., ticks, lice}).

        \end{itemize}

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Positive Interactions}

      \textbf{Positive interactions} benefit one or both interacting species without harming either.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Mutualism (+/+)}: Both species benefit from the interaction. Examples include pollination relationships and mycorrhizal associations between plants and fungi.
        }{
          \myfig{0.9}{ecology_figures/mutualism}{Mutualism between acacia trees and ants}{fig:mutualism}
        }

        \sbs{0.5}{
          \item \textbf{Commensalism (+/0)}: One species benefits while the other is neither helped nor harmed. An example is epiphytic plants growing on tree branches.
        }{
          \myfig{0.9}{ecology_figures/commensalism}{Commensalism between cattle egrets and African buffalo}{fig:commensalism}
        }

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Diversity and Trophic Structure}
        
    \begin{core}{Diversity and Richness}

      \textbf{Species diversity} encompasses the variety of different organisms in a community, which can be measured in \textbf{species richness} (\textit{the number of different species in the community}) and \textbf{relative abundance} (\textit{the proportion each species represents of all individuals in the community}).

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Trophic Structure}

      \textbf{Trophic structure} describes the feeding relationships between organisms, determining energy flow through ecosystems. Organisms are organized into trophic levels based on their food source.

      \sbs{0.48}{
        \begin{enumerate}

          \item \textbf{Primary Producers}: Autotrophs that form the foundation of the trophic structure by converting solar or chemical energy into organic matter through photosynthesis or chemosynthesis.

          \item \textbf{Consumers}: Heterotrophs that obtain energy by feeding on other organisms, further classified by feeding order into primary (herbivores), secondary, and tertiary consumers (carnivores).

          \item \textbf{Decomposers}: Organisms such as fungi and bacteria that break down \textbf{detritus} (\textit{dead organic matter}), returning nutrients to the abiotic environment and completing the flow of matter through the ecosystem.

        \end{enumerate}

        A community trophic structure is nicely represented by a \textbf{food chain} (\textit{a linear sequence of consumption from producers through successive consumer levels}) diagram. However, nature is quite rarely this simple. Instead a \textbf{food web} provides a more accurate representation of trophic interactions, capturing the complex network of feeding relationships.
      }{
        \myfig{0.87}{ecology_figures/food_web}{Food web}{fig:food_web}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Biomass \& Trophic Control}

      \textbf{Biomass} refers to the total mass of living organisms in a given area, representing stored energy and serving as a key determinant of carrying capacity at each trophic level. The length of a food chain is constrained by the \textbf{energy hypothesis}, which states that the inefficiency of energy transfer between trophic levels limits the number of viable trophic levels --- on average, only approximately 10\% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. Community structure is further shaped by trophic cascades, which operate through the following two mechanism.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Bottom-up control}: Nutrient supply or food availability at lower trophic levels limits the abundance of organisms at higher trophic levels.

        \item \textbf{Top-down control}: Predation at higher trophic levels regulates the abundance of organisms at lower trophic levels.

      \end{itemize}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{High Impact Species}

      Within an ecosystem certain species exert disproportionate influence on community structure.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Foundation species}: Species that strongly influence their communities through their large size or high abundance. Examples primarily include forest trees, desert shrubs, and marine algae.

        \sbs{0.6}{
            \item \textbf{Keystone species}: Species that are not necessarily abundant but exert strong control on community structure through their pivotal ecological roles. Sea stars exemplify keystone species because they prevent mussels---which are competitively dominant and would otherwise monopolize space on rocky intertidal surfaces.
          }{
            \myfig{0.9}{ecology_figures/starfish}{Keystone species (\textit{starfish})}{fig:keystone}
          }

            \item \textbf{Ecosystem engineers}: Species that create or dramatically alter their environment, such as beavers that construct dams.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Disturbance}

    \begin{core}{Disturbance}

      A \textbf{disturbance} is an event such as a storm, fire, flood, drought, or human activity that changes a community by removing organisms or altering resource availability. Disturbances play a critical role in maintaining species diversity by preventing competitive exclusion from one particular species for a prolonged period of time.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Ecological Succession}

      \textbf{Ecological succession} is the gradual change in species composition over time. Ecological succession can be categorized into primary and secondary succession.

      \textbf{Primary succession} begins in \textbf{virtually lifeless areas} where no soil or biological community previously exists, such as newly exposed rock following glacial retreat. It progresses through the following stages.

      \myfig{0.9}{ecology_figures/p_succession}{Primary succession}{fig:p_succession}

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Pioneer Stage}: Colonization by lichens and mosses, which break down rock and accumulate organic matter to initiate soil formation.

        \item \textbf{Dryas Stage}: Low-growing flowering plants establish as thin soil develops.

        \item \textbf{Alder Stage}: Nitrogen-fixing alder shrubs colonize, further enriching the soil.

        \item \textbf{Spruce Stage}: Conifers such as spruce establish and eventually dominate as the climax community.

      \end{enumerate}

      \textbf{Secondary succession} occurs when a \textbf{major disturbance} removes most but not all organisms, allowing recolonization from surviving roots and seeds.

      \myfig{0.9}{ecology_figures/s_succession}{Secondary succession}{fig:s_succession}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Biogeography and Pathogens}

    \begin{core}[label=core:pathogen]{Pathogens}

      \textbf{Pathogens} have profound effects on community structure and can cause population oscillations or local extinctions. \textbf{Zoonotic pathogens} are those transmitted to humans from other animals, either through direct contact or via an intermediate species known as a \textbf{vector}.

\section{Ecosystem Ecology}

  \begin{core}{Ecosystem}

    An \textbf{ecosystem} comprises all organisms living in a given area along with the abiotic factors with which they interact. Biological and geochemical processes continuously cycle nutrients and water through ecosystems.

  \end{core}

  \subsection{Geochemical Cycles}

    \textbf{Geochemical cycles} represent the continuous pathways through which crucial elements---such as water, carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus---circulate between the Earth's biotic and abiotic counterparts. These closed loops constantly recycle finite nutrients to sustain planetary life and regulate global climate systems.

    \begin{core}{The Water Cycle}

      \sbs{0.45}{
        The \textbf{water cycle} describes the continuous movement of water through Earth's ecosystems and atmosphere. Water transitions between different states and reservoirs through several key processes.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item \textbf{Precipitation}: Atmospheric water vapor condenses and falls to Earth's surface as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.

          \item \textbf{Evaporation}: Liquid water transforms into water vapor from surfaces such as oceans, lakes, rivers, and soil.

          \item \textbf{Evapotranspiration}: Water is transferred from land to the atmosphere through both evaporation from soil and other surfaces and transpiration from plant tissues.

          \item \textbf{Runoff \& Percolation}: Water flows over land surfaces (\textit{runoff}) or infiltrates through soil into groundwater reservoirs (\textit{percolation}).

        \end{itemize}
      }{
        \myfig{0.95}{ecology_figures/water_cycle}{Water cycle}{fig:water_cycle}
      }

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{The Carbon Cycle}

      \sbs{0.45}{
        The \textbf{carbon cycle} describes the biogeochemical pathways through which carbon atoms circulate within the atmosphere. Carbon exchanges between organic and inorganic forms through several fundamental processes.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item \textbf{Burning of fossil fuels and wood}: Combustion releases stored carbon into the atmosphere as \ce{CO2}, representing a major artificial flux in the modern carbon cycle.

          \item \textbf{Photosynthesis}: Autotrophic organisms fix atmospheric \ce{CO2} into organic compounds, transferring carbon from the atmosphere into biomass (\textit{\autoref{cyt-core:photosynthesis}}).

          \item \textbf{Cellular Respiration}: Organisms oxidize organic molecules to release energy, returning carbon (\textit{in the form of \ce{CO2}}) to the atmosphere (\textit{\autoref{cyt-core:cell_respiration}}).

          \item \textbf{Decomposition}: Decomposer organisms break down dead organic matter, releasing carbon back into the atmosphere and soil.

        \end{itemize}
      }{
        \myfig{0.95}{ecology_figures/carbon_cycle}{Carbon cycle}{fig:carbon_cycle}
      }
    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{The Nitrogen Cycle}

      The \textbf{nitrogen cycle} describes the sequential transformations of nitrogen through various chemical forms as it circulates among atmospheric, terrestrial, and aquatic reservoirs. Atmospheric nitrogen (\ce{N2}) must be converted into biologically available forms through specialized processes, as most organisms cannot directly utilize gaseous nitrogen despite its atmospheric abundance.
      
      In the general atmosphere, nitrogen exchanges occur through the following processes.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Fixation}: Atmospheric \ce{N2} is converted into ammonia (\ce{NH3}) or ammonium (\ce{NH4+}) by nitrogen-fixing bacteria (\textit{e.g., Rhizobium in legume root nodules}) or through abiotic processes such as lightning.

        \item \textbf{Denitrification}: Anaerobic bacteria reduce nitrate (\ce{NO3-}) back to gaseous nitrogen (\ce{N2}) or nitrous oxide (\ce{N2O}), returning nitrogen to the atmosphere and completing the cycle.

        \item \textbf{Runoff}: Water-soluble nitrogen compounds (\textit{nitrates and ammonium}) are transported from terrestrial ecosystems into aquatic systems through surface water movement, potentially causing \textit{eutrophication}.

      \end{itemize}
      
      Zooming into the terrestrial environment, nitrogen undergoes transformations within soil and organisms.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Decomposition}: Decomposer organisms break down organic nitrogen compounds in dead biomass and waste products into simpler forms.

        \item \textbf{Ammonification}: Decomposers convert organic nitrogen from decomposing matter into ammonia (\ce{NH3}) or ammonium ions (\ce{NH4+}), making nitrogen available for subsequent transformations.

        \item \textbf{Nitrification}: Nitrifying bacteria oxidize ammonium (\ce{NH4+}) first to nitrite (\ce{NO2-}) and then to nitrate (\ce{NO3-}) through a two-step aerobic process.

        \item \textbf{Assimilation}: Plants absorb inorganic nitrogen (\textit{primarily as \ce{NO3-} or \ce{NH4+}}) from soil and incorporate it into organic molecules such as amino acids, nucleotides, and proteins.

      \end{itemize}
      
      \myfig{0.9}{ecology_figures/nitrogen_cycle}{Nitrogen cycle; general atmosphere (\textit{left}) and terrestrial environment (\textit{right})}{fig:nitro_cycle}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{The Phosphorus Cycle}

      \sbs{0.45}{
        The \textbf{phosphorus cycle} tracks the movement of phosphorus through geological/biological systems. Unlike other biogeochemical cycles, the phosphorus cycle \textbf{lacks a significant atmospheric component}.

        \begin{itemize}

          \item \textbf{Geologic Uplift}: Tectonic activity exposes phosphorus-containing rocks, which weather to release phosphate ions (\ce{PO4^3-}).

          \item \textbf{Uptake}: Plants absorb dissolved phosphate from soil and incorporate it into essential biomolecules such as ATP and phospholipids.

          \item \textbf{Decomposition}: Decomposers break down organic matter, converting organic phosphate into inorganic forms available for uptake.

          \item \textbf{Runoff}: Phosphorus is transported from land to aquatic systems through surface water flow, where excess levels can trigger \textit{eutrophication}.

        \end{itemize}
      }{
        \myfig{0.95}{ecology_figures/phos_cycle}{Phosphorus cycle}{fig:phos_cycle}
      }
    \end{core}
