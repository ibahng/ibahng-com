\section{Chapter 52: Ecology Overview}

Ecology is the the scientific study of the interactions between organisms and the environment.

Ecosystems: biotic and abiotic components of an environment
Communties: biotic only
Populations: pertains to a group of individuals within the same species

Climate: the long term prevailing weather conditions in a given area controlled by temperature, precipitation, sunlight, and wind

Regional and Local Effects on Climate

  Seasonality: Earth's tilted axis of rotation and its annual passage around the sun cause strong seasonal cycles in day length, solar radiation, and temperature

  Bodies of Water: Ocean currents influence climate along the coasts of continents by heating or cooling overlying air masses that pass across the land

  Mountains: Like large bodies or water, mountains influence airflow over land.

Ecotone: the area of intergradation between biomes

\section{Chapter 53: Population Ecology}

Population:  a group of individuals of a single species living in the same general area

Density: number of individuals per unit area or volume
Disperson: the patterm of spacing among individuals withint he boundaries of the population
  
  Clumped: sea stars grouped together
  
  Uniform: penguins -> territoriality: the defense of a bounded physical space against encroachment by other individuals

  Random: dandelions

Demography: study of key characteristics of populations and how they change over time
  
  Birth/Immigration leads to an increase in a population count
    
    Life tables: summarizes the survivial and reproductive rates of individuals in a specific agre group within a population
    Survivorship Curve: a plot of the proportion or number in a cohort still alive at each age

  Death/Emigration leads to a decrease in population count

 ===================================================================================================

Exponential growth: population growth in an idealized, unlimited environment -> IMPORTANT EQUATION

Logistic growth: population growth as the population nears its environment's carrying capacity (denoted as K) -> IMPORTANT EQUATION

 ===================================================================================================

Natural selection favors traits that improve an organism’s chances of survival and reproductive success. In every species, there are trade-offs between survival and reproductive traits such as frequency of reproduction, number of offspring (number of seeds produced by plants; litter or clutch size for animals), or investment in parental care. 

Life History: the traits that affect an organism's schedule of reproduction and survival

Semelparity: a "one-shot" pattern of big-bang reproduction

Iteroparity: repeated reproduction

 ===================================================================================================

density dependent population regulation: a birth or death rate does not change with population density
  competition for resources
  disease
  territoriality
  intrinsic factors
  toxic waste
  
density independent population regulation: a death rate that increases with population density or a birth rate that falls with rising density
  usually natural disasters

Population dynamics: population fluctuations from year to year or place to place

Metapopulation: a number of local populations that are linked (human beings is the obvious example)

 ===================================================================================================

Demographic transition: the movement from high birth and death rates toward low birth and death rates

Age structure: the relative number of individuals of each age in a population
  
  rapid growth: pyramid
  slow growth: block
  no growth: inverted pyramid

\section{Chapter 54: Community Ecology}

Community ecology deals with the interactions between differen species of a general area.

Interactions between species can help, harm, or have no effect on the individuals involved

  Competition (-/-): individuals of different species each use a resource that limits the survival and reproduction of both individuals

    Competitive Exclusion: a slight reproductive advantage will eventually lead to local elimination of the inferior competitor

    Ecological niche: a specific set of biotic and abiotic resources that an organism uses in its environment

    Resource partitioning: the differentiation of niches that enables similar species to coexist in a community

    Character displacement: the tendency for characteristics to diverge more in sympatric than in allopatric populations of two species due to resource partitioning

  Exploitation (+/-)

    in these cases, the (-) and (+) species both have developed adaptations to better execute their role

    Predation: one species hunts another
      
      Aposematic coloration: warning coloration

      Cryptic coloration: camouflage

      Batesian mimicry: a palatable or harmless species mimics an unpalatable or harmful species

      Mullerian mimicry: two or more unpalatable species resemble each other

      Mimicry also develops in predators (octopus resembling a snake)

    Herbivory: an herbivore eats plants

      herbs adaptations (cactus spikes, toxins, poisons)

      herbivore adaptations (chemical sensors, better shaped beak)

    Parasitism: a parasite derives nourishment from a host at the expense of the host

      endoparasites: internally feeding parasites (tapeworms)

      ectoparasites: externally feeding parasites (ticks or lice)

  Positive interactions

    Mutualism (+/+): both species benefits

    Commensalism (+/0): one species benefit but the other doesn't benefit nor get harmed

 ===================================================================================================

Species diversity: the variety of different kinds of organisms that make up the community

Species richness: the number of different species in the community

Relative adbundance: the proportion each species represents of all individuals in the community

Biomass: the total mass of all organisms in a habitat

Trophic Structure: the feeding realtionships between organisms

  Food chain: Primary producers -> primary consumer -> secondary consumer -> tertiary consumer -> quaternary consumer (these are TROPHIC LEVELS)

  Food web: a more accurate represenation of a trophic structure of an ecosystem

  Energy hypothesis: the length of a food chain is limited by the inefficiency of energy transter along the chain. On average, only about 10\% of the energy stored in the organic matter of each trophic level is converted to organic matter at the next trophic level

High impact species

  Foundation species: species that have strong effect on their communities as a result of their large size or high abundance (usually trees, desert shrubs, marine algae, etc.)

  Keystone species: non usually abundant in a community but exert a strong control on community structure not by numerical might but by their pivotal ecological roles (sea starsh)

  Ecosystem engineers: species that create or dramatically alter their environment (beavers)

Controls

  bottom-up controls: nutrient supply or the availability of food at lower trophic levels limits the abundance of organisms at higher trophic levels

  top-down controls: the abundance of organisms at higher trophic levels controls the abundance of organisms at lower trophic levels

 ===================================================================================================

Disturbance: an event, such as a storm, fire, flood, drought, or human activity, that changes a community by removing organisms from it or altering resource availability.

Intermediate disturbance hypothesis: moderate levels of disturbance foster greater species diversity than do high or low levels of disturbance.  
  
  too high levels create too much environmental stress

  too low levels allow competitively cominant species to exclude others

Ecological succession: The disturbed area may be colonized by a variety of species, which are gradually replaced by other species, which are in turn replaced by still other species

Primary succession: ecological succession that begins in a virtually lifeless area

  (1) pioneer stage

  (2) dryas stage

  (3) alder stage

  (4) spruce stage

Secondary succession: recolonization of an area after a major disturbance has removed most but not all of the organisms in a community

 ===================================================================================================

Biogeographic factors affect community diversity

  Latitudinal gradients: differences in the environment due to the latitude it locates on
    
    evapotransiration: the evaporation of water from soil and plants

  Area effects
    
    species-area curve: all factors beign equal, the larger the geographic area of a community, the more species it has

  Island Equilibrium model: how the number of species on an island stays relatively constant over time through a balance of two opposing forces: immigration and extinction.

 ===================================================================================================

Pathogens also have profound effects on a community

  zoonotic pathogens: those that are transferred to humans from other animals, either through direct contact with an infected animal or via an intermediate species called a VECTOR
