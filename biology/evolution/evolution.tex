\section{Chapter 22}

  Evolution: descent with modification

  Darwin
    The voyage of the beagle: finch bird differences across galapagos island

  Adaptations: inherited characteristics of organisms that enhance their survival and reproduction in specific environments

  Natural Selection: A process in which individuals that have certain inherited traits tend to survive and reproduce at higher rates than do other individuals because of those traits

  Artificial Selection: the selective breeding of individuals that possess desired traits (vegetation, dogs)

  Darwins observations and inferences
    observation 1: members of a population often vary in their inherited traits
    observation 2: all species can produce more offspring than their environment can support, and many of these offspring fail to survice and reproduce
    inference 1: Individuals whose inherited traits give them a higher probability of surviving and reproducing in a given environment tend to leave more offspring than do other individuals.
    inference 2: This unequal ability of individuals to sur vive and reproduce will lead to the accumulation of favor able traits in the population over generations.

  Natural selection main ideas
    - Natural selection is a process in which individuals that have certain heritable traits survive and reproduce at a higher rate than do other individuals because of those traits.
    - Over time, natural selection can increase the frequency of adaptations that are favorable in a given environment
    - If an environment changes, or if individuals move to a new environment, natural selection may result in adaptation to these new conditions, sometimes giving rise to new species.

  *INDIVIDUALS DO NOT EVOLVE

  Evolution is supported by an overwhelming amount of scientific evidence

    (1) Direct observations of evolutionary change
          such as the evolution of drug resistant bacteria

    (2) Homology: similarity resulting from common ancestry

          homologous structures: underlying characteristics that represent variations of a theme present in a common ancestor
            such as the underlying skeletons of arms, forelegs, flippers, and wings
            similarities between embryo shapes across species

          vestigal structures: reminants of features that served a function in the organisms ancestors
            snakes retain vestiges of the pelvis and leg bones of their ancestors

          evolutionary tree: a diagram that reflects evolutionary relationships among groups of organisms

          convergent evolution: the independent evolution of similar features in different lineages (these are said to be ANALOGOUS STRUCTURES)
            bat wings, bird wings, dragonfly wings

    (3) The fossil record: shows evidence of evolution over long periods of time

    (4) Biogeography: scientific study of the geographic distributions of species
          Islands generally have plant and animal species that are found nowhere else in the world (endemic)

\section{Chatper 23}

  Genetic variation: refers to genetic differences among individuals within a population
    formed thorugh new alleles formation, altering gene number or position, sexual reproduction, rapid reproduction, mutations (most are harmful, but a few beneficial)

  Population: a group of individuals of the same species that live in the same area and interbreed, producing FERTILE offspringA

  Gene pool: a population's genetic make up

  Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium: a state in which a population is not evolving, allele and genotype frequencies remain constant from generation to generation, provided that only Mendelian segregation and recombination of alleles are at work
    dominant allele frequency = p
    recessive allele frequency = q
    p + q = 1 (dominant:recessive)
    p2 + 2pq +q2 = 1 (geneotype ratio)

  Natural selection results in alleles being passed to the next generation in proportions that differe from the present generation

  Adaptive evolution: traits that enhance survival or reproduction tend to increase in frequency over time

  Genetic Drift: chance events can also cause allele frequencies to fluctuate unpredictably from one generation to the next

    Founder effect: When a few individuals become isolated from a larger population, this smaller group may establish a new population whose gene pool differs from the source population
      when a few members of a population are blown by a storm to a new island

    Bottleneck effect: A sudden change in environment, such as a fire or flood may drastically reduce the size of a population

    THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN THE TWO IS THAT FOUNDER IS ABOUT MIGRATION, THE BOTTLENECK IS ABOUT ENVIRONMENTAL DISASTER

    Effects of genetic drift
      (1) genetic drift is significant in small populations
      (2) genetic drift can cause allele frequencies to change at random
      (3) genetic drift can lead to a loss of genetic variation within populations
      (4) genetic drift can cause harmful alleles to become fixed

  Gene Flow: the transfer of alleles into or out of a population due to the movement of fertile individuals or their gametes (this INCREASES genetic variation in the receiving population)
    this is different from the founder effect, which is referring to a loss of genetic variation due to isolation

  Natural Selection is the only mechanism that consistently causes adaptive evolution

    Relative fitness: the contribution an individual makes to the gene pool of the next generation relative to the contribution of other individuals

    Selection types
      
      Directional Selection: conditions favor individuals exhibiting one extreme of a phenotypic range (bell curve shifts to the right or left)

      Disruptive Selection: conditions favor individuals at both extremes of a phenotypic range over individuals with intermediate phenotypes (bell curve splits into two)

      Stabilizing selection: acts against both extreme phenotypes and favors intermediate variants (thinning of the bell curve)

  Sexual Selection: a process in which individuals with certain inherited characteristics are more likely than other individuals of the same sex to obtain mates

    Sexual dimorphism: a difference in secondary sexual characteristics between males and females of the same species

    Intrasexual selection: individuals of one sex compete directly for mates of the opposite sex
      the male lion preventing other male lions from mating with his group of females

    Intersexual selection: one sex (usually the females) is choosy in selecting their mates from the other sex
      male peacocks having the prettiest patterns on their feathers (showiness of the male's appearance or behavior)

  Balancing Selection: selection that preserves variation at some loci
    
    Frequency-Dependent Selection: the fitness of a phenotype depends on how common it is in the population
      left and right mouthed scale eating fish maintain a 50:50 genetic varaition of each phenotype because more concentration on one side leads to more competition compared to the other side

    Heterozygote Advantage: Individuals who are heterozygous at a particular locus have greater fitness than do both kinds of homozygotes
      for a gene regarding blood cells in humans
        homo dom: high risk of severe illness or death from Malaria
        homo ress: sickle cell anemia
        hetero: dont suffer sickle cell anemia, presence of sickle cells makes it more difficult for malaria to develop

  Limitations of Natural Selection (cannot fashion perfect organisms)
    (1) Selection can act only on existing variations
    (2) Evolution is limited by historical constraints
    (3) Adaptations are often comprimises (red neck sac of bird species as good for sexual selection but more visible to predators)
    (4) Chance, natural selection, and the environment interact

\section{Chapter 24}

  Speciation: the process by which one species splits into two species

  Specifation forms a conceptual bridge between MICROEVOLUTION, change over time in allele frequencies in a population, and MACROEVOLUTION, the broad pattern of evolution above the species level.

  Biological species concept (primary definition of a species): a group of populations whose members have the potential to interbreed in nature and produce viable fertile offspring---but do not produce viable, fertile offspring with members of other such groups.

  Reproductive Barriers

    Prevents from mating via the environment (PREZYGOTIC BARRIER)
      
      Habitat isolation: occupies different habitats

      Temporal isolation: breeding during different times of the day, different seasons, or different years

      Behavioral isolation: courtship rituals that attract mates and other behaviors unique to a species are effective reproductive barriers

    After mating attempt (PREZYGOTIC BARRIER)

      Mechanical isolation: morphological difference prevent successful mating

      Gametic isolation: sperm of one species may not be able to fertilize the eggs of another species due to surivivability issues, biochemical mechanisms, etc.

    Post mating (POSTZYGOTIC BARRIER)
      
      Reduced hybrid viability: genes of parents impair the hybrid's development or survival

      Reduced hybrid fertility: hybrids are infertile or sterile

      Hybrid breakdown: first generation hybrids are good but the offspring of the next generation are feeble or sterile

  Other definitions of species

    Morphological SC: distinguishes a species by body shape and other structural features (disadvantage may be that this relies on subjective criteria)

    Ecological SC: defines a species in terms of its ecological niche, the sum of how members of the species interact with the nonliving and living parts of their environments

  Allopatric speciation: gene flow is interrupted when a population is divided into geographically isolated subpopulations

    fish orignally part of the same species where geographically separated into two ponds (one containing predators and one not). the one one containing predators developed bodies that favored short bursts of speed while the other pond favored ones that allow swimming for a while
    Once manually put in the same environment, they have developed behavior differences as well, solidifying a reproductive barrier

  Sympatric speciation: speciation occurs in populations that live in the same geographic area
    
    new species may originate from an accident during cell division like polyploidy (extra sets pf chromosomes)

    sexual selection may result in a large binary in orignally same species populations

    switch in habitat or food source not used by parent population
 
   =================================================================================================

  Hybrid Zones: a region in which members of different species meet and mate, producing at least some offspring of mixed ancestry

  Hybrid zones over time

    Reinforcement: Hybrids often are less fit than members of their parent species. In such cases, natural selection should strengthen prezygotic barriers to reproduction, reducing the formation of unfit hybrids.

    Fusion: Barriers to reproduction may be weak when two species meet in a hybrid zone. Indeed, so much gene flow may occur that reproductive barriers weaken further and the gene pools of the two species become increasingly alike. In effect, the speciation process reverses, eventually causing the two hybridizing species to fuse into a single species.

    Stability: continued formation of hybrid individuals

   =================================================================================================

  Speciation can occur very rapidly or slowly 

  Punctuated model: new species change most as they branch from a parent species and then change little for the rest of their existance

  Gradual model: species diver from one another more slowly and steadily over time
