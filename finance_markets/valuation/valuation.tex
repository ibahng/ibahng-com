\section{Valuation Overview}

  \begin{core}{Defining Valuation}

    A valuation does not give a pinpoint price, it gives a range of possible values for a company
    A football field graph of valuation ranges using different models and time periods can summarize valuations results very well
    It is how you’d normally present the data to investors, potential buyers, and the company itself

    A valuation is used to vice the company an idea of what it’s worth, or to see whether or not an acquisition offer is reasonable

    Look at BIWS page 15 in Valuations for the chart example

    Valuation is a combination of info from the numbers and narrative people

  \end{core}

  \subsection{Valuation Types}

    Fundamentally, there are only two ways to value a company: \textbf{relative valuation} and \textbf{intrinsic valuation}.

    \begin{core}{Relative Valuation}

      Relative Valuation
        you mostly look at other public companies (Comparable Public Companies, AKA Public Comps) and recent M&A deals (Precedent Transactions) to estimate company worth

      when to use this method
      Relative Valuation - Public Comps & Precedent Transactions
        Almost always used because they are universally applicable
        You will use different metrics and valuation multiples depending on the industry
        Works best when there’s a lot of good market data and there are truly similar companies

      Generally, Precedent Transactions will almost always have higher valuations than Public Comps in the same industry due to the nature of premiums when buying out 100% of a company

      How Do You Pick Companies and Transactions?
      Public Comps
        Geography (US? China? Europe?)
        Industry (Diversified Consumer? Food and Beverages?)
        Financial (Revenue of EBITDA above, below, or between certain numbers)
        You may tweak these assumptions and cut or add companies as you continue with the analysis; senior bankers often have their own idea of what should be in there and you’ll have to change your assumptions to support their ideas
      Precedent Transactions
        Geography
        Industry
        Financial
        Time (Transactions Since… or Transactions Between Year X/Y)
        Time is very important for M&A deals because markets change over time; multiples are only meaningful when you limit the transactions to a specific time period

      Pricing: The value of an asset is estimated based upon what investors are paying for similar assets. In general, this takes the form of value or price multiples and comparing firms within the same business

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Intrinsic Valuation}

      Intrinsic Valuation
        most methods come down to estimating future cash flows and discounting them back to their present value (DCF Model)
        Valuing the firm’s Assets and assuming that the firm’s total value is linked to its adjusted Asset value minus its Liabilities in some way (Net Asset Value)

      when to use this method
      Intrinsic Valuation - DCF Analysis
        Used in most “standard” industries (consumer/retail, technology, healthcare, industrials)
        In some industries, DCF is irrelevant because: [1] “Free Cash Flow” is not a meaningful metric; or [2] The industry is asset-centric and so you’re better off valuing the company’s Assets and Liabilities
        Works well for stable, mature companies w/ predictable growth rates and profit margins

      DCF can usually produce higher valuations because of its dependence on assumptions compared to relative valuations, however you could make extremely conservative assumptions as well

      What About Other Methodologies
        Asset-Based Valuations (alternatives to DCF)
        Liquidation Valuation: Determine the true Asset and Liability values, repay all liabilities w/ Assets and whatever remains is the company’s Equity Value
        Also called the “Net Asset Value” model
        There are also some industry-specific variations to this model as well
        Public Comps and Precedent Transactions variants
        M&A Premiums Analysis: Precedent transactions but instead of calculating valuation multiples, you calculate the premium that the buyer paid for the seller in each case
        Future Share Price Analysis: Projecting a company’s future share price based on the P/E (or other) multiple of public comps, and then discount to present value
        Sum of Parts: Split conglomerates into individual segments and pick different sets of Public Comps and Precedent Transactions for each and add up at the end

      Leveraged Buyout (LBO) Analysis—assume a PE firm acquires a company and needs to achieve a certain Internal Rate of Return and work backwards to calculate how much they could potentially pay to achieve that return
      Variation of DCF model b/c it involves future cash flow valuations

      Intrinsic valuation: The value of an asset is a function of its fundamentals—cash flows, growth and risk. In general, DCF models are used to estimate intrinsic value

      Intrinsic Valuation 101
      The value of a risky asset can be estimated by discounting the expected cash flows on the asset over its life at a risk-adjusted discount rate

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Contingent Claim Valuation}

      Contingent claim valuation: When the cash flows on an asset are contingent on an external event, the value can be estimated using option pricing models

    \end{core}

    There is no “best” methodology and no “correct” number. Valuation models are for the purpose of determining a valuation range and not a precise number + TRANSITION to next section

    \begin{core}{Trade-Offs}

      Advantages and Disadvantages of Different Methodologies
        Public Comps
          Pros: Based on real data as opposed to future assumptions
          Cons: May be no true comparables; less accurate for thinly traded stocks or volatile companies
        Precedent Transactions
          Pros: Based on what really companies have actually paid
          Cons: Data may be spotty; may be no truly comparable transactions
        Discounted Cash Flow Analysis
          Pros: Not as subject to market fluctuations; theoretically sound
          Cons: Subject to far-in-the-future assumptions; less useful for fast-growing, unpredictable companies

      Other Methodologies
        Liquidation Valuation:
          Pros: Ignores market “noise” and determines value based on Assets and Liabilities
          Cons: Not useful for very healthy companies b/c it tends to produce extremely low values
        M&A Premiums Analysis
          Pros: Same as Precedent Transactions
          Cons: ‘’; only application to public companies w/ stock price
        Future Stock Price Analysis
          Pros: Predicts future company worth in 1-2 years
          Cons: Dependent on assumptions
        Sum of Parts
          Pros: More accurately values diversified conglomerates
          Cons: Often lacks data for each division
        Leveraged Buyout (LBO) Analysis
          Pros: It sets a “floor” on valuation by determining maximum amount a PE firm could pay to achieve its returns
          Cons: Gives a relatively low/“floor” number rather than a wide range of values

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Correlations}

      Comparing Expected Values from Different Methodologies
      Precedent Transactions vs Public Comps
        Transactions tend to be higher due to the control premium
      DCF
        Tends to be the most variable because of its dependence on future assumptions
      Liquidation Valuation
        99\% of the time, it will produce the lowest value because a company is usually worth much more than its book value
      Sum of Parts
        Tend to produce higher values than relative valuation methodologies if a company is worth more in “parts”
      LBO Analysis
        Tends to produce the lowest valuations

      There are very few hard-and-fast rules, and almost all of these guidelines have exceptions or do not hold up in many cases

      Generally, there’s a correlation between growth rates and relevant multiples, and sometimes also between margins and multiples
      All else being equal, a company with higher revenue growth will also have higher revenue multiples than companies not growing as quickly
      You see this behavior for other multiples as well. To account for this, you well sometimes calculate PEG (PE Divided by EPS Growth) multiples to get a better view

    \end{core}
 
  \subsection{Metrics and Reality}

    \begin{core}{Core Metrics}

      Metrics
        P/E: Price per share / EPS or MKT CAP / Net Income
        EBIT: Earnings before Interest and Taxes; This is a company’s operating income
        EBITDA: EBIT + Depreciation + Amortization; The idea is to remove most of the non-cash charges and make it more accurately reflect cash flow potential; may add back other non-cash charges like Stock-Based Compensation
        Unlevered Free Cash Flow (FCF to Firm)
        EBIT * (1-Tax Rate) + Non-Cash Charges - Change in Operating Assets and Liabilities - CapEx
        Levered Free Cash Flow (FCF to Equity)
        Net Income + Non-Cash Charges - Change in Operating Assets and Liabilities - CapEx - Mandatory Debt Repayments

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Profitability Multiples}

      Metric and multiple comparisons are very dependent on the industry you’re analyzing and what stage of growth the company is in
      Often use two or more Profitability Multiples

      Profitability Multiples
        Enterprise Value/EBIT
          Useful for when CapEx is a more important factor since D and A follows closely
          Company value relative to operating income
        Enterprise Value/EBITDA
          Useful for when CapEx and D&A are not as important
          Company value relative to operational cash flow
        P/E
          Most relevant for banks and financial institutions; distorted by non-cash charges, capital structure, and tax rates
          Company value relative to after tax earnings
        Equity Value/Levered FCF
          Most accurate measure of a company’s true “cash flow” and how valuable it is relative to that
        Enterprise Value/Unlevered FCF
          Used when CapEx or changes in Operational Assets and Liabilities such as Deferred Revenue have a big impact
          Capital structure neutral—so better for comparing different companies

      EV/EBITDA and EV/EBIT are by far the most common in finance because of convenience and comparability

      P/E is probably the “least accurate” multiple because it includes non-cash charges and is impacted by tax rates and capital structures

      The FCF multiples are “more accurate” but they take more time and may not be standardized b/c companies include very different items in the CFS

      Book Value Multiples (Equity Value/Book Value, or Price per Share/Book Value per Share) tell company value relative to its Balance Sheet
        Book value is essentially Shareholders’ Equity(with slight adjustments sometimes)
        P/BV multiples become less relevant over time because most industries, company Equity Values vastly differ because companies have become more and more service-oriented and intellectual property-oriented over time

      Depending on the industry you’re in, there may be specific multiples
        Ex. Retail, Restaurant: EV/EBITDAR(R stands for “Rent” - you add back the rental expenses) is often used for comparability purposes
        No need to memorize, just be aware of these other multiples’ existence

      What should you do if a company has no profit and/or no revenue?
        In industries like Consumer Internet you can alternate metrics and multiples, such as EV/Unique Visitors or EV/Registered Users, or even more creative ones
        Sometimes for Biotech and Pharma, you will actually create a far-in-the-future, multi-stage DCF—potential profits from a drug with a known market size are easier to estimate

      Once you’ve calculated all the relevant multiple, you normally find the minimum, 25th percentile, median, 75th percentile, and maximum each year of public comps and then apply them to the company’s own financial figures
      Normally, you’d create a graph based on the full range of values, and you may weight certain multiples more than others

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Valuation in the Real World}

      The entire purpose of valuation is to give a client an idea of what it’s worth, to justify an acquisition offer or price, and also value for internal purposes

      Always present in football field graphs and focus on ranges rather than specific numbers

      Premiums or discounts can come for many reasons
        market position
        competitive advantages not reflected in financial statements
        recent press releases

      * You can pick certain multiples and ranges or focus on them for many reasons—for example, if a company is truly outperforming its peers, maybe you’ll focus on the 75th percentile in a set of comps when displaying the multiples and the “Football Field” graph

      Accounting choices and oddities can also greatly affect valuation—for example, owning vs leasing buildings makes a big impact on EBITDA
        Owning: Depreciation and Interest are neither included
        Leasing: Rental expenses show up on the IS

      Two other very important points
        Plenty of other factors besides revenue/EPS/EBITDA growth rates impact the multiples… and there are many exceptions
          Ex. lawsuit rulings, intellectual property, talented employees
        Basic math can also impact the multiples,especially when companies have very different margins
          Ex. A greater margin increases raw EBITDA, lowering the EV/EBITDA value and resulting a lower valuation multiple than a company w/ similar EV and lower margins

      To recap briefly, EV/Revenue and P/E multiples, while easy to calculate, are taken the least seriously because
        A company should be valued based on its profits, not its sales… earning revenue is easy, keeping it is hard
        P/E is subject to non-cash and non-recurring charges, significantly different tax rates, the company’s capital structure, and a host of other problems

    \end{core}

\section{Equity \& Enterprise Value}

  \subsection{Equity Value}

    \begin{core}{Equity Value}

      Equity Value is essentially just Market Capitalization—answering the simple question of “How much is this company worth?
      When you read about “a \$10 billion company” in the news, usually the “\$10 billion” refers to the Equity Value of the company

      Where is nothing complicated about determining a company’s share price; calculating the total number of shares can require more work because there is Basic Equity Value and Diluted Equity Value (which we’ll get into in Key Rule #3)

      Equity Valuation
      The value of equity is the sum of the discounted FCFE (FCFE starts with net income, not NOPAT) forever
      The dividend discount model is a specialized case of equity valuation, and the value of a stock is the present value of expected future dividends (has limitations because it undervalued low dividend, cash retaining firms)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Diluted Shares Outstanding}

      Calculating the exact number of shares a company has can be tricky due to dilutive securities
      A security is “dilutive” if it could potentially create more shares
      Ex. Call Options
      The potential for additional shares is always there… and that’s what we care about when calculating the diluted shares for a company

      Calculating the Impact of Diluted Shares (Treasury Stock Method)
      You assume that the new shares get created when options are exercised, and that the company buys back some of those new shares with the funds it receives
      Ex. Firm spot price = \$10.00; 100 options at \$5.00 strike price
      Firm receives \$500 and gives 100 shares
      Firm buys back 50 shares with \$500
              Result is that there are 50 additional shares outstanding and the diluted share count goes up by 50
      With the TSM, you assume all in-the-money options contribute to dilution

      Options are the most common dilutive securities, but there are others
      Warrants
      Uses TSM in its diluted calculation
      Convertible Bonds
      Either counted as 100% debt when out-the-money or 100% additional shares when in-the-money; no TSM
      Convertible Preferred Stock
      Same calculation treatment as Convertible Bonds
      Restricted Stock Units
      This is just straight addition
      Performance Shares
      If the stock price is above a certain level, they count as additional shares

      Warrants are issued by the underlying company, are less short-term, and have less liquidity in comparison to call options
      Restricted Stock Units (RSUs) are a form of equity-based compensation that companies use to attract, retain, and incentivize employees. RSUs represent a promise by the company to grant the employee a specific number of shares of the company’s stock at a future date, subject to certain conditions and restrictions
      Performance Shares are also a form of equity-based compensation usually granted to executives because they are tied to specific performance metrics or goals

      We calculate the Diluted Equity Value of a company to see what it would really cost to acquire a company

      When you buy another company, the contract normally states that any in-the-money dilutive securities get cashed out or get converted into an equivalent number of the buyer’s securities. So it’s more accurate to calculate Diluted Equity Value when you’re determining the Enterprise Value—otherwise you’re underestimating how much the company would truly cost to acquire

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Enterprise Value}

    \begin{core}{Enterprise Value}

      If you compare buying a company to buying a house, Equity Value is like the “sticker price”
      However there are additional items that might push up the effective price afterward, or possible push down the effective price
      The company might have Debt that needs to be repaid upon acquisition
      The company might have Excess Cash that we can claim for ourselves
      The company might have Unfunded Pension Obligations and other Liabilities that we’ll have to repay at some point

      Enterprise Value
      = [Equity Value] +
      [Debt, Debt-Like Items and Other Obligations] +
      [Cash, Cash-Like Items, and Anything That Saves Us Money]

      More Precise Definition = [Equity Value] + [Debt] + [Preferred Stock] + [Noncontrolling Interests] - [Cash & Cash Equivalents]

      Essentially we add anything that we’re going to have to set aside funds to pay off in the future, and subtract anything that can save us money in the future

      Firm Valuation
      The value of the firm is the sum of the discounted FCFF forever
      This makes the cash flows are after meeting all operating expenses as well as tax on EBIT, but prior to debt payments
      The discount rate is WACC

      When we calculate FCFF, we start from operating income (which does not take into consideration interest from debt). However, wouldn’t this mean that the tax benefits of debt are not captured in the cash flows. Yes, but it is instead captured in the WACC where the cost of debt is calculated WITH the tax benefit as well. 
      If you include interest expenses in the FCFF, then you would effectively be double counting the tax benefits from debt

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Enterprise Value Deductions}

      When do you subtract an item?
        Normally when it saves you money or potentially gives you extra cash, either immediately or in the long-term

      Few examples of items to subtract
      Cash
      (Potentially) Short-Term, Long-Term, and Equity Investments
      Sometimes these are subtracted—depending on how liquid they are
      Net Operating Losses
      These could potentially save you cash as future tax deductions, so sometimes they are factored in (standards vary widely)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Enterprise Value Additions}

      When do you add an item?
      When it is something that must be paid immediately (Debt)
      When it is something that must be repaid in the future (Unfunded Pension Obligations)

      Unfunded pension obligations, also referred to as an unfunded pension liability, represent the portion of a company’s pension plan obligations that exceed the plan’s assets. In other words, it’s the deficit between the amount of money needed to cover future pension benefits promised to employees and the assets available in the pension fund to meet those obligations

      When you’re adding it back for comparability purposes (Noncontrolling Interests)

      Noncontrolling interests (NCI), also known as minority interests, refer to the ownership stake in a company that is held by external parties or investors who do not have control over the company’s operations. Noncontrolling interests represent a portion of a subsidiary’s equity that is not owned by the parent company.

      Few examples of items to add
      Debt
      Preferred Stock
      It’s similar to Debt because of the required dividends, which act as interest expense; also, normally it must also be repaid upon acquisition

      Examples of items to add but wouldn’t necessarily come from the company’s normal cash flows
      Unfunded Pension Obligations
      Capital Leases
      Restructuring/Environmental Liabilities
      All three points above have the same scenarios of having to be paid through debt because regular firm cash flows aren’t nearly enough to pay for them

      You also add Noncontrolling Interests (AKA Minority Interests) for comparability purposes
      When you own over 50\% of another company, you consolidate 100\% of its financial statements with your own. But Equity Value only reflects the value of the percentage that you own, not 100\%. So you need to reflect 100\% of that other company in Enterprise Value—if you did not add Noncontrolling Interests, you would be reflecting 60\%, or 70\%, or however much you own.

      Comparability purposes refers to the comparisons the acquirer makes with other potential acquisition targets and the current firm under inspection.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Enterprise vs. Equity Valuation Multiples}

      Equity Value is like the “sticker price” and Enterprise Value is how much the company would actually cost to acquire

      There usage depends on what’s in the denominator when calculating valuation multiples

      If the denominator included (uses) interest income and expense, you use Equity Value; if it does not include interest income and expense, you use Enterprise Value

      Common Valuation Multiples
      Enterprise Value / Revenue
      Enterprise Value / EBITDA
      Enterprise Value / EBIT
      Equity Value / Net Income (P/E)
      Enterprise Value / FCFF
      Unlevered Free Cash Flow (Free Cash Flow to Firm)
      Equity Value / FCFE
      Levered Free Cash Flow (Free Cash Flow to Equity)

      When in doubt, think: “Does this include interest income and expense, i.e. do we subtract interest expense and add interest income to get to this metric?”
      If it does, use Equity Value; if it does not, use Enterprise Value

    \end{core}

\section{Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)}

  \begin{core}{Discounted Cash Flow Overview}

    The basic concept of DCF analysis is that a company is worth the present value of its future cash flows
    Cash today is worth more than cash tomorrow, thus any future cash flows must be discounted to represent the time value of money

    DCF is divided into two periods before discounted
    Projection Period: Cash flows over the next 5-10 years, which is fairly predictable with mature, consistent firms
    Terminal Value: Approximation of firm value in the distant future

    DCF Analysis Steps
    Project a company’s FCF over a 5-10 year period
    Calculate the company’s Discount Rate, usually using WACC (Weighted Average Cost of Capital)
    Discount and sum up the company’s FCF
    Calculate the company’s Terminal Value
    Discount the Terminal Value to its Present Value
    Add the discounted FCF to the discounted Terminal Value

  \end{core}

  \subsection{Free Cash Flow}

    \begin{core}{Revenue \& Growth}

      3 - Expected Growth
      Value is driven by expected future growth, not past growth, and generically there are three approaches to estimating growth:
      past performance
      outsourcing analysts and management
      Fundamentals: by looking at reinvestment
      For a company with stable margins and returns on equity/capital, the expected growth becomes a product of how much the firm reinvests (as a percent of earnings) and how well it reinvests (a return on equity or capital)

      1 - Revenue Growth
      To gauge revenue growth and magnitude, we have to observe the current market size and growth as well as the firm’s share in that market.
      The worst condition is when a mature, large-market-share owning firm is operating in a mature, slow-growing market. There is simply not much room left to grow on both dimensions.
      A smaller market share owning firm in a quickly advancing market is the best, because it has both dimensions left to growth

      Market Size and Growth
      Market Share
      1. Current Market Size: The size of the market for the company’s products & services, given geography it is targeting and product type

      2. Expected Growth in Market: Growth in total market, as technology and market conditions change
      1. Company’s current market share: If company’s current market share is low, potential for growth in market share at expense of competition

      2. Industry Economics: Nature of the business (a few big winners or splintered competition).

      3. Strength of company’s competitive advantages: Stronger and more sustainable competitive advantages should allow for higher market share.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Margins}

      2 - Target Margins (and path there)...

      Target Operating Margin
      Pathway to Profitability
      1. Unit Economics: Profits on extra unit sold (GM), as percent of price, with higher profitability going with higher operating margin

      2. Economies of scale: Costs of growth relative to revenue growth, with greater economies of scale allowing for higher margins

      3. Competition: Pricing behavior among competitors, with more aggressive pricing leading to lower margins
      1. Company’s current operating margin: The lower a company’s current margin, relative to the target, the steeper the path to profitability

      2. Profitability vs Growth trade off: Companies that put growth ahead of profitability will wait longer before getting a target margin

      3. Business model: The more well formed a business model, the speedier the pathway to the target margin

      While all companies would like higher margins in steady state, the level of these margins will be determined by the sector in which a firm operates and its choice of business model, and the speed with which you move towards those target margins will be determined by a company’s ambitions and business model choices 

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Reinvestment}

      REINVESTMENT is a critical ingredient to estimating the growth of the company and we can calculate the reinvestment rate by

      Reinvestment Rate=(Net Capex+Change in WC)/NOPAT

      Another factor you can add into a cash flow model is also the reinvestment rate as well

      Reinvestment and Return: Measures
      Earnings Measure
      Reinvestment Measure
      Return Measure
      EPS
      Retention Ratio = % of net income retained by the company = 1- Payout Ratio
      Return on Equity = Net Income / Book Value of Equity
      Net Income from non-cash assets
      Equity reinvestment Rate = (Net Capex + Change in non-cash WC - Change in Debt) / (Net Income)
      Non-cash ROE = Net Income from non-cash assets / (Book value of equity - Cash)
      Operating Income
      Reinvestment Rate = (Net Capex + Change in non-cash WC - Change in Debt) / (NOPAT)
      Return on Capital or ROIC = NOPAT / (Book value of equity + Book value of debt - Cash)

      However, remember to base these figures / results on reality (the ROE may be unrealistically)

      When everything is in flux: Changing growth and margins
      The elegant connection between reinvestment and earnings growth, captured by a sustainable growth rate, when you have a company in transition, and margins are changing over time
      If that is the case, you have to estimate cash flows in three steps:
      Forecast revenue growth and revenues in future years, taking into account market potential and competition.
      Forecast a “target” margin in the future and a pathway from current margins to the target
      Estimate reinvestment from revenues, using a sales to capital ratio (measuring the dollars of revenues you get from each dollar of investment)

      3 - Sales to Invested Capital: A Pathway to estimating Reinvestment
      Current (Historical) Sales to Capital
      Future Sales to Capital
      The sales to IC ratio relates the revenues of the firm to its invested capital, with the latter defined the same way that you would in the return on invested capital calculation

      Sales to Capital
      = Revenues / (Book Equity + Book Debt - Cash)

      The ratio measures the efficiency with which a firm delivers its revenue growth, with higher values indicating more efficiency. You can look at:
      The company’s historical sales to capital ratio
      The industry average sales to capital ratio
      1. Scaling Effects: As companies get bigger, the sales to IC ratio can rise or fall, depending on the sector being analyzed. (Looking at the peer group may give some guidance)

      2. Excess Capacity: If a company has excess capacity, created by past investments, it should be able to generate revenues growth with less investment, i.e., with higher sales to capital ratios

      3. Lag between investment and growth: If reinvestment creates growth quickly (or instantaneously), the reinvestment in a year can be estimated based upon revenue change in that year. If there is a lag, the reinvestment may have to be tied to revenue change in a future year.


    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Free Cash Flow}

      Free Cash Flow Definition
      After tax cash flow the firm generates on a recurring basis, after taking into account
      Account non-cash changes
      Changes in Operating Assets and Liabilities
      Required CapEx
      FCF closely corresponds to the actual cash flow an investor would receive each year if he/she buys the entire company

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Calculating Unlevered and Levered FCF}

      Types of FCF
      Unlevered FCF (Free Cash Flow to Firm); used more, easier to calculate, excludes net interest expense and mandatory debt repayments
      Levered FCF (Free Cash Flow to Equity); includes net interest expense and mandatory debt repayments

      Calculating Unlevered FCF
      Project the company’s revenue growth (\%) for the 5-10 year “near-future” period
      Assume an operating margin to calculate EBIT. Usually this is based on historical margins
      Calculate the company’s effective tax rate and its Net Operating Profit After Tax, or NOPAT
      Ex. Tax Rate = 40\% ; NOPAT = EBIT * (1-40\%)
      Next, Project the 3 key cash flow items
      Non-cash changes
      Changes in Operating Assets and Liabilities
      Capital Expenditures
      The main Non-Cash Charges are Depreciation \& Amortization; you may also project others, such as Stock-Based Compensation. You can make these additions percentages of the total revenue
      If assets increase more than liabilities, change in Operating Assets and Liabilities is negative. Liabilities more than assets—positive. Assets eat up money; Liabilities produce cash flow. Make this as a percentage of revenue.
      Estimate CapEx, which always reduces cash flow. Also a percentage of revenue

      Through this, we replicate the CFS, but exclude interest, debt repayments, and everything in Cash Flow from Financing. We’re also eliminating everything under CFI except for CapEx because that is the only usually recurring item.

      Operating Assets \& Liability (Review)
      Accounts Receivable
      Prepaid Expenses
      Inventory
      Accounts Payable
      Accrued Expenses
      Deferred Revenue

      Unlevered vs Levered FCF
      Main difference is that you add net interest income / (expense) after the EBIT calculation, thus more like net income rather than NOPAT in step 3
      Then you must subtract mandatory debt repayments after CapEx subtraction, which is hard to find
      There are alternate definitions, but do not mind those

  \subsection{Discount Rates}

    \begin{core}{Discount Rates}

      You need to discount both the company’s future FCF and it sTerminal Value because of the time value of money
      What return are investors expecting to earn, at the minimum, when they invest in this company?
      The Discount Rate also represents the “risk” of a company, because higher potential returns correspond to higher risk

      All else being equal, smaller companies and companies in emerging markets have higher Discount Rates because their risk is inherently higher.

      2 - Discount Rates
      Critical ingredient in DCF valuation. Errors in estimating the discount rate or mismatching cash flows and discount rates can lead to serious errors in valuation
      At an intuitive level, the discount rate used should be consistent with both the riskiness and the type of cash flow being discounted
      The cost of equity is the rate at which we discount cash flows to equity (dividends or free cash flows to equity). The cost of capital is the rate at which we discount free cash flows to the firm

      We have to be cognizant that the cost of equity and capital can and should change over time. Especially in the case of an initially small cap company growing, the cost of capital should become lower over time because of economies of scale

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Weighted Average Cost of Capital}

      The Discount Rate has three components
      Cost of Equity
      Cost of Debt → Interest Rate
      Cost of Preferred Stock → The Effective Yield
      Typically, there are variables/adjustments, but for now cost of debt = IR on that debt

      Equity costs the company in two ways
      Dividend issues
      Giving up future stock appreciation

      Cost of Equity = Risk-Free Rate + Equity Risk Premium * Levered Beta
      Risk-Free Rate = IR we earn by investing in a “riskless” security, such as 30-year US Treasury notes
      Equity Risk Premium = the extra yield you could earn by investing in an index that tracks the market in your country of choosing (usually within 3-10%)
      Beta = refers to the “riskiness” of this company relative to all other companies in the stock market (β = 1 is as volatile as index, <1 is less, >1 is more)

      You can also estimate you own β through public comps rather than historical data

      Unlevering and Relevering Beta
      You assume that a company’s “true” riskiness is more in-line with how risky similar companies in the market are than its own historical track record

      Unlevered Beta=Levered Beta1+(1-Tax Rate)DebtEquity Value

      We say that there are two types of risks with a company
      the inherent, business risk
      the risk that comes from debt
      Unlevered Beta represents just the business risk
      Levered Beta represent both business and debt risk; the denominator in the equation above simply “removes” the debt risk from Levered β, creating Unlevered β
      Assume that debt risk is proportional to Debt/Equity value ratio; debt is tax-deductible, so that helps reduce risk from debt slightly
      Next, calculate the Unlevered β for public comps, take the median, then re-lever to approximate levered β for target firm

      Levered Beta=Unlevered Beta(1+(1-Tax Rate)DebtEquity Value)

      To recap, we unlever public comp beta, assume the target firm has the same inherent business risk, then we relever this unlevered beta with the target firm’s current debt condition to capture its inherent business risk + debt risk

      Alternate Cost of Equity formula (only if firm has stable dividends)
      Cost of Equity=Dividends per ShareShare Price+Growth Rate of Dividends

      Calculating the Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)
      WACC = (Cost of Equity * % Equity) + (Cost of Debt * % Debt * (1 - Tax Rate)) + (Cost of Preferred Stock * % Preferred Stock)
      If you’re using Unlevered FCF, use WACC because you care about all parts of the company’s capital structure and all investors
      If you’re using Levered FCF, use Cost of Equity as the Discount Rate because you only care about Equity investors

      WACC Implications
      Debt will almost always push down WACC because IRs are lower than the cost of equity usually and interest is tax-deductible
      Preferred Stock is generally cheaper than Equity, but not debt because it is not tax-deductible
      Equity tends to “cost” the most because it is inherently more risky

      Cost of Equity Implications
      Higher Risk-Free Rates and Equity Risk Premiums always increase it
      Debt increases the Cost of Equity, because debt increases the chance of default, leaving the common shareholder with nothing

      Regardless of which type of FCF used, you always use LeveredBeta when calculating Cost of Equity. Remember that if a company has Debt, it makes both the Equity of the company and the entire firm itself riskier.

      Targeted vs. Existing Capital Structures
      What if the company’s capital structure changes in the future?
      You’d use this capital structure if known in the new Debt and Equity value calculations
      In real life this almost never comes up because no one “knows” how a company’s capital structure will change far in advance

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Terminal Value}

    \begin{core}{Terminal Value}

      4 - Getting Closure in Valuation
      When a firm’s cash flows grow at a “constant” rate forever, the present value of those cash flows can be written as:

      Value = Expected Cash Flow Next Period / (r-g)
      r = discount rate
      g = expected growth rate forever

      The “constant” growth rate is called a stable growth rate and cannot be higher than the growth rate of the economy in which the firm operates


      Myths
      Truths
      The only way to estimate terminal value is to use the perpetual growth model

      The perpetual growth model can give you an infinite value

      The growth rate is your biggest driver of terminal value

      Your growth rate cannot be negative in a perpetual growth model


      If you terminal value is a high proportion of your DCF value, it is flawed
      The terminal value can be based on annuities or a liquidation value

      Not if growth forever is capped at the growth rate of the economy

      Growth is not free & increasing growth can add or destroy value

      Growth can be negative forever & is often more reflective of reality
      fossil fuel companies (falling industry)

      The terminal value should be a higher percentage of value today

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Terminal Value Calculation Methods}

      Method #1: Assume That the Company Gets Sold for a Certain Multiple
        Ex. EBITDA = \$500 million in Year 5
        EBITDA multiple = 10x
        thus EV is \$5billion = Terminal Value
        This method is simple and commonly used in banking, but estimating this exact multiple is difficult, so you always use a range of multiples in a sensitivity table

      Method #2: Assume That the Company Keeps Operating Indefinitely and Sum Its Future Cash Flows
        Called the “Gordon Growth” for “Perpetual Growth” method
        Although FCF grows theoretically forever, the present value of the FCF keeps shrinking because the Discount Rate is higher than the growth rate of these FCF
        The sum of these future cash flows will eventually converge on a single number

        Terminal Value=Final Year FCF1+Terminal FCF Growth RateDiscount Rate-Terminal FCF Growth Rate

        The Terminal Growth Rate should always be very low—less than or equal to the country's GDP growth rate. Otherwise, the company’s FCF will exceed GDP.

      Which Method to Use?
      There is no best method and neither will necessary produce lower/higher results because you can’t directly compare the
      In both cases, the key variables—the Terminal Multiple and the Terminal Growth Rate—are difficult to determine precisely
      If the industry is cyclical or multiples are unpredictable, Gordon Growth may be better
      If multiples are predictable, the Multiples Method may be better

    \end{core}
    
    \begin{core}{End Game}

      Discount the terminal value and add it to the discounted FCF from the Projection Period to generate Enterprise Value (with Unlevered FCF) or Equity Value (with Levered FCF) and thus the implied share price after finding out diluted shares outstanding
      Remember that in a presentation to investors, we always show a range of values for the analysis using a sensitivity table

    \end{core}

  \subsection{DCF Sensitivities}

    \begin{core}{Factors That Impact a DCF and WACC}

      Overall Impact: The Discount Rate and Terminal Value tend to have the biggest impact here compared to, say, a change in a company’s revenue growth rate or margins.

      It gets trickier when questions are like, “Well, what about a 10% change in revenue vs a 1% difference in the Discount Rate?”
      In this case, you’re better off “hedging your answer and saying that at a certain level, the revenue increase or decrease will make more of a difference than the new Discount Rate, but it varies greatly by company and by specific assumptions

      Rules of Thumb for Cost of Equity:
      Smaller companies generally have a higher Cost of Equity because expected returns are higher
      Companies in emerging markets are the same for the same reasons
      Additional Debt increases cost of equity because it makes the company riskier for all investors
      Additional Equity lowers the cost of equity by decreasing the percentage of debt capital
      Using Historical vs Calculated Beta doesn’t have a predictable impact—it could go either way

      Rules of Thumb for WACC:
      WACC is higher for smaller companies and ones in emerging markets
      Additional Debt will reduce WACC because it shifts the capital structure and equity is more expensive than debt. Levered Beta goes up, but the additional debt in WACC makes up for more
      Additional Preferred Stock generally reduces WACC because it is less expensive than Equity (Common Stock)
      Higher Debt IR will increase WACC b/c it increases cost of equity
      
    \end{core}

\section{Leveraged Buyout (LBO)}

  \begin{core}{Leveraged Buyout Overview}

    When buying out a company, the returns go up when you buy through debt because reducing the amount of cash you pay up-front for an asset has a disproportionate effect on your returns… since money today is worth more than money tomorrow

    Private equity firms buy companies using a combination of debt and equity (cash), and sell it in 3-5 years into the future to realize a return

    Why LBOs work
    By using debt, you reduce the up-front cash payment for the company, which boosts your returns
    Using the company’s cash flows to repay debt principal and pay interest also produces a better return than keeping the cash flow
    You sell the company in the future, which allows you to gain back the majority of the funds you spent to acquire it in the first place

    The Mechanics of an LBO
    The PE firm calculates how much it will cost to acquire all the shares outstanding (public) or to simply acquire it (private)
    To raise funds the PE firm will use a small amount of cash on-hand (almost always <50% of valuation) and then raise debt from investors to pay for the rest…
    To investors, the PE firm says, “We’re using the debt to buy an income-generating asset—this company. And we’ll repay everything because we’ll sell this company in the future and use the proceeds to pay you back”
    PE firms raises debt from investors and combines with its own cash to acquire the company
    PE firm operates the company for years into the future, and uses its cash flow to pay interest and debt principal it used
    After 3-5 years, the PE firm sells the company or takes it public via IPO and realizes a return

    What makes a good LBO candidate
    Stable/predictable cash flows for debt repayment (most important)
    Relative undervaluation to public comps
    Low-risk businesses
    Not much need for ongoing investments like CapEx
    Opportunity to cut costs and increase margins
    Strong management team
    Solid base of assets to use as collateral

  \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Debt Structure}

      3 major things to do:
      Assume a purchase price and the amount of debt and equity you’ll be using
      Figure out the debt terms, including IR and annual repayment
      Create a Sources & Uses schedule that tracks where your funds are coming from, and where they’re going

      For the purchase price, you would use all the standard valuation techniques and also look at the premium if public
      Focus on Equity Value
      The percentages of debt and equity would be based on recent, similar deals as well as what lenders will go for

      Generally lower IR
      10-20% annual principal repayment
      “less risky” since backed by collateral
      High-yield debt

      Generally no annual repayment

      Bank debt has maintenance covenants (Eg. Total debt must always be below 4x), whereas high-yield debt has incurrence covenants (Eg. the company cannot sell off assets)

      Assessing potential debt structure
      Leverage Ratio (Debt/EBITDA)? Too high or too low relative to public comps
      Interest Coverage Ratio (EBITDA/Interest) Is that too high or too low compared to other companies?

  \subsection{Statement Projections}

    \begin{core}{Free Cash Flow Projections}

      Projecting financial statements in an LBO is a fundamental part of the due diligence process. It helps investors assess the financial feasibility, risk, and potential returns associated with acquiring a company through leverage

      Few guidelines for the IS
      Revenue growth: You generally want this to decline over time (e.g. 10% initially down to 5% in Year 5)

      It’s not necessarily that you want revenue growth to slow down, but rather that the focus shifts from high revenue growth to other financial priorities. While revenue growth remains important, it must be balanced with the need to service debt, generate cash flow, and enhance overall profitability to create value for the LBO investors

      EBIT / EBITDA Margins: Should stay in the same range each year
      BS Items: Many of these can be percentages of revenue, COGS, or Operating Expenses; you can use historical averages
      D&A and Other Non-Cash Charges: Historical averages and percentages of revenue
      Capital Expenditures: Make it a percentage of revenue, use a historical average, or assume a fixed dollar amount growth each year.

      After calculating for the period, you can use the numbers to calculate how much debt the company pays off each year.

      Calculating Free Cash Flow
      In the context of an LBO model, FCF means Cash Flow from Operations minus CapEx. Different from Levered/Unlevered FCF
      “How much cash do we have left to pay debt principal each year, after we’ve already paid for our normal expenses and for the interest expenses on that debt?”
      If there are other recurring items, you may add those
      Eg. Investment purchases or sales each year

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Debt Repayment}

      Once FCF is calculated FCF, logic of debt repayment is simple
      Make mandatory repayments first
      With remaining cash flows, make optional repayments
      However, most companies have a minimum cash balance
      Not all types of debt can be repaid early
      And the company may not have enough cash flow for its minimum mandatory debt repayments, in which it would need to borrow more via a Revolver

      Circular References
      The interest expense on the IS is dependent on how much debt is paid off throughout the year
      Normally, you average the beginning and ending Debt balances to determine annual interest expense—but that creates a paradox because the ending Debt balance depends on how much cash flow you had, after paying interest… but the interest expense depends on the Debt balance
      To simplify models, you can base the interest expense on only the beginning Debt balance each year to get around this problem

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Returns and IRR}

    \begin{core}{Returns}

      Simple Explanation of Returns
      The equity (cash) that the PE firm invests is a negative
      Any cash/dividends are a positive (most of the time it is \$0 b/c any cash flow available is used for debt repayment)
      The sale proceeds minus debt outstanding at the end are also positive and represents what the PE firm “gets” when selling the company

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Internal Rate of Return}

      The internal rate of return (IRR) is just the effective IR on the investment

      If the PE firm receives cash of dividends from the company, those would increase the IRR because they’d boost the total amount of funds received by the firm

      In an LBO model, you assume an exit EBITDA multiple for the company, usually close to or below the purchase EBITDA multiple

      In this case, the actual purchase price would be lower than the exit price because total EBITDA would increase throughout the 3-5 years

      Once the Enterprise value is calculated, you work backwards to calculate the Equity Value

      IRR calculation rules of thumb
      PE firm doubles its money in 5 years → 15% IRR
      PE firm triples its money in 5 years → 25% IRR
      PE firm doubles its money in 3 years → 26% IRR
      PE firm triples its money in 3 years → 44% IRR

      Time plays a huge factor; if a PE firm can get a good price for a company early on, it will always sell the company earlier rather than later

      Greatest impact on IRR in an LBO
      Purchase Price
      %Debt and %Equity Used
      Exit Price

      Changes That Increase IRR: Lower Purchase Price, Less Equity, Higher Revenue Growth, Higher EBITDA Margins, Lower IR, Lower CapEx
      Changes that Reduce IRR: Higher Purchase Price, More Equity, Lower Revenue Growth, Lower EBITDA Margins, Higher IR, Higher CapEx
      Ask, “Will this change boost cash flow?”

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Advanced LBO Topics}

    \begin{core}{Dividend Recapitalizations}

      Dividend Recapitalizations (Dividend Recaps)
      PE firm forces the company to take on additional debt and issues a big cash dividend to itself with the proceeds from that debt (Debt UP and Retained earnings DOWN on BS)
      This boosts returns because it allows the PE firm to regain some of their initially invested capital
      Debt investors dislike dividend recaps because they worsen the company’s credit quality and only helps a select few (PE owners)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Private Companies}

      Private Companies
      LBOs of private companies are difficult because
      The company may not want to sell, especially if it’s owned by one person, and unlike with public companies the PE firm cannot “force” a sale
      Info is limited, so an LBO assessment may not be feasible
      Purchase and Exit Prices are based on implied value rather than the premiums method

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Other Debt Types}

      Other Types of Debt
      Bank Debt can be split into different types of “Term Loans” which have different characteristics
      “High-Yield Debt” is split into Senior Notes, Subordinated notes, and Mezzanine which also have different characteristics
      Payment-in-Kind (PIK) allows interest to accrue into the debt principal rather than being mandatorily paid in cash

    \end{core}
