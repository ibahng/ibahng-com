% Introducing the Stock Market
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Equities, stocks, and shares all mean the same thing—part ownership of a company

% The stock market’s purpose is to allocate resources to productive enterprises. The stock exchange is appealing for two reasons:
% * Stock market makes it easy to enter and exit company investments. A private investor can quickly and easily own parts of thousands of companies
% * Picking the right company could earn you millions

% The total bond market value is larger than the total stock market value—in both value and number of distinct securities.

% Companies typically go public to raise money to expand the business or enable founders or owners to sell their stakes to the public, or both. Going public is down through Initial Public Offerings or IPOs—which are managed between the company and an investment bank.

% While equities list in splashy IPOs, they tend to delist more discreetly. They can delist for many reasons:
% * The company may have been bought by a private equity fund
% * Management may have been fed up w/ the regulatory burden of being public
% * The company may have gone bust
% * A whole industry may have died (EX. Kodak)
% * There are also good reasons, such as successful mergers/acquisitions

% Stocks go up and down throughout the trading day. Most US stocks trade from 9:30 AM to 4:00 PM, New York Time (after hours also exist as well).

% Indices are baskets of stocks.
% Equity indices are useful not only for monitoring market movements. They can also tell whether a money manager has beaten the market.
% Indices are diverse:
% EX. Broken down by region
% However, while an index may be domiciled in one country, companies therein may have revenues coming from many other countries.

% Company size, industry, and country are the three most common organizing principles for stock market indices.
% 	EX. S&P 500 membership → size (MKT CAP)

% Size in the S&P 500 tells you how much it also contributes towards the moves in the index.
% 	EX. Apple MKT CAP = $724.89B; S&P 500 = $18.2T

% Index Weight % = ($724.89B / $18.2T) * 100% = 3.98%

% Summary: Introducing the Stock Market
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * IPOs raise money and/or transfer ownership
% * Companies delist when they are bought, go bust, or balk at reporting requirements
% * Equity indices come in all shapes and sizes
% * Index performance is calculated from the performance of index members

% The Nature of Equities
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% An equity promises residual cash flows from a company—that is, what is left when all other claims are satisfied.

% Stocks are high risk, high return assets while bonds are low risk, low return. Closer to retirement usually entails a larger ownership of bonds relative to stocks.

% With decades worth of data, we can clearly see that the bond market has been much less volatile than the stock market.

% Factors to consider when comparing stock/bond returns:
% * Returns from dividends: The reinvestments of dividends into the index fund viewed can entail a massive difference when magnified over several years. This is the main distinguisher that makes equities far more attractive than bonds.
% * The nominal nature of stock and bond returns: Inflation eats up returns for both types of securities; when comparing returns to GDP, make sure to compare with nominal GDP.

% Bond downsides and upsides are both finite while stock upsides are infinite and downsides are only finite.

% Summary: The Nature of Equities
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * Shareholders own a share of company earnings and assets
% * Stocks are volatile because earnings are volatile
% * Shareholder returns come from both shares going up and payments of dividends
% * The range of shareholder outcomes is asymmetrical. Shares can go to zero or can multiply in value

% Equity Research
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% What do shareholders actually own:
% * The earnings that a company generates after paying of all of its expenses
% * The sum total of company assets that are not owed to lenders—“net assets”

% Earnings are a stream of future cash flow just like bond repayments are a stream of future cash flows. However, fixed income repayments are preordained, whereas equity earnings are not. Changes in revenues and costs make residual earnings volatile.

% Quarterly financial numbers are reported to investors at earnings announcements.

% While most quarterly financial releases align with the calendar year, many companies also don’t. However here is the typical date range for the quarterly financial releases (earnings season):
% * Q1: Mid April
% * Q2: Late July
% * Q3: Late October
% * Q4: Early February

% How to estimate future company earnings:
% * Industry Classification
% * Suppliers and Buyers
% * Revenue Projections
% * Cost Base

% Industry Classification
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Same firms operate in only one or several industries.
% Industries are arranged into hierarchies. In the S&P 500, they are arranged into 11 big industry families. 
% When examining a company’s revenue, it is best to distinguish it into distinct industries.

% Suppliers and Buyers
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% All companies exist in an ecosystem of suppliers, competitors, and buyers.
% Understanding the web of relationships within this supply chain can lead to more accurate estimates.

% Revenue Projections
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% How big is the pie and how big is the company’s slice of that pie?
% The more successful a product is against its competitors, the larger chunk of market share it receives (the bigger slice).

% Cost Base
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Dividing the costs of different parts of a product gives insight into how cost changes may impact product earnings largely or miniscule.

% Rolls-Royce Case Study
% Most of RR’s revenue comes from selling aircraft engines. From 2003 to 2014, revenue rose from 6B to 14B pounds.
% This is because aircraft manufacturers did well because airlines did well because of increased plane ticket demand.
% Once this web is mapped out, an analyst looks at historic performance using reported financial number to estimate earnings (Earnings statement, investor conference calls’ transcripts).

% The world is full of surprising underlying drivers:
% * Pandemics affect the number of air travelers
% * High Wall Street bonuses boost jewelry sales
% * Booming stock markets lift recreational vehicle sales etc.

% Investing analysts estimate upcoming earnings by examining industry drivers and seeing their relative impact on earnings and comparing it to other analysts’ estimates.

% When the actual results are above the estimates, it is called a “beat” and when below, a “miss”. When a company “beats” or “misses” tends to be in the headline of an earnings news story.
% Analysts are not infallible. They often struggle to spot turning points—which companies can use to beat estimates several quarters in a row.

% While earnings estimate beats usually lead to a rise in share price and a miss leads to a fall, sometimes this relationship doesn’t hold off.
% There is more to guessing share price movements than accurately estimating earnings. It’s called valuation.

% Summary: Equity Research
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * Analysts must know the industries in which a company operates
% * Industry estimates are foundational to a company financial model
% * Industry drivers help formulate earnings estimates
% * Investors assess company results by comparing them to estimates

% Absolute Valuation
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% In the US, 3% of all stocks change hands each business day.

% The number of shares a company multiplied by the price per share gives you the size of the company, otherwise known as the market capitalization, the main factor investors care about when valuing a company (do not compare share price to one another).

% A stock split is when a company feels that its share price is too high for retail investors, so it divides into two or more shares.
% A reverse stock split, also known as a stock consolidation, it the opposite.

% When a company thinks its own stock is sheep, it might explore another corporate action: stock buybacks. When a company does a stock buyback, the value of each stock remaining in the market goes up because there are now fewer slices of bread (supply and demand).

% Share prices are facts while valuations are opinion. Remember that equity holders own residual cash flow, thus they care deeply about earnings estimates.
% Analysts tend to change their earnings estimates for a company after quarterly earnings announcements and after any other news that forces a rethink of the company’s prospects.

% There are two common ways in which investors value companies:
% * Absolute Valuation is when an analyst estimates future earnings and then, in turn, estimates how much those estimates are worth in today’s money.
% * Relative Valuation is when an analyst compares a company to another similar company or to the overall market to define relative value.

% Absolute Valuation vs Relative Valuation
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Feature | Absolute Valuation | Relative Valuation
% ---|---|---
% Pros | Precise, Anchored to earnings, Disciplined thought process | Easier to understand, Simple to calculate, Does not demand long-term functions
% Cons | Demands clairvoyance, Laborious, Prone to subtle manipulation | Directional, Hard to find truly comparable firms, Presupposes that the company you are comparing it to is fairly valued

% Absolute Valuation (DCF Valuation) Process
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% 1. Estimate long-term future cash flows from the firm
% 2. Estimate the rate at which to discount those cash flows to derive today’s value. This rate is known as the weighted average cost of capital, or simply the WACC
% 3. Take the outputs from step 1 and 2, and discount the estimated future cash flows by the WACC
% 4. Take this total firm value and, from it, derive the market capitalization
% 5. Slice up the MKT CAP by the # of shares outstanding to arrive at the estimated fair share price

% Estimating Cash Flows
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% An analyst must look at the whole firm’s cash flows and use his company projection of revenues and costs to project this so called “free cash flow”.
% The derivation of free cash flow from the revenue and cost projections is rule-based. The hard part of generating these projections is not so much the mechanics as it is making the required assumptions about the future.

% Estimating discount rates (WACC)
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% If an investor is looking to invest in a firm, remember he could always simply buy US treasury bonds. US bond yields, therefore, set the baseline for the discount rate for equity investors.
% Investors need to discount their cash flows at a higher rate than the government bond yield in order to reflect the added risk of investing in a firm. How much higher? The discount rate used for the overall firm is a blend of the discount rates for the equity and debt, blended in proportion to the relative split of equity and debt financing.

% WACC = (equity financing% * cost of equity) + (debt financing% * cost of debt)
% Cost of debt is simply the firm’s bond yields

% Estimating cost of equity
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% People expect to be compensated for taking extra risk from equities and expect greater annual returns than US bond yields.
% The calculation used is “capital asset pricing model” → general riskiness of stock market as starting point → volatility of stock being valued.

% Step A) Take the 10-year government bond yield
% Step B) Take the historic overall market return
% Step C) Calculate market risk premium
% 	market return (B) - 10-year bond yield (A)
% Step D) “beta" is how much riskier the stock is compared to the overall market
% 	higher β: move in exaggerated way relative to movement in the market
% 	lower β: move more subdued

% FIGURE OF A BETA REGRESSION GRAPH>>

% Step E) Multiply β w/ market risk premium (equity risk premium)
% Step F) Add the figure from Step E to the 10-year government bond yield (risk-free rate)

% Discounting Cash Flows
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% After discounting cash flows from the future free cash flow amounts, the estimated value of the firm at this point is the total of these “discounted” cash flows.
% The riskier the firm, the less you would value the future cash flows.

% Deriving Market Capitalization
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Debt is not part of the market cap of the company because it doesn’t relate to shareholders.
% This value is calculated from the trading value of the bonds or from the value of the debt on the balance sheet of the firm.
% Cash balances, however, belong to the shareholders along w/ the earnings.

% MKT CAP = EV - bondholder value + cash + equity value

% Estimating Share Price
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Divide the estimated equity value by the number of shares to arrive at the estimated fair share price.

% Absolute Valuation Observations
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Ten year bond yields (and thus interest rates) are foundational to the WACC, but not the end all be all.
% An analyst has to be on top of the earnings cycle and the interest rate cycle to form a view on where the stock market is going next.
% No company can grow faster than nominal GDP growth in the long-term or it will subsume the global economy.
% Therefore long-term company revenue growth is limited to nominal GDP growth which is typically lower than common WACC level.

% WACC (~8%) > nominal GDP growth (~5%) > long term company revenue growth

% The very long run is unimportant to equity investors because in the long run we are all dead b/c WACC is greater than nominal GDP growth.
% Heavily indebted companies have more volatile share prices than companies with no debt. This is because when the total value of the firm changes, the amount owed back to bond holders does not. Therefore, the piece owned by the shareholders—moves sharply.

% Absolute Valuation Sensitivities
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% The whole process has moving parts. Therefore, it is not sufficient to be an expert solely on the company and industry specifics in order to have a handle on valuation.
% Common sensitivities:
% * Revenue and costs may be affected by the FX market
% * WACC will be affected by moves in the overall stock market—which change the equity risk premium—and by moves in the 10-year bond yield—which changes the risk free rate
% * Changes in share price can determine how easily a firm may receive funding
% When analysts ignore these factors (which they often do) they get surprised after detailed spreadsheets and calculations provide them with a false sense of confidence.

% Summary: Absolute Valuation
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * Absolute valuation involves the discounting of future cash flows
% * Future profits in the long term are worth less than future profits in the short-term
% * Good financial models balance simplicity with insight
% * The concept of absolute valuations are usually precisely wrong

% Relative Valuation
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Relative valuation is not an effort to calculate an actual fair share price, but rather to determine whether a company is undervalued, overvalued, or fairly valued.
% It is a two-step process. The first step in the process is to choose a relevant metric with which to conduct the relative valuation. The second step is to compare your favored metric to something else.

% Dividend yield
% regular, steady payments to shareholders that are carved out of earnings (variable) and easier to calculate than bond yields.

% Dividend yield = dividend per share / latest share price

% companies frequently pay dividends twice or four times a year—the sum of these is used as the “dividend per share”.
% Another valuation measure is earnings yield = EPS / latest share price which is typically greater b/c dividends are a part of earnings.

% Price to Earnings Yield (PE Ratio)
% the reciprocal of an earnings yield is the famous price-to-earnings or P/E ratio.

% PE Ratio = latest share price / EPS

% It is also customary to use the estimated earnings per share to calculate the so-called “prospective P/E ratio”.

% Use of P/E ratios is valuation
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% P/E ratios are the mainstay of relative equity valuations.
% If you have an earnings estimate, and you have a sense for a fair P/E ratio to apply to that earnings estimate, then equity valuation is a piece of cake. You simply multiply the earnings by the P/E ratio to get P.
% If either E or P/E goes up, P goes up but the guesses of earnings and of a reasonable P/E ratio are fiercely hard to generate.
% Rapidly growing companies can grow into very high P/E ratios; don’t assume high P/E ratios make a stock expensive.
% Estimates are particularly difficult when a company is going through a rapid growth phase.

% An investor will compare his favored metric to one of three things to gauge relative value:
% 1. That same metric over history: How has the company’s metric trended over time?
% 2. What same metric for similar companies: How does the company’s metric compare to the average of its peer group?
% 3. That same metric for the overall market: How does the company’s metric compare to that of the market?

% Relative Valuation with Self
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Share prices alone do not give a full picture of valuation.

% Just because shares go up or down does not make them expensive or cheap per se. One always needs to look at P/E multiples in the context of earnings.
% EX) McDonalds
% The earnings went up steeply from the early 2000s while the P/E stayed the same.
% If the P/E remains stable, as it has done over many years and the recent dip in earnings reverses as people buy burgers again, then a resurgence in earnings will lead to a rise in share price. If this hypothesis on the faddish nature of the fast-food industry is correct and McDonald’s reignites growth, McDonald’s is a buy.

% Relative Valuation with Peers
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% The artistry of peer group valuation comes in the selection of which companies are truly peers.
% It’s very difficult to compare conglomerates which operate in several different industries.
% It’s also very frustrating when a direct peer is private preventing any PE ratio comparisons because private companies don’t have a share price.

% Relative Valuation with Market
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% If a whole industry is overvalued, this can give peer group valuation a generation of a false sense of security. To guard against this, you can compare the company metrics to the overall market to make sure that the company is not on a dramatically higher multiple than the overall market.

% How the economy drives the stock market
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Recall that earnings press conferences are released weeks after their relevant period, thus an increase in market value of an index is simply a reaction to the announcement of earnings increase or decrease.

% Economic Indicators -> Real GDP Growth -> Corporate Earnings -> Equities

% P/E ratios are subject to the “reversion to the mean”, however this law breaks down when we consider corporate earnings because they are linked to the economy which has grown consistently since the industrial revolution.

% Summary: Relative Valuation
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * Relative valuation is the quick and easy comparison of one valuation to another
% * The risk of relative valuation is being led astray by unrealistic reference points
% * Earnings and multiples are used to estimate fair share prices
% * Fast growing companies warrant high multiples and vice versa
% * The state of the economy is a key driver of relative valuation
