== OPPORTUNITY COST

Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost
  - As more and more of a good is produced, the opportunity cost of producing the good increases
  - Reason: The resources that are best suited for producign a good are used first, and once they are used, producing more requires resources that are less suited for producing it, so more resources are required

Cost-Benefit Analysis
  Individuals and businesses make the best decisions by maximizing difference between total benefits and total costs

  Costs should include both explicit and implicit (opportunity) costs

  Example Problem: Adam decides to start a business. He spends \$200,000 in expenses to start the business, and earns \$255,000 in revenue during the first year. If he didn't start the business, he could’ve instead worked as an accountant earning a salary of \$50,000 and placed the \$200,000 he spent in a savings account with an annual interest rate of 3%.

  What are the explicit and implicit costs of running the business for a year?

  Explicit costs: 200,000 spent to start business

  Implicit Costs
  - Forgone Salary: 50,000
  - Forgone Interest: 0.03 x 200,000 = 6,000
  - Total Implicit Costs: 56,000

  Accounting vs Economic Profit
  - Accounting profit = revenue - explicit costs
  - Economic profit = revenue - explicit and implicit costs

Utility: the satisfaction from consuming a good
- marginal utility: additional utility from consuming an additional unit (ice cream cone example)

Marginal benefit vs marginal cost analysis
- optimal quantity is where MB = MC
- there is a graph of mc as a horizontal line and mb as a downward sloping line because marginal benefit of consumption decreases as you consume more (constant marginal cost)
- there is another graph of mc as an upward sloping line (increasing marginal cost)

Utility Maximization Using MU Analysis
- when faced with buying a combination of two products, the individual should always incrementally buy the product(s) that have the highest MU per dollar on that incremental purchase.

\section{UNIT 2: Supply and Demand}

== DEMAND AND DEMAND DETERMINANTS

== SUPPLY AND SUPPLY DETERMINANTS

Elasticity
- Measures how responsive something is to a change in something else
- Price Elasticity of Demand: Measures how much quantity demanded changes in response to changes in price

\[
E_D = \left| \frac{\%\Delta Q_{\text{demanded}}}{\%\Delta P} \right| = \left| \frac{\frac{Q_{\text{new}} - Q_{\text{old}}}{Q_{\text{old}}}}{\frac{P_{\text{new}} - P_{\text{old}}}{P_{\text{old}}}} \right|
\]

Determinants of Price Elasticity of Demand
- Substitutes
    More substitutes available = more elastic
    more similar substitutes = more elastic
- Want vs Need
    need = more elastic
- Timeframe
    short term = less elastic
- share of income
    lower share of income = less elastic

Describing Elasticity
- a horizontal demand curve is perfectly elastic, meaning that price increases will lead to zero demand and price decreases will lead to infinite demand
- an elastic coefficient between 1 and infinity is elastic
- "" at 1 is unit elastic
- "" between 0 and 1 is inelastic
- "" at 0 is perfectly inelastic (vertical demand curve)

Total revenue test
- if revenue decreases as price and quantity increases, elastic
- if revenue doesn't change as price and quantity change, unit elastic
- if revenue increases as price and quantity increases, inelastic

the total revenue test also implies that a standard downward sloping demand curve is elastic towards the top left, unit elastic in the middle, and inelastic on the bottom right

Price Elasticity of Supply: Measures how much quantity supplied changes in response to changes in price 
\[
E_S = \frac{\%\Delta Q_{\text{supplied}}}{\%\Delta P} = \frac{\frac{Q_{\text{new}} - Q_{\text{old}}}{Q_{\text{old}}}}{\frac{P_{\text{new}} - P_{\text{old}}}{P_{\text{old}}}}
\]

Determinants of Price Elasticity of Supply
- Timeframe
    short term = less elastic

Other Elasticities

- Income Elasticity of Demand: measures the elasticity of a good based on the income level of consumers
    if positive, good is normal
    if negative, good is inferior

- Cross Elasticity of Demand: measures the elasticity of a good compared to levels of a substitute of complementary product
    if positive, x and y are substitutes
    if negative, x and y are complementary

== MARKET EQUILIBRIUM

== SHIFTS IN EQUILIBRIUM

Consumer and Producer Surplus
  
  First, we can define the demand curve as the marginal utility from the consumer side (down sloping because of diminishing marginal utility)
  Also, we can define the supply curve as the marginal cost from the producer side (upward sloping because law of diminishing returns)

  The curves represent the marginla utility and cost summation of individual consumer and producer curves

  At the market equililbrium, since the purchaser of the first units is willing to pay more than the equilibirum price, his/her consumer surplus would be the difference between what he pays and what we would have paid

  The reverse is true on the supplier side (producer surplus)

  The total consumer and producer surplus is represented by the area between the eq price line and the respective mb and mc curves

  Allocative Efficiency: total economic surplus is maximized, occurs at quantity where MB = MC (market equilibrium)

  Keep in mind that the consumer and producer surplus can be greater or smaller based on the elasticity of the d or s curve

Effects of Government Intervention:
  
  Price Ceiling
  A price ceiling below the market equilibrium would set the price below market eq price, thus CS increases and PS decreases. however there is also deadweight loss

  Price Floor
  The opposite effect occurs when there is a price floor (increased PS, decreased CS, deadweight loss)

  Per unit Taxes
  The supply curve would shift up exactly by that amount, raising the eq price and decreasing both CS and PS, the square section between CS and PS is the tax revenue and the triangle that sticks out is DWL
    Depending on how inelastic and elastic the S and D curves are, the incidence of tax can be more carried by the consumers or the producers

International Trade

  World supply and demand curves are found by horizontally adding market supply and demand curves
    In this case, because everyone now switches to the world supply and demand curve, a country will export or import the surplus or deficit of goods it possess based on its economic supply and demand curve

Public Policy

  Tariff: a fee on domestic importer of the goods
  When a tariff of 1 dollar is imposed, consumer surplus is increased, producer surplus is increased, and a portion of the plot is captured as tariff revenue 

  Quotas: a limits on how many units of a product you can import
  This as essentially the same effect as tariffs, exporters can charge a higher price because you are importing less in terms of count
 
\section{UNIT 3: Production, Cost, and the Perfect Competition Model}

Production Function: shows how much output is created for various different inputs (factory size, count, number of workers, capital, etc.)

Short Run vs Long Run
- short run: at least one input is fixed
- long run: all inputs can be adjusted

Total Marginal, and Average Product
- marginal is the incremental additional count of product you get from increasing a production input (such as workers)
- average product is the average amount of product you get from increasing a production input (such as workers)

Graphing TP, MP, and AP - PLOT THIS
- MP intersects AP at the AP's maximum

Short run fixed and variable costs
  
  Fixed costs: stay constant, equal to total costs at 0 units of output (ex. rent)

  Variable costs: depend on amount of output (ex. payment to workers)

  Remember: implicit costs are also included in economic terms

Short run 
  total cost
  marginal cost
  average total cost
  average fixed cost
  average variable cost

Graphs of MC, ATC, AFC, and AVC - PLOT THIS
  
  MC intersects ATC and AVC at their minimums
  
  ATC and AVC are U-shaped

  AFC constantly decreases

When marginal revenue for each product sold is fixed, it appears as a horizontal line

In order to maximize profits, produce at quantity where MR = MC

Long Run ATC
  
  In long run, costs aren't fixed (example. you can choose what size factory to rent)

  LRATC is also U-shaped and is made up of many SRATC curves

  The LRATC is U shaped for the follow reasons

    Economies of Scale: decreasing LRATC
      increased specialization
      deals for larger purchases

    Diseconomies of Scale: increasing LRATC
      firm is too large, hard to manage
      hiring more workers requires higher wages

    Minimum Efficient Scale: Minimum LRATC

Returns to Scale

  Increasing Returns to Scale: Output increases by more than increase in inputs
  - Example: Increasing all inputs by 70\% increases outputs by 80\%

  Decreasing Returns to Scale; Output increases by less than increase in inputs
  - Example: Increasing all inputs by 70\% increases output by 60\%

  Constant Returns to Scale: Output increases by same amount as increase in inputs
  - Example: Increasing all inputs by 70\% increases output by 70\%

Perfect Competition

  Characteristics:
  - Large number of firms
  - Each firm makes up a negligible amount of market output
  - No barriers to entry
  - The firms are PRICE TAKERS: they sell at market price
    - The firm's demand is perfectly elastic
      - setting a price higher than market price will cause 0 demand for firm's product
      - they can sell any amount of output at market price

TAKE A LOOK AT THE PERFECT COMP MARKET AND FIRM PLOTS

Firms’ Decisions to Enter or Leave

  If P > min ATC, firms make economic profit
  • More firms will enter in long run

  If P = min ATC, firms make a normal profit (0 economic profit)
  • Firms will neither enter nor leave

  If min AVC < P < min ATC, firms produce to minimize loss in short run
  • Firms will leave in long run

  If P < min AVC, firms shut down and leave immediately

Long Run Equilibrium

  Long Run: The firms will produce where P = min ATC and make a normal profit
  • Productive Efficiency: Producing at min ATC (quantity where MC = ATC)

  Reason
  • If P > min ATC, firms will enter, causing the market supply curve to shift right and price to decrease
  • If P < min ATC, firms will leave, causing the market supply curve to shift left and price to increase

\section{UNIT 4: Imperfect Competition}

Types of Imperfect Competition

  Monopoly: One firm selling product, price maker, very high barriers to entry

  Oligopoly: Small number of firms selling identical or differentiated products, characterized by interdependence, high barriers to entry (like gas station oil firms)

  Monopolistic Competition: Large number of firms selling differentiated products, low to no barriers to entry (like nike, adidas, underarmour, puma, etc)

  *In imperfect competition, to sell more of a product, the price must be lowered

Examples of Barriers to Entry
  Economies of Scale
  Patents \& Legal Statutes
  High Start-up Costs
  Ownership of Resources

Monopoly Demand \& Marginal Revenue
  The demand curve is downward sloping and the MR curve is even more downward sloping because of the price effect

  To maximize profit, produce at quantity where MR=MC, and price at the D curve price for that quantity
    this maximizes producer surplus at the expense of consumer surplus and the creation of DWL

Natural Monopoly
  
  Occurs when only one firm can achieve economies of scale (Decreasing LRATC)

  ATC decreases throughout the entire effective demand of the product, so MC is constantly below ATC

Price Discrimination
  
  Charging different prices for the same product

  Examples: Different ticket prices based on age, selling good in different places with different prices

  Increases producer surplus at the expense of consumer surplus

  Perfect Price Discrimination: charging each consumer the maximum they're willing to pay, MR becomes equal to D curve because of the absence of the price effect
    here all the surplus is the producer's, no consumer surplus

Monopolistic Competition
  Large number of firms selling differentiated products, low to no barriers to entry

  Usually demand is more elastic than for a monopoly

  Firms make the same decisions as a monopoly: sell at the quantity where MR = MC and at the price on the demand curve

  In the long run, firms enter or leave until D intersects ATC once at the quantity where MR = MC; each firm makes a normal profit (0 economic profit)
    this is not efficient because it leaves excess capacity (the difference beteen productively efficient quantity and quantity produced) 

Oligopolies

  Small number of firms selling identical or differentiated products, characterized by interdependence, high barriers to entry (Examples: Apple, Google, Samsung, Coke/Pepsi)

  Cartel: Different firms colluding (making agreements, such as collectively raising prices and limiting quantity), making the firms more similar to a monopoly

  • Antitrust laws often restrict cartels or make them illegal
  • Firms may have incentive to break agreement out of own interest

Game Theory Payoff Table

Two firms selling a similar product can either set their price low or high

• If both firms price high, they each earn \$100 in profit
• If one firm prices high and the other low, the firm that priced high earns \$50 in profit and the firm that priced low earns \$130 in profit
• If both firms price low, they each earn \$80 in profit

  Strategy: A course of action a firm can take

  Dominant Strategy: A strategy that will always produce a better outcome for a firm, regardless of what the other firm decides

  Nash Equilibrium: A situation where neither firm can obtain a better outcome by changing their strategy, assuming the other firm doesn't change their decision

\section{UNIT 5: Factor Markets} 

Factor Market: pretty much talking about labor

  Demand for Factor: The amount of that factor (e.g. workers) that firms are willing to purchase at each price (e.g. wage)

  Demand for a factor is derived, meaning that it depends on the demand for something else (in this case, the product)

  Supply of Factor: The amount of that factor available at each price

Determinants of Factor Demand
  price of product (wage)
  productivity of factors
  substitute and complementary resources (AI?)

Determinants of Factor Supply
  workforce size
  education
  alternative options
  preference of leisure

Marginal Revenue Product and Marginal Resource Cost

  Marginal Revenue Product (MRP): The additional revenue generated by one additional unit of the factor (Equal to MP * P in a perfectly competitive product market)

  Marginal Resource Cost (MRC): The additional cost for one additional unit of the factor (Constant in a perfectly competitive factor market)

  Profit Maximization: MRP = MRC -> IMPORTANT PLOT (MRP is a upside down U curve and MRC is flat, this is assuming a perfectly competitive factor market)

  Total product maximization using marginal product analysis is done by incrementally choosing the factor that provides the current best marginal product per dollar spentA

Monopsony (monopoly in the context of product inputs) Labor Market

  Only one firm employing workers; firm decidest he workers wage

  MRC increase faster than supply because purchasing additional workers requires raising wages for everyone (analogous to MR with demand)
    The best mode of action is to acquire inputs at the quantity where MRC intersects MRP and pay at the supply curve price, allowing for greater profits

\section{UNIT 6: Market Failure and the Role of the Government}

Market Failure & Government Intervention Methods

  Market Failure: When market forces fail to produce the allocatively efficient quantity of output

  Government Intervention Methods

  • Lump Sum Tax/Subsidy: Doesn't affect MB, MC, or profit-maximizing output; only affects fixed costs

  • Per-Unit Tax/Subsidy: Affects either MB or MC and therefore profit-maximizing output
    ◦ Taxes decrease output; Subsidies increase output

  • Price Ceiling/Floor: If binding, affects profit-maximizing output

  • Imposing Regulations
    ◦ Antitrust Laws

Externalities

  Externality: When an individual or individuals not involved in a market transaction are affected by the transaction

  • Negative Externality (Example: A factory releases pollution)
  • Positive Externality (Example: Streetlights ensure driver safety at night)

The graphs of marginal cost and marginal benefit we've seen so far have been referring to only private costs and benefits. In reality there could be a greater cost due to negative externaltiies (marginal social cost) or greater benefits due to positive externalities (marginal social benefit)

    Negative externality: Government should tax per unit to shift MPC up and reduce output to allocatively efficient amount

    Positive externality: Government should subsidize per unit to shift MPB up and increase output to allocatively efficient amount

Interventions on Monopolies

  Socially optimal: ceiling at price where D = MC

  OR

  Socially optimal: subsidize per unit so that MR = MC at allocatively efficient quantity

Effect of Price Ceilings on Natural Monopolies

  Without Price Ceiling: Firm produces at quantity where MR = MC and sells at D curve price for that quantity

  Fair return: ceilign price where D = ATC; firm makes a normal profit

  Socially optimal: ceiling at price where D = MC; total economic surplus is maximized, but firm produces at an economic loss
    solution: provide a lump sum subsisty to offset the loss

Problems

  Tragedy of the Commons: People overconsume common goods (Example: People overfish and deplete the amount of fish left in the future)

  * Solution: Government should limit use (Example: Giving permits)

  Free Rider Problem: People who benefit from non-excludable goods don’t pay or underpay for them

  * Solution: Government should funds the goods



Lorenz Curve: a graph that shows the cumulative share of income earned by the cumulative share of the population. 

Gini Coefficient: single number that summarizes the Lorenz Curve. It measures the area between the "Line of Perfect Equality" and the actual "Lorenz Curve." 
    
  0 <= G <= 1
  0\% <= G <= 100\%

  G = 0 means perfect equality (everyone has the same amount of income)

  G = 1 means perfect inequality (one person hold all the income)

Effects of Government Intervention on Inequality

  Progressive Taxes (Higher income = Higher percent of income taxed) decrease inequality

  Proportional Taxes (Fixed percent of income taxed) don’t affect income distribution

  Regressive Taxes (Lower income = Higher percent of income taxed) increase inequality

  Transfer payments to people with lower income reduces inequality

  Minimum wage and anti-discrimination laws can also reduce inequality
