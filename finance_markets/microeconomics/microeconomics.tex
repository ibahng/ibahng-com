\section{Fundamental Concepts}

  \textbf{Economics} is the social science that studies the production, distribution, and consumption of goods and services. At its core, economics addresses the fundamental problem of \textbf{scarcity}: while resources such as labor, capital, and natural resources are limited, human wants are essentially unlimited. This creates the central challenge that all economies must navigate---how to allocate scarce resources among competing uses. 

  \textbf{Microeconomics} is the branch of economics that studies how individuals, firms, and markets make decisions about the allocation of scarce resources. Unlike macroeconomics, which examines the economy as a whole, microeconomics focuses on the behavior of individual economic agents and the interactions between them.

  \begin{core}{Factors of Production} 

    The production of goods and services requires four essential factors of production: 

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item \textbf{Land} encompasses all natural inputs used in production, including agricultural land, mineral deposits, water resources, and the physical space upon which production facilities are constructed.

      \item \textbf{Labor} represents the human effort devoted to production, encompassing both physical and mental work. Labor includes the services of workers across all sectors, from agricultural laborers harvesting crops to knowledge workers developing software.

      \item \textbf{Capital} refers to goods that are produced and then used to produce other goods and services. Unlike consumer goods that satisfy wants directly, capital goods facilitate future production. Examples include manufacturing facilities, machinery, transportation equipment, and tools.

      \item \textbf{Entrepreneurship} represents the creative effort of individuals who combine the other three factors of production to create goods and services. Entrepreneurs bear risk, organize production, and drive innovation.

    \end{enumerate}

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Economic Systems}

    An \textbf{economic system} is the mechanism by which a society decides what to produce, how to produce it, and who receives what is produced. The two most prevalent economic systems in the modern world are market economies and command economies.

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item In a \textbf{market economy}, individuals make decisions about production and consumption, and individuals keep what they produce. This system is characterized by private property rights and prices determined by supply and demand, a framework often associated with \textbf{capitalism}.

      The market economy grows through the \textbf{invisible hand} whereby individuals pursuing their own self-interest inadvertently promote the general welfare. However, market economies face challenges, including income inequality and the potential for suboptimal business practices when left unregulated.

      \item In a \textbf{command economy}, the government makes all decisions about production and resource allocation, with no private property rights, thus often associated with \textbf{communism}. 

      This system faces significant challenges, including lack of incentive for individuals to invest, improve quality, or innovate.

    \end{enumerate}

    In practice, most contemporary economies, including major global economic powers such as the United States and China, operate as \textbf{mixed economies} that combine elements of both market and command systems. The relative balance between market forces and government intervention varies considerably across nations and remains a subject of ongoing economic and political debate.

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Opportunity Cost}

    When making economic decisions, individuals and societies must choose among mutually exclusive alternatives. The \textbf{opportunity cost} of a choice represents the value of the best alternative forgone. This concept is fundamental to rational decision-making because choosing one option necessarily means sacrificing another.

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Law of Increasing Opportunity Cost}

    The \textbf{law of increasing opportunity cost} states that as more and more of a good is produced, the opportunity cost of producing additional units increases. This occurs because resources are not equally suited for producing all goods. The resources best suited for producing a particular good are used first, and as production expands, less suitable resources must be employed, requiring increasingly larger quantities of resources to produce each additional unit.

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}[label=core:ppc]{Production Possibilities Curve}

    The \textbf{Production Possibilities Curve (PPC)} illustrates the maximum combinations of two goods that an economy can produce given its available resources and technology.

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item Points \textbf{on the curve} represent efficient production where all resources are fully utilized. 

      \item Points \textbf{inside the curve} indicate underallocation of resources

      \item Points \textbf{outside the curve}---although appearing unattainable---can occur temporarily during periods of economic overheating when resources are used beyond their sustainable capacity. However, this state is not sustainable and typically leads to inflation, resource depletion, or economic crisis.

    \end{enumerate}

    The \textbf{shape of the PPC} reveals important information about opportunity costs. A concave curve (\autoref{fig:ppc}) indicates increasing opportunity costs, meaning that producing additional units of one good requires sacrificing increasingly larger quantities of the other good. This typically reflects the fact that resources are not equally adaptable to producing both goods.

    Shifts in the PPC represent economic growth or decline. \textbf{Outward shifts indicate economic growth}, resulting from increases in available resources, improvements in technology, or enhancements in human capital. On the other hand, \textbf{inward shifts indicate economic decline}, potentially from natural disasters, war, or resource depletion.

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Comparative and Absolute Advantage}

    An economy has an \textbf{absolute advantage} when it can produce more of a good than another economy using the same resources. \textbf{Comparative advantage} is when an economy can produce a good at a lower opportunity cost than another economy.

    It is impossible for one economy to have a comparative advantage in both goods simultaneously. This principle, known as the \textbf{law of comparative advantage}, implies that all economies can benefit from trade by specializing in goods where they hold a comparative advantage. The gains from trade arise from this specialization and the subsequent exchange of goods.
    
  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Cost-Benefit Analysis}

    \textbf{Cost-benefit analysis} is a decision-making framework in which individuals and businesses compare the total benefits of an action against its total costs. The optimal decision is made when the marginal benefit equals the marginal cost.

    Costs should include both \textbf{explicit costs} (\textit{direct monetary payments}) and \textbf{implicit costs} (\textit{opportunity costs of resources already owned}).

    For instance, consider an entrepreneur who invests \$200,000 to start a business and earns \$255,000 in revenue during the first year. Subtracting explicit costs yields an \textbf{accounting profit} of \$55,000. However, this calculation ignores opportunity costs. Suppose this individual quit a \$50,000 salary job to pursue the business and foregone the 3\% annual interest (\$6,000) that the \$200,000 could have earned in a savings account. These implicit costs total \$56,000. When subtracted from the accounting profit, the \textbf{economic profit} is actually -\$1,000, revealing that the entrepreneur would have been financially better off keeping the salaried position and depositing the capital in savings (\textit{at least in the short run}).

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Utility}

    \textbf{Utility} represents the satisfaction or pleasure derived from consuming a good or service. Utility is subjective and varies across individuals.

    \textbf{Marginal utility} is the additional satisfaction gained from consuming one more unit of a good. For most goods, marginal utility diminishes as consumption increases---a phenomenon known as the \textbf{law of diminishing marginal utility}. For example, the first ice cream cone provides substantial satisfaction, but the tenth cone provides much less.

  \end{core} 

  \begin{core}{Utility Maximization Using Marginal Analysis}

    When faced with purchasing a combination of multiple products, a rational consumer should always incrementally purchase the product that provides the highest marginal utility per dollar spent on that marginal unit.

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Marginal Benefit and Cost}

    \textbf{Marginal benefit} represents the additional benefit received (\textit{often measured in monetary terms}), while \textbf{marginal cost} represents the additional cost of producing one more unit. Rational decision-making requires comparing marginal benefits and marginal costs.

    When $\text{MB} > \text{MC}$, producing additional units increases total welfare. When $\text{MB} < \text{MC}$, producing fewer units increases total welfare.

  \end{core}

\section{Supply and Demand}

  \begin{core}{Demand}

    \textbf{Demand} represents the quantity of a good that consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices during a given time period. The \textbf{Law of Demand} states an inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded: as prices rise, quantity demanded falls, and vice versa. This reflects both the \textbf{substitution effect} (\textit{consumers shift to cheaper alternatives}) and the \textbf{income effect} (\textit{higher prices reduce purchasing power}).

    \pgfig{
      \begin{axis}[
        \econplot{Quantity}{Price}
        \axispresetstwo
      ]

      \downslope{0}

      \pgfiglabel{10,2}{Demand}

      \end{axis} 
    }{Demand curve}{fig:demand}{0.75}

    The determinants of demand, factors that can cause the entire demand curve to shift, include:

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item \textbf{Consumer income}: Normal goods experience increased demand as income rises, while inferior goods experience decreased demand.

      \item \textbf{The number of buyers} in the market.

      \item \textbf{The prices of substitute and complementary goods}.

      \item \textbf{Consumer expectations} about future prices.

      \item \textbf{Consumer tastes and preferences}.

    \end{enumerate}

  \end{core}
    
  \begin{core}{Supply}

    \textbf{Supply} represents the quantity of a good that producers are willing and able to offer for sale at various prices. The \textbf{Law of Supply} indicates a direct relationship between price and quantity supplied: higher prices incentivize producers to increase production.

    \pgfig{
      \begin{axis}[

        \axispresetstwo
      ]

      \upslope{0}

      \pgfiglabel{10,8}{Supply}

      \end{axis} 
    }{Supply curve}{fig:supply}{0.75}

    Determinants of supply include:

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item \textbf{Production costs}, including input prices and technology.

      \item \textbf{The prices of related goods} firms could produce.

      \item \textbf{Expectations about future prices}.

      \item \textbf{The number of sellers} in the market.

      \item \textbf{Government policies}, such as taxes, subsidies, and regulations.

    \end{enumerate}

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}[label=core:market_eq]{Market Equilibrium}

    \textbf{Market Equilibrium} occurs where the supply and demand curves intersect. At this point, the quantity supplied equals the quantity demanded, and the market clears. The equilibrium price and quantity are determined automatically through the price mechanism without external intervention. Correspondingly, adjustments in equilibrium price occur when supply and demand curve shifts.

  \subsection{Elasticity}

    \textbf{Elasticity} measures how responsive one variable is to changes in another variable. In economics, elasticity quantifies the percentage change in one variable resulting from a one percent change in another variable.

    \begin{core}{Price Elasticity of Demand}

      \textbf{Price Elasticity of Demand} measures how much quantity demanded changes in response to changes in price and is defined by the following equation.

      \[
        E_D = \left| \frac{\%\Delta Q_d}{\%\Delta P} \right| = \left| \frac{\frac{Q_2 - Q_1}{Q_1}}{\frac{P_2 - P_1}{P_1}} \right|
      \]

      The following are the determinants of the price elasticity of demand.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Availability of substitutes}: More substitutes available means more elastic demand; more similar substitutes means more elastic demand.

        \item \textbf{Necessity versus luxury}: Necessities tend to have more inelastic demand.

        \item \textbf{Time frame}: Short-run demand tends to be less elastic than long-run demand.

        \item \textbf{Share of income}: Goods that represent a smaller share of income tend to have less elastic demand.

      \end{enumerate}

      The following are the terms economists use to describe elasticity.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Perfectly elastic} ($\text{coefficient} = ∞$): A horizontal demand curve---price increases lead to zero demand, price decreases lead to infinite demand.

        \pgfig{
          \begin{axis}[
            \econplot{Quantity}{Price}
            \axispresetstwo
          ]

          \horizontalslope{5}

          \pgfiglabel{5,6}{Demand}

          \end{axis} 
        }{Perfect elasticity}{fig:perfect_elasticity}{1}

        \item \textbf{Elastic} ($\text{coefficient} > 1$): Quantity demanded responds more than proportionally to price changes.

        \item \textbf{Unit elastic} ($\text{coefficient} = 1$): Quantity demanded responds proportionally to price changes.

        \pgfig{
          \begin{axis}[
            \econplot{Quantity}{Price}
            \axispresetstwo
          ]

          \downslope{0}

          \pgfiglabel{10,3}{Demand}

          \end{axis} 
        }{Unit elasticity}{fig:unit_elasticity}{1}

        \item \textbf{Inelastic} ($\text{coefficient} < 1$): Quantity demanded responds less than proportionally to price changes.

        \item \textbf{Perfectly inelastic} ($\text{coefficient} = 0$): A vertical demand curve—quantity demanded does not respond to price changes.

        \pgfig{
          \begin{axis}[
            \econplot{Quantity}{Price}
            \axispresetstwo
          ]

          \verticalslope{5}

          \pgfiglabel{7,5}{Demand}

          \end{axis} 
        }{Perfect inelasticity}{fig:perfect_inelasticity}{1}

      \end{itemize}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Total Revenue Test}

      The \textbf{total revenue test} is a method for determining elasticity without calculation:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item If total revenue decreases as price increases, demand is \textbf{elastic}.

        \item If total revenue remains constant as price changes, demand is \textbf{unit elastic}.

        \item If total revenue increases as price increases, demand is \textbf{inelastic}.

      \end{itemize}

      For a standard downward-sloping demand curve, elasticity varies along the curve: it is elastic in the upper-left portion, unit elastic at the midpoint, and inelastic in the lower-right portion.

    \begin{core}{Price Elasticity of Supply}

      \textbf{Price Elasticity of Supply} measures how much quantity supplied changes in response to changes in price:

      \[
        E_S = \frac{\%\Delta Q_s}{\%\Delta P} = \frac{\frac{Q_2 - Q_1}{Q_1}}{\frac{P_2 - P_1}{P_1}}
      \]

      The primary determinant of price elasticity of supply is \textbf{time frame}. Short-run supply tends to be less elastic because producers cannot easily adjust their production capacity, while long-run supply is more elastic as firms can adjust inputs and production processes.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Other Elasticities}

      \textbf{Income Elasticity of Demand} measures how quantity demanded responds to changes in consumer income.

      \[
        E_I = \frac{\%\Delta Q_{\text{demanded}}}{\%\Delta \text{Income}}
      \]

      \begin{itemize}

        \item If $E_I > 0$, the good is a \textbf{normal good} (\textit{demand increases with income}).

        \item If $E_I < 0$, the good is an \textbf{inferior good} (\textit{demand decreases with income}).

      \end{itemize}

        \begin{axis}[
          \econplot{Quantity}{Price}
          \axispresetstwo
        ]

        \downslope{-3}

      \textbf{Cross Elasticity of Demand} measures how quantity demanded of one good responds to price changes in another good:

      \[
        E_{XY} = \frac{\%\Delta Q_{\text{demanded of X}}}{\%\Delta P_Y}
      \]

      \begin{itemize}

        \item If $E_{XY} > 0$, X and Y are \textbf{substitutes} (\textit{price increase in Y increases demand for X}).

        \item If $E_{XY} < 0$, X and Y are \textbf{complementary goods} (\textit{price increase in Y decreases demand for X}).

      \end{itemize}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Surplus}
    
    \begin{core}{Consumer Surplus}

      \textbf{Consumer surplus} represents the benefit consumers receive when they pay less than their maximum willingness to pay. The demand curve can be interpreted as the \textbf{marginal benefit} consumers receive from each unit consumed, reflecting the law of diminishing marginal utility.

      \pgfig{
        \begin{axis}[
          \econplot{Quantity}{Price}
          \axispresetstwo
        ]

        \downslope{0}
        \pgfiglabel{10,2}{Demand}

        \pgfiglabel{10,8}{Supply}

        \end{axis} 
      }{Consumer surplus}{fig:csurplus}{1}

      At market equilibrium, consumers who value the good more than the equilibrium price receive surplus because they pay the equilibrium price despite being willing to pay more. The total consumer surplus is represented by the area between the demand curve and the equilibrium price line, thus may increase or decrease depending on the demand elasticity of the good or service

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Producer Surplus}

      \textbf{Producer surplus} represents the benefit producers receive when they sell at a price higher than their minimum acceptable price. The supply curve can be interpreted as the \textbf{marginal cost} of producing each additional unit.

      \pgfig{
        \begin{axis}[

        \end{axis} 
      }{Producer surplus}{fig:psurplus}{1}

      At prices above the market equilibrium, producers receive surplus because they sell at the equilibrium price despite being willing to accept less. The total producer surplus is represented by the area between the supply curve and the equilibrium price line, thus may increase or decrease depending on the supply elasticity of the good or service.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Allocative Efficiency}

      \textbf{Allocative efficiency} occurs when resources are distributed in a way that maximizes the total benefit to society. In the context of consumer and producer surplus, allocative efficiency is a state in which \textbf{consumer and producer surplus is maximized}.

    Certain market interventions and imperfections can cause markets to deviate from allocative efficiency, resulting in deadweight loss and reduced social welfare.

    \begin{core}{Government Intervention and Surplus}

      Governments can intervene directly in domestic markets through policies that alter equilibrium prices and quantities. 

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item A \textbf{price ceiling} is a government-mandated maximum price below the equilibrium. When binding, a price ceiling increases consumer surplus but decreases producer surplus, creating a \textbf{deadweight loss} due to reduced quantity exchanged.

\section{Production and Cost}

  In microeconomics, a \textbf{production function} represents the overarching relationship between \textbf{inputs} (\textit{factors of production}) and the resulting quantity of \textbf{output}:
  \[
    Q = f(L,K)
  \]

  where $L$ denotes labor (\textit{hours worked, number of employees, etc.}) and $K$ denotes capital (\textit{machinery, factory space, etc.}).
 
  \subsection{Product Measures}

    \begin{core}{Total, Marginal, and Average Product}

      Economists measure production output using three key metrics.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Total Product (TP)} refers to the total output produced.

        \item \textbf{Marginal Product (MP)} is the additional output gained by increasing one input by one unit.

        \item \textbf{Average Product (AP)} is the output per unit of input.

      \end{itemize}

      Here is a theoretical scenario of product as a function of input (\textit{workers}), assuming other inputs are fixed.

      \begin{table}[H]
        \centering
        \begin{tabular}{llll}
          \hline
          \hline
          \textbf{Workers} & \textbf{Total Product} & \textbf{Marginal Product} & \textbf{Average Product} \\
          \hline
          0 & 0 & --- & --- \\
          1 & 10 & 10 & 10 \\
          2 & 22 & 12 & 11 \\
          3 & 30 & 8 & 10 \\
          4 & 32 & 2 & 8 \\
          5 & 28 & -4 & 5.6 \\
          \hline
          \hline
        \end{tabular}
        \caption{Total, marginal, and average product}
        \label{tab:tamp}
      \end{table}

      When plotted against input quantity (\textit{e.g., the number of workers}), these metrics follow a characteristic shape governed by the \textbf{law of diminishing returns}: AP and MP are predominantly downward sloping, while TP eventually plateaus and declines. Notably, MP intersects AP exactly at AP's maximum point.

      \myfig{0.35}{microeconomics_figures/tamp.png}{Total, marginal, and average product}{fig:placeholder1}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Short Run}

    \begin{core}{Fixed and Variable Costs}

      At the foundational level, economists categorize costs into the following three general categories.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Fixed costs (FC)} remain constant regardless of output---such as rent---and persist even at zero production.

        \item \textbf{Variable costs (VC)} fluctuate with output level, including payments to workers and raw materials.

        \item \textbf{Total cost (TC)} is simply their sum, $TC = FC + VC$.

      \end{itemize}

      Note that economic analysis accounts for both explicit and implicit costs.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Short Run Cost Curves}

      In the short run, the following cost curves are central to analyzing firm behavior.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Marginal cost (MC)} measures the additional cost of producing one more unit, defined as $MC = \Delta TC / \Delta Q$.

        \item \textbf{Average total cost (ATC)} is total cost per unit of output, $ATC = TC/Q$.

        \item \textbf{Average variable cost (AVC)} and \textbf{average fixed cost (AFC)} disaggregate into their variable and fixed components respectively, $AVC = VC/Q$ and $AFC = FC/Q$. Note that $ATC = AVC + AFC$ at every level of output.

      \end{itemize}

      Again, consider the following theoretical situation.

      \begin{table}[H]
        \centering
        \begin{tabular}{llllll}
          \hline
          \hline
          \textbf{Output} & \textbf{TC} & \textbf{MC} & \textbf{ATC} & \textbf{AFC} & \textbf{AVC} \\
          \hline
          0   & \$5,000  & ---   & ---     & ---     & ---   \\
          100 & \$10,000 & \$50  & \$100   & \$50    & \$50  \\
          200 & \$14,000 & \$40  & \$70    & \$25    & \$45  \\
          300 & \$20,000 & \$60  & \$66.67 & \$16.67 & \$50  \\
          400 & \$29,000 & \$90  & \$72.50 & \$12.50 & \$60  \\
          500 & \$45,000 & \$160 & \$90    & \$10    & \$80  \\
          \hline
          \hline
        \end{tabular}
        \caption{Marginal, average total, average fixed, and average variable cost}
        \label{tab:matav}
      \end{table}

      The resulting plot of MV, ATC, AVC, and AFC exhibit predictable shapes also rooted in the \textbf{law of diminishing marginal returns}.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Both ATC and AVC are U-shaped}, initially falling as output rises and fixed costs are spread across more units, then rising as diminishing returns drive up marginal cost.

        \item \textbf{AFC declines continuously}, since fixed costs are distributed over an ever-larger quantity.

        \item MC, being directly tied to marginal returns, is also U-shaped and crucially \textbf{intersects both ATC and AVC at their respective minimum points}---a consequence of the relationship between marginal and average values.

      \end{enumerate}

      \myfig{0.4}{microeconomics_figures/matav.png}{Marginal, average total, average fixed, and average variable cost}{fig:placeholder2}

      In perfectly competitive markets (\autoref{core:perf_comp}), a firm faces a constant market price, making marginal revenue $MR = P$. The firm maximizes profit by producing at the quantity where $MR = MC$, since any unit where $MR > MC$ adds to profit, while any unit where $MR < MC$ erodes it.

      \myfig{0.45}{microeconomics_figures/matav2.png}{Profit maximization during perfect competition}{fig:placeholder3}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Long Run}

    \begin{core}{Long-Run Average Total Cost}

      In the long run, \textbf{all costs become variable} as firms are free to adjust every factor of production, including capital. The \textbf{Long-Run Average Total Cost (LRATC)} curve is constructed as the combination of many short-run average total cost (SRATC) curves, each corresponding to a different fixed level of capital (\textit{such as factory size})---representing the lowest attainable average cost at each output level.

      \myfig{0.51}{microeconomics_figures/lratc.png}{LRATC curve as a combination of SRATCs at differing factory sizes (\textit{capital levels})}{fig:placeholder4}

      As shown in \autoref{fig:placeholder4}, the LRATC curve is U-shaped. This characteristic shape reflects the follow three distinct phases of production scale.

      \myfig{0.53}{microeconomics_figures/lratc_es.png}{Economies and diseconomies of scale on the LRATC}{fig:placeholder5}

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item Initially, \textbf{economies of scale} cause LRATC to fall as output expands: larger operations benefit from greater worker specialization, more efficient use of capital, and bulk purchasing discounts. 

        \item Beyond a certain point, \textbf{diseconomies of scale} take hold and LRATC begins to rise, driven by managerial inefficiencies and coordination difficulties that emerge as firm size grows.

        \item The output level at which LRATC reaches its minimum---the transition between these two phases---is known as the \textbf{minimum efficient scale (MES)}, and represents the most productively efficient scale of operation.

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Returns to Scale}

      \textbf{Returns to scale} describe how output responds when \textit{all} inputs are scaled up proportionally in the long run. Formally, suppose all inputs are multiplied by some factor $\lambda > 1$. The production function $f(L, K)$ exhibits:

      \[
        f(\lambda L,\, \lambda K) \begin{cases} > \lambda Q & \text{increasing returns to scale} \\ 
        = \lambda Q & \text{constant returns to scale} \\ 
        < \lambda Q & \text{decreasing returns to scale} \end{cases}
      \]

      Consider, for example, increasing all inputs by 70\%, so $\lambda = 1.7$ and $Q' = f(1.7L,\, 1.7K)$.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Increasing Returns to Scale}: Output grows by a greater proportion than inputs. With $\lambda = 1.7$, output rises by more than 70\%---say, 80\%, so $Q' > 1.7Q$. This often arises from specialization gains and more efficient use of capital at larger scales.

        \item \textbf{Constant Returns to Scale}: Output grows in exact proportion to inputs. With $\lambda = 1.7$, output also rises by exactly 70\%, so $Q' = 1.7Q$. This implies that doubling all inputs exactly doubles output: $f(2L,\, 2K) = 2Q$.

        \item \textbf{Decreasing Returns to Scale}: Output grows by a smaller proportion than inputs. With $\lambda = 1.7$, output rises by less than 70\%---say, 60\%, so $Q' < 1.7Q$. This typically reflects coordination difficulties and resource constraints that emerge as scale increases.

      \end{itemize}

      \textit{Note that while returns to scale tends to be correlated with economies of scale, they do not exactly pertain to the same situation. Economies of scale can exist even without increasing returns to scale---for example, if input costs fall as you buy in bulk (purchasing power), average cost can fall even with constant returns to scale. \textbf{Returns to scale is purely about the physical input and output relationship, ignoring prices entirely.}}

    \end{core}

\section{Perfect Competition}
    
  \begin{core}[label=core:perf_comp]{Characteristics of Perfect Competition}

    \textbf{Perfect competition} is a market structure characterized by the following assumptions.

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item \textbf{Large number of firms}: No single firm controls a significant market share. If one producer decides to double or half their production, the total supply barely shifts. 

      \item \textbf{Price-taking behavior}: Each firm sells at the market price determined by industry supply and demand equilibrium (\autoref{core:market_eq}).

      \item \textbf{No barriers to entry or exit}: Firms can freely and easily enter or leave the market due to no patents, high start-up costs, or government regulations blocking new players. 

      \item \textbf{Identical products}: All firms sell the same product, meaning there is \textbf{no brand loyalty}, no product differentiation, and products across firms are \textbf{perfect substitutes} for each other.

    \end{enumerate}

    \myfig{0.7}{microeconomics_figures/pc.png}{Market supply and demand equilibrium (\textit{left}) setting price for individual firmMR curve (\textit{right}); \textit{notice the difference output scale between the market and firm}}{fig:placeholder6}

    As shown in \autoref{fig:placeholder6}, the individual firm's marginal revenue curve is \textbf{perfectly elastic} (\textit{horizontal}) at the market price, meaning that if a individual firm raises its price above market price, quantity demanded falls to zero and that the firm can sell any quantity at the market price.

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Firm Entry and Exit}

    The decision for any individual firm to enter or exit a perfectly competitive market depends on profitability categorized into the following scenarios.

    \begin{itemize}

      \item If \textbf{P > ATC}, individual firms earn \textbf{economic profit}, attracting new firms to enter over time until price is pushed down due to the influx of market supply from more producers.

      \myfig{0.42}{microeconomics_figures/fee1.png}{Price is greater than average total cost}{fig:placeholder7}

      \item If \textbf{P = ATC$_{\text{\textbf{min}}}$}, firms earn \textbf{normal profit} (\textit{zero economic profit}), which is the equilibrium state where firms neither enter nor leave. \textit{Mind that accounting profit would be still be positive in this scenario.}

      \myfig{0.42}{microeconomics_figures/fee2.png}{Price is equal to minimum average total cost}{fig:placeholder8}

      \item If \textbf{AVC$_{\text{\textbf{min}}}$ < P < ATC$_{\text{\textbf{min}}}$} firms \textbf{incur losses} due to high initial fixed cost but \textbf{continue producing in the short run} because they still make an economic profit with every unit in the context of AVC, which helps to cover a portion of the fixed costs, but not all.

      \myfig{0.42}{microeconomics_figures/fee3.png}{Price lies above minimum average variable cost but below average total cost}{fig:placeholder9}

      \item If \textbf{P < AVC$_{\text{\textbf{min}}}$}, firms shut down immediately as the producer incurs loss with every unit of production.

      \myfig{0.42}{microeconomics_figures/fee4.png}{Price lies below minimum average variable cost; \textit{the loss area shown only refers to the fixed cost portion which should be the only loss for an economically rational producer because any production would simply lead to greater total loss}}{fig:placeholder10}

    \end{itemize}

    In the long-run, a perfectly competitive market price would converge to where \textbf{P = ATC$_{\text{\textbf{min}}}$}, leading to all individuals firms operating at \textbf{normal profit} or \textbf{zero economic profit}. This equilibrium state is said to be \textbf{productively efficient} because goods are produced at the minimum ATC.

    If the market is not at equilibrium within the short run, new firms will enter and leave accordingly to price levels to minimum average total cost as discussed in the above scenarios.

  \end{core}

\section{Imperfect Competition}

  \begin{core}{Imperfect Competition}

    \textbf{Imperfect competition} describes a market structure where individual firms possess a non-negligible market share, granting them a degree of \textbf{price-setting power}. Unlike the price-taking firms of perfect competition, these entities face downward-sloping demand curves; consequently, they must lower their prices to increase the volume of output sold. The following are the primary subcategories of imperfect competition.

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item \textbf{Monopoly}: A single firm selling a product with no close substitutes. High barriers to entry allow the firm to be the sole price setter.

      \item \textbf{Monopolistic Competition}: \textbf{Many} firms selling \textbf{differentiated} products with \textbf{low barriers to entry}. \textit{Examples include restaurants and clothing brands like Nike, Adidas, and Under Armour.}

      \item \textbf{Oligopoly}: A \textbf{small} number of firms selling identical or differentiated products. Characterized by \textbf{interdependence} and \textbf{high barriers to entry}. \textit{Examples include smartphone manufacturers (Apple, Samsung).}

    \end{enumerate}

    \textbf{Barriers to entry} are structural or legal obstacles that prevent new firms from entering an industry. These are primarily comprised of economies of scale, patents and legal statutes, prohibitive start-up costs, and exclusive ownership of key resources, such as critical commodities.

  \end{core}

  \subsection{Monopolistic Competition}

    \begin{core}{Monopolistic Competition}

      \textbf{Monopolistic competition} features many firms selling \textbf{differentiated} products with low barriers to entry, such as restaurants. Additionally, like monopolies, these firms produce where $\text{MR} = \text{MC}$ and set price on the demand curve.

    \begin{core}{Long Run Equilibrium}

      In the long run equilibrium, new firms enter until economic profit equals zero, which is until when the demand curve is \textbf{tangent} to ATC at the quantity where $\text{MR} = \text{MC}$.

      \myfig{0.35}{microeconomics_figures/lr_mono_comp.png}{Long run monopolistic competition}{fig:placeholder13}

      Monopolistically competitive markets are \textbf{not productively efficient} because they produce below minimum ATC (\textit{leaving excess capacity}) and \textbf{not allocatively efficient} because price exceeds marginal cost.

      \textit{It's important to keep in mind that this long run equilibrium state is more a long run tendency rather than a state of dynamic equilibrium because, in reality, business entities may achieve a long run economic profit due to factors outside of competitors reach, such as brand loyalty or business secrets, blurring the line between monopolistic competition and monopolies. }

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Oligopoly}

    \begin{core}{Oligopoly}

      An \textbf{oligopoly} refers to a market with a \textbf{small number of firms} with \textbf{high barriers to entry} and where firms are \textbf{interdependent}, meaning each firm's decisions significantly affect competitors.

      \textit{Smartphone companies such as Apple, Google, Samsung, etc. is a nice example of oligopolies on all levels of its supply chain. Soda beverage companies such as Coca Cola and Pepsi are also good examples in the context of global logistical capabilities but becomes more of a monopolisitic competition in the context of a local beverage market.}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Cartels}

      A \textbf{cartel} is a group of firms that \textbf{collude} to act like a single monopolist, restricting output and raising prices. \textbf{Antitrust laws} often restrict or prohibit cartels. However, individual firms have \textbf{incentives to cheat on agreements to increase their own profits} (\textit{the basis of this behavior is explained in \autoref{core:payoff}}).

    \end{core}

\section{Game Theory: (A Brief Lesson)}

  \textbf{Game theory} analyzes strategic interactions where outcomes depend on all players' decisions.

  \begin{core}[label=core:payoff]{Payoff Tables}

    \textbf{Payoff tables} show outcomes for each combination of \textbf{strategies}---a courses of action participants can take. The following is a payoff table of two firms choosing between high and low prices.

    \begin{table}[H]
      \centering
      \begin{tabular}{c|c|c|}
        & \textbf{Firm B Low} & \textbf{Firm B High} \\
        \hline
        \textbf{Firm A Low} & (80, 80) & (130, 50) \\
        \hline
        \textbf{Firm A High} & (50, 130) & (100, 100) \\
      \end{tabular}
      \caption{Firm A and Firm B payoff table; \textit{the first and second value in each pair corresponds to Firm B and Firm A gains}}
      \label{tab:payoff}
    \end{table}

    \textbf{Dominant strategies} yield a better outcome regardless of the choice the other participant makes. In the scenario presented in \autoref{tab:payoff}, the dominant strategy for both firms would be to \textbf{set a high price} because it provides the superior gain when looking at each choice of the other firm in isolation.

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Nash Equilibrium}

    A \textbf{Nash equilibrium} occurs when neither participant can improve its outcome by changing strategy, assuming the other participant doesn't change. This results in neither player having an incentive to deviate unilaterally, which is the case presented in \autoref{tab:payoff}.

    \textit{It's important to note that not all scenarios possess a Nash equilibrium.}

  \end{core}

\section{Factor Markets}

  \textbf{Factor markets} study the demand and supply for the factors of production, namely labor.

  \begin{core}{Factor Demand}

    \textbf{Factor demand} is the quantity of a factor (\textit{e.g., labor}) that firms are willing to purchase at each price (\textit{e.g., wage}). Factor demand is a \textbf{derived demand}---it does not exist for its own sake but stems directly from the demand for the final good or service that the factor helps produce. \textit{A rise in demand for housing, for instance, pulls up the demand for construction workers.} As a result, factor demand shifts in response to the following determinants.

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item \textbf{Price of the end product:} since factor demand is derived from product demand, any change in the price of the final good ripples back to the factor market.

      \item \textbf{Productivity of the factor:} a more productive factor generates more output per unit, increasing its marginal revenue product and thus the firm's willingness to hire it at any given price.

      \item \textbf{Prices of substitute and complementary resources:} factors of production can be substitutes or complements for one another.

      \textit{Namely, the general rise of and falling cost of AI and automation---a close substitute to human labor in relevant fields---shifts the demand for human labor leftwards.}

    \end{enumerate}

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Factor Supply}

    \textbf{Factor supply} is the quantity of a factor that owners are willing to offer at each price. It shifts in response to the following determinants.

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item \textbf{Workforce size:} a larger population of workers eligible to supply a given factor shifts supply rightward, placing downward pressure on wages.

      \item \textbf{Education and training:} factors that require specialized skills are in limited supply by nature. As more workers acquire the necessary qualifications, supply expands and the skill premium narrows.

      \item \textbf{Alternative options available:} if returns in an alternative occupation rise, workers may reallocate toward it, reducing supply in the original market. Factor supply in any given market is always shaped by the opportunity cost of not supplying elsewhere.

      \item \textbf{Preferences for leisure:} at sufficiently high wages, workers may choose to work fewer hours rather than more---prioritizing leisure over additional income. This produces the characteristic \textbf{backward-bending labor supply curve}, where supply eventually turns leftward as the wage rises.

      \myfig{0.45}{microeconomics_figures/backward_supply.png}{Backward-bending labor supply curve}{fig:placeholder14}

    \end{enumerate}

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Marginal Revenue Product and Marginal Resource Cost}

    \textbf{Marginal Revenue Product (MRP)} is the additional revenue generated by employing one more unit of a factor. In a perfectly competitive product market, $\text{MRP} = \text{MP} \times \text{P}$ because the price (\textit{marginal revenue}) curve is horizontal.

    \textbf{Marginal Resource Cost (MRC)} is the additional cost of employing one more unit of a factor. In a perfectly competitive \textbf{factor} market, MRC simply equals the factor price and is \textbf{constant}.

    To maximize profit, firms produce and employ factors at the quantity where $\text{MRP} = \text{MRC}$.

\section{Government Intervention}

  \begin{core}{Market Failure}

    \textbf{Market failure} occurs when market forces fail to produce the allocatively efficient quantity of output. This can result from externalities, public goods, imperfect competition, or information asymmetries.

    % Market Failure: When market forces fail to produce the allocatively efficient quantity of output

  \end{core}

  \begin{core}{Government Intervention Methods}

    The government can intervene in markets through various mechanisms:

    \begin{enumerate}

      \item \textbf{Lump-sum tax/subsidy}: Does not affect marginal benefit, marginal cost, or profit-maximizing output; only affects fixed costs.

      \item \textbf{Per-unit tax/subsidy}: Affects either marginal benefit or marginal cost, thereby changing profit-maximizing output. Taxes decrease output; subsidies increase output.

      \item \textbf{Price ceiling/floor}: When binding, affects profit-maximizing output.

      \item \textbf{Regulations}: Including antitrust laws to promote competition.

    \end{enumerate}

    % • Per-Unit Tax/Subsidy: Affects either MB or MC and therefore profit-maximizing output
    % * Taxes decrease output; Subsidies increase output
    % • Price Ceiling/Floor: If binding, affects profit-maximizing output
    % • Imposing Regulations
    %   * Antitrust Laws

  \end{core}

  \subsection{Externalities}

    An \textbf{externality} occurs when individuals not involved in a transaction are affected by it.

    \begin{core}{Positive Externalities}

      A \textbf{positive externality} exists when a transaction generates benefits for third parties. Example: Streetlights improve driver safety for all road users, not just those who pay for them.

      % * Positive Externality (Example: Streetlights ensure driver safety at night)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Negative Externalities}

      A \textbf{negative externality} exists when a transaction imposes costs on third parties. Example: Factory pollution affects the health of nearby residents.

      % * Negative Externality (Example: A factory releases pollution)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Marginal Social Cost and Benefit}

      Previously, our marginal cost and benefit curves represented only \textbf{private} costs and benefits. In reality, externalities create \textbf{social} costs and benefits.

      \textbf{Marginal Social Cost (MSC)} = Marginal Private Cost + Marginal External Cost

      \textbf{Marginal Social Benefit (MSB)} = Marginal Private Benefit + Marginal External Benefit

      For negative externalities, the government should impose a per-unit tax equal to the marginal external cost to shift marginal private cost upward and reduce output to the allocatively efficient level.

      For positive externalities, the government should provide a per-unit subsidy equal to the marginal external benefit to shift marginal private benefit upward and increase output to the allocatively efficient level.

      % The graphs of marginal cost and marginal benefit we've seen so far have been referring to only private costs and benefits. In reality there could be a greater cost due to negative externaltiies (marginal social cost) or greater benefits due to positive externalities (marginal social benefit)
      % Negative externality: Government should tax per unit to shift MPC up and reduce output to allocatively efficient amount
      % Positive externality: Government should subsidize per unit to shift MPB up and increase output to allocatively efficient amount

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Monopoly Interventions}

    \begin{core}{General Monopoly Intervention}

      To address monopoly inefficiency, the government can:

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item Impose a \textbf{price ceiling} where demand equals marginal cost, achieving the socially optimal quantity.

        \item Provide a \textbf{per-unit subsidy} so that marginal revenue equals marginal cost at the allocatively efficient quantity.

      \end{enumerate}

      % Socially optimal: ceiling at price where D = MC
      % OR
      % Socially optimal: subsidize per unit so that MR = MC at allocatively efficient quantity

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Natural Monopoly Price Ceilings}

      Without regulation, a natural monopoly produces where MR = MC and charges the price on the demand curve.

      \textbf{Fair return pricing}: Price ceiling where demand equals ATC, allowing the firm to earn normal profit.

      \textbf{Socially optimal pricing}: Price ceiling where demand equals MC, maximizing total economic surplus but causing the firm to incur an economic loss. The government can provide a lump-sum subsidy to offset this loss.

      % Effect of Price Ceilings on Natural Monopolies
      % Without Price Ceiling: Firm produces at quantity where MR = MC and sells at D curve price for that quantity
      % Fair return: ceilign price where D = ATC; firm makes a normal profit
      % Socially optimal: ceiling at price where D = MC; total economic surplus is maximized, but firm produces at an economic loss
      %   solution: provide a lump sum subsisty to offset the loss

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Public Goods}

    \begin{core}{Types of Goods}

      Goods can be classified by two characteristics:

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Rivalrous}: One person's consumption reduces availability for others.

        \item \textbf{Excludable}: People can be prevented from consuming the good.

      \end{enumerate}

        \begin{tabular}{c|c|c|}
          & \textbf{Rivalrous} & \textbf{Non-Rivalrous} \\
          \hline
          \textbf{Excludable} & Private Goods & Club Goods \\
          & (Clothing, Coffee) & (Netflix, Gyms) \\
          \hline
          \textbf{Non-Excludable} & Common Resources & Public Goods \\
          & (Fish in ocean) & (National Defense) \\
        \end{tabular}
      \end{center}

    \begin{core}{Public Goods Issues}

      \textbf{Tragedy of the Commons}: People overconsume common goods because they don't bear the full cost of consumption. Example: Overfishing depletes fish stocks.

      Solution: Government limits use through permits or regulations.

      \textbf{Free Rider Problem}: People benefit from non-excludable goods without paying for them.

      Solution: Government funds public goods through taxation.

  \subsection{Income Equality}

    \begin{core}{Lorenz Curve and Gini Coefficient}

      The \textbf{Lorenz Curve} graphs the cumulative share of income against the cumulative share of population.

      The \textbf{Gini Coefficient} (G) is a single number summarizing the Lorenz Curve, measuring the area between the line of perfect equality and the actual Lorenz Curve:

      \[
        0 \leq G \leq 1 \quad \text{or} \quad 0\% \leq G \leq 100\%
      \]

      \begin{itemize}

        \item G = 0: Perfect equality (everyone has same income)

        \item G = 1: Perfect inequality (one person holds all income)

      \end{itemize}

      % Lorenz Curve: a graph that shows the cumulative share of income earned by the cumulative share of population. 
      % Gini Coefficient: single number that summarizes the Lorenz Curve. It measures the area between the "Line of Perfect Equality" and the actual "Lorenz Curve." 
      %   0 <= G <= 1
      %   0% <= G <= 100%
      %   G = 0 means perfect equality (everyone has the same amount of income)
      %   G = 1 means perfect inequality (one person hold all the income)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Government Interventions to Reduce Inequality}

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Progressive taxes}: Higher income = higher tax percentage. Reduces inequality.

        \item \textbf{Proportional taxes}: Fixed tax percentage. Does not affect income distribution.

        \item \textbf{Regressive taxes}: Lower income = higher tax percentage. Increases inequality.

        \item \textbf{Transfer payments}: Direct payments to lower-income individuals reduce inequality.

        \item \textbf{Minimum wage and anti-discrimination laws}: Can also reduce inequality.

      \end{enumerate}

      % Transfer payments to people with lower income reduces inequality
      % Minimum wage and anti-discrimination laws can also reduce inequality

    \end{core}
