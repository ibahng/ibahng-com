\section{Commodity Trading Mechanics}

  \subsection{Market Structure \& Price}

    \begin{core}{Market Structure}

      % ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
      % Many factors are taken into consideration when determining the price of a commodity. Variations of quality, grade, and location must all be taken into consideration.

      % For oil, hundreds of types of crude are traded worldwide and so the handful of futures contracts that exist are not always suitable for those wanting to purchase physical oil. With this, commodity trading remains a largely bilateral business, bringing buyers and sellers together in over-the-counter (OTC) deals.

      % ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
      % With all this to consider, how does the market determine reference prices?
      % Price Reporting Agencies such as Platts and Argus provide price assessments for a wide range of spot and derivative oil contracts.
      % These assessments provide transparency and most physical OTC transactions are set at an agreed differential to a Platts or Argus benchmark.

      % There is no singular global market price for crude. Instead, regional markets exist each with their own benchmarks. Under normal market conditions the price differentials between each regional market is limited. If an arbitrage becomes too great, the supply and demand pressures adjust price differentials again as they are exploited.
      % With hundreds of crude grades that exist, there is not a financial future for every grade. As such, traders can use benchmarks to also hedge the risk and exposure on OTC contracts.

      % ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
      % * Producers, consumers, and traders of physical oil can use the financial benchmarks to hedge risks and exposures on OTC contracts. Price reporting agencies like Platts and Argus provide a price assessment for a wide range of oil types and contracts
      % * The main reasons why commodity trading happens are:
      %   * Safe haven during economic uncertainty
      %   * Inflation hedging
      %   * Diversification of portfolio
      %   * Speculation
      % * Like any other financial market, market sentiment can impact price. This phenomenon has become more pronounced with the increasing financialization of the commodities markets, as more people trade benchmark crude oils

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Price Drivers}

      Weather \& Natural Disasters
      % ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
      % * Mother Nature
      %   * EX. UK gas prices rose as heating demand increased during a cold wave

      Geopolitical Events
      % ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
      % * Trade War
      %   * As no country is completely self-sufficient, each country relies heavily on both commodity imports and exports. Any large disruption of supply and demand to any key importing or exporting country can have adverse effects on the global economy.
      %   * EX. China-US trade war 2018
      % * Saudi Attacks
      %   * On Sept 14, 2019, two major oil facilities in Saudi Arabia were attacked, causing them to shut down, knocking out approximately 5% of global supply at the time

      Policy \& Regulation
      % ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
      % * Policy
      %   * Some policies can increase demands in certain types of commodities that can also affect profits of commodity refining firms depending on if their equipment limits them or not
      %   * EX. International Maritime Organization mandates all vessels required to burn fuels with 0.5% sulfur or less

      Demand Elasticity
      % ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
      % Demand tends to be more inelastic as people consider commodities a necessity. The economic slow-down will have an impact but slowly. There are exceptions however.
      % Coronavirus quickly crippled a third of global demand for fuel and other such commodities.
      % During this time, the market entered a "super contango" (the opposite being "backwardation"); meaning the spot price of oil was trading dramatically below the futures price.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Financialization of Commodities}

    \begin{core}{Historical Evolution}

      % ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
      % In previous modules, we looked at how fundamentals can drive commodity prices. Like any traded market, other factors such as market sentiment can also impact price. This phenomenon has become more pronounced with oil becoming increasingly financialized over the past few decades with an ever-increasing number of non-traditional market participants trading oil.
      % A popular view is that the oil price increase between 2003-2008 cannot be explained by the economic fundamentals and was instead facilitated by the financialization of oil futures markets (volume 20-30x greater; <$10B → $450B in April 2011); this transformed these real markets into financial markets.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Market Sentiment \& Speculation}

      % Speculation is buying low to sell high later. When a futures contract looks favorable, an increased demand for it increases price.
      % In the week leading up to the sudden price drop of the May WT1 contract in 2020, investors kept piling investments into oil ETFs, trying to call the bottom in expectation of the market rebounding after the COVID slowdown.
      % A sudden inflow of investors into oil ETFs and the nature of ETFs rolling over their positions for futures contracts from one month to another opened an unusually wide spread. Many feel that it contributed to market mayhem and pushed crude prices to below zero.

    \end{core}

      % * Commodities such as gold are often used as hedges during periods of global economic uncertainty because they tend to retain their value even during economic turbulence
      % * Diversification b/c of little correlation w/ equities and fixed income assets

    \begin{core}{Speculator vs Investor}

      % ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
      % Feature | Speculator | Investor
      % ---|---|---
      % Time Horizon | Shorter | Longer
      % Trade Frequency | Trade more | Trade less

    % ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ SUMMARY
    % * Corporations include producers and consumers of commodities
    % * Producers of commodities have exposure to price decreases and hedge the risk by buying instruments to lock in the highest selling price possible

    % * Trading houses act as intermediaries between producers and consumers, moving raw materials from their place of production to their place of consumption

      % Oil that is originally extracted from the ground is referred to as crude oil. The refinery process converts raw crude oil into usable oil products such as diesel oil, jet fuel, and gasoline.
      % Crude oil is classified by its American Petroleum Institute gravity, or API gravity → density, sulfur content.
      % Higher API = lighter crude = better (more expensive).
      % Refineries differ by the type of crude oil they can refine and what end products (crude slate) they create.
      % The profit margin from oil production and refining is called "the refinery margin".
      % Fundamentals such as storage, transportation, and variations in quality provide insight into the supply and demand structure of each commodity market.
      % Demand for oil is forecasted to grow in the long term, mostly due to increasing demand in developing countries.
      % OPEC was founded in the 1960s to monitor the stability and prices of oil markets to secure an efficient and regular supply of petroleum to consumers.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Supply Dynamics}

    \begin{core}{Seven Sisters \& Oil Majors}

      % From 1940's to 1970's, a group of seven American and British companies controlled 85% of the world's petroleum reserves (vertical integration, cartel operations) → Seven Sisters.
      % by the 1970s, this monopolistic position eroded due to both the emergence of an intergovernmental organization called OPEC and oil producing nations, especially in the Middle East, asserting national sovereignty over their natural resources.
      % The new power players → "new Seven Sisters".
      % Surviving companies of the original seven are BP, Chevron, ExxonMobil, and Royal Dutch-Shell, often referred to as the oil majors.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC)}

      % OPEC (Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries)
      % Founded in the 1960s to monitor the stability and prices of oil markets to secure an efficient and regular supply of petroleum to consumers.
      % Initially, OPEC members emulated the oil majors and set prices directly, but they soon switched to market-based methods affecting price by varying production.
      % In addition to tracking production levels, the spare capacity, which is capacity—production is also monitored (in the case of an oil shortage).

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Strategic Petroleum Reserves}

      % OPEC controlled half of global crude production and 80% of proven reserve. With low demand elasticity, this allowed them to exert a high degree of control.
      % In fear of this, countries created strategic petroleum reserves (SPRs) and crude oil inventories (or stockpiles) in order to provide economic and national security.

    \end{core}
