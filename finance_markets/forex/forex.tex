% Currency Market Mechanics
% ===========================

% At the wake of the “Bretton Woods Agreement,” all allies agreed to lock
% their currencies to the US dollar, which was in turn convertible to gold
% (The Gold Standard).

% As the US economy stagnated during the Vietnam War, President Nixon
% suspended this gold convertibility, beginning the modern fiat currency
% regime.

% Today, more than $5 trillion is transacted each day in the FOREX market.

% The currency market operates 24 hours a day during the business week:
%     Tokyo → London → New York

% The majority of currency transactions are conducted by:
%     - Financial investors (institutional investors)
%     - Corporations
%     - Travellers/tourists


% Pegged Currencies
% ---------------------------

% These are currencies that are linked, or pegged, to another country’s
% currency value.

% Example:
%     - Hong Kong dollar (HKD) is pegged to the US dollar (USD)

% One of the key mechanisms governments use to maintain currency pegs is
% foreign exchange (FX) reserves:
%     - Reserve cash used to manipulate the supply and demand of a currency
%     - This helps stabilize or maintain its value

% Once a pegged currency fails to maintain its peg:
%     - Any unpaid foreign debt becomes exceedingly difficult to repay


% Floating Currencies
% ---------------------------

% These are currencies that are not pegged and fluctuate freely in the
% global currency market.

% Exchange rates are expressed in terms of how much of one currency is
% needed to purchase another currency.


% Triangular Arbitrage
% ---------------------------

% This process keeps the currency matrix coordinated and prevents traders
% from profiting off pricing inconsistencies between currencies.

% When converting from one low-volume currency to another:
%     - Investors often transact through the US dollar

% Approximately 85% of all FX volumes involve the US dollar.


% Summary: Currency Market Mechanics
% ===================================

% - Over $5 trillion of currencies are traded every day
% - 1971 marked the dawn of the modern fiat currency market
% - Several countries peg their currencies to other currencies
% - Locked exchange rates are government aspirations, not guarantees
% - Floating currencies move relative to one another in a currency matrix
% - The US dollar is the world’s reserve currency and the most heavily
%   traded currency


% Currency Valuation
% ===========================

% Observing Currency Strength
% ---------------------------

% Overall strength or weakness of a currency is determined using
% “trade-weighted baskets”:
%     - Indices that calculate the aggregate value of one currency against
%       its major trading partners
%     - Larger trading partners are weighted more heavily


% The “Law of One Price”
% ---------------------------

% In the long run:
%     - Identical goods and services should cost the same regardless of
%       where they are sold


% Three Main Drivers of Currency Value
% --------------------------------------

% 1. Surprise changes in interest rates
% 2. Surprise changes in inflation
% 3. Surprise changes in trade


% Changes in Interest Rates
% ---------------------------

% All else being equal:
%     - Surprise increases in interest rates generally strengthen a
%       currency

% When a central bank unexpectedly decreases interest rates:
%     - Government bond yields fall
%     - Global investment becomes less attractive
%     - Demand for that country’s currency decreases
%     - The currency typically weakens


% Changes in Inflation
% ---------------------------

% The law of supply and demand applies to money:
%     - Excess money supply contributes to inflation

% When the money supply of one currency expands more rapidly than another:
%     - The first currency generally depreciates relative to the second


% Changes in Trade
% ---------------------------

% Exports:
%     - Lead to currency appreciation
%     - Foreign buyers must exchange their currency for the exporter’s
%       currency
%     - This increases demand for the home currency

% Imports:
%     - Tend to depreciate the importing country’s currency
%     - The opposite mechanism applies


% Summary: Currency Valuation
% ===================================

% - Currency value is relative, not absolute
% - Trade-weighted baskets measure overall currency strength and weakness
% - In the long run, the “Law of One Price” influences currency values
% - In the short run, three major drivers are:
%     1. Surprise changes in interest rates
%     2. Surprise changes in inflation
%     3. Surprise changes in trade


% Central Banks and Currencies
% ===============================

% Interest rate expectations and inflation expectations are strongly
% influenced by central bankers through monetary policy and rate-setting
% agendas.

% The principal goal of central banks is to guard against inflation
% (and, more rarely, deflation).

% Typical inflation target:
%     - Approximately 2%

% Low but positive inflation is generally considered beneficial for
% economic growth.

% Inflation targeting is partly psychological:
%     - Once expectations of rising prices become ingrained, they are
%       difficult to reverse


% Inflationary Cycle
% ---------------------------

% Sometimes central banks must take extreme measures to break an
% inflationary cycle:
%     - Rising wages lead to rising prices
%     - Rising prices lead to demands for even higher wages

% Workers expect price increases -> Demand for higher pay -> Companies raise wages -> companies lift prices -> CYCLE

% Deflationary Cycle
% ---------------------------

% Deflation can also become self-reinforcing:
%     - Consumers delay purchases expecting lower future prices
%     - Reduced demand leads to layoffs and slower economic activity

% Prices decline -> consumers defer purchases to await lower prices -> company revenues decline -> companies let go of workers to cut costs -> CYCLE


% Summary: Central Banks and Currencies
% ========================================

% - Central bankers strongly influence currency markets
% - The standard inflation target for industrialized nations is around 2%
% - Inflation can create a vicious cycle of wage and price increases
% - Deflation can create a vicious cycle of delayed spending and layoffs


% Currency Risk
% ===========================

% Currency movements can significantly impact:
%     - Investment returns
%     - Corporate financial planning

% Example:
%     TOPIX (Japanese stock index):
%         888.51 → 1301.29 in 2013

%     Yen per US dollar:
%         86.75 → 105.26 in 2013
%         (The yen weakened against the dollar)

% Suppose you invested:
%     - $1 million USD into TOPIX
%     - Equivalent to approximately 87 million yen

% By the end of 2013:
%     - TOPIX increased by 46%
%     - However, your return in USD terms was only about 21%
%     - This occurred because the yen weakened relative to the dollar


% Hedging Currency Risk
% ---------------------------

% Investors can lock in currency exposure for long periods using the
% currency forward market.

% Example:
%     Spot price:
%         $1.36 per euro

%     10-year forward offer:
%         $1.52 per euro

% Interpretation:
%     - The market expects the dollar to weaken against the euro
%     - You benefit if the actual exchange rate exceeds $1.52 per euro
%       after 10 years
%     - This allows you to purchase euros at a cheaper predetermined rate


% Gold: The Ancient Currency
% ===============================

% Gold is valuable primarily because it cannot be replicated at the whim
% of governments.

% Gold is commonly viewed as:
%     - An inflation hedge
%     - A store of value during financial crises

% Example:
%     - Gold reached historic highs during the Greek debt crisis of 2012

% Since 1971:
%     - Gold has generally been a better store of value than paper currency


% Summary: Currency Risk
% ===================================

% - Currency movements can significantly affect corporations and investors
% - Historical volatility and exchange-rate forecasts help assess risk
% - Forward agreements allow investors to hedge or speculate on currencies
% - Gold’s scarcity and inability to be freely printed have helped it
%   retain long-term value
