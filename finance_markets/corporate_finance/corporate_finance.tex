      A business has many stakeholders
      Shareholders invest in equity & own company
      Banks & bondholders lend to the company
      Employees help make the products & services that the company sells
      Corporate managers make decisions on what to invest in, how much debt to take & how much cash to return to shareholders

      Society receives side benefits and bears the side costs of corporate actions
      Customers pay for & receive benefits from company’s products & services

      We put shareholders at the top of the business because they have a residual claim on the cash flows

      A narrower objective of a company is to maximize stockholder wealth. When the stock is traded and markets are viewed to be efficient, the objective is to maximize the stock price

      If the company is traded, the stock price gets chosen as the optimizing metric because:
      Stock price is easily observable and constantly updated
      If investors are rational, stock prices reflect the wisdom of decisions, short-term and long-term, instantaneously

      Employees are often stockholders in many firms

    \begin{core}{Managerial Conflict of Interest}

      When managers do not fear stockholders (the main drivers), they will often put their interests over stockholder interests. Examples…
      Greenmail: The (managers of) target of a hostile takeover buy out the potential acquirer’s existing stake, at a price much greater than the price paid by the raider, in return for the signing of a ‘standstill’ agreement. The managers are essentially using shareholder money in order to prevent a takeover which will increase stock value through the price premium the acquirer offers.
      Golden Parachutes: Provisions in employment contracts, that allows for the payment of a lump-sum or cash flows over a period, if managers covered by these contracts lose their jobs in a takeover. Guaranteed individual exit strategy at the price of the shareholders.
      Poison Pills: A security, the rights of cash flows on which are triggered by an outside event, generally a hostile takeover, is called a poison pill
      Greenmail, Golden Parachutes, and Poison Pills are all legal and DO NOT REQUIRE BOARD APPROVAL
      Shark Repellents: Anti-takeover amendments are also aimed at dissuading hostile takeovers but differ on very important counts. They require the assent of stockholders to be instituted
      Overpaying on takeovers: Acquisitions often are driven by management interests rather than stockholder interests

      The Kodak acquisition of Sterling Pharmaceuticals is a perfect example of deals leading to value destruction

      We need to ask ourselves “Who owns/runs your firm?”
      Who are the top stockholders in your firm?
      What are the potential conflicts of interests that you see emerging from this stockholding structure?


      Case 1: Splintering of Stockholders Disney’s top stock holders in 2003 (all passive, institutional investors)
      Case 2: Voting versus Non-voting Shares & Golden Shares: Vale (skewed decision making motives)
      Case 3: Cross and Pyramid Holdings Tata Motor’s top stockholders in 2013 (bigger interest for the group rather than a single part of the conglomerate)
      Case 4: Legal rights and Corporate Structure: Baidu (Chinese government may inhibit the power the shareholders have for at least the China sector of the shell company)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Stockholder vs. Bondholder Objectives}

      Stockholders’ objectives vs Bondholders’ objectives
      In theory: there is no conflict of interests between stockholders and bondholders
      In practice: Stockholders and bondholders have different objectives. Bondholders are concerned most about safety and ensuring that they get paid their claims. Stockholders are more likely to think about upside potential

      Examples of conflict:
      Dividend / buyback surge: When firms pay cash out as dividends, lenders to the firm are hurt and stockholder may be helped because the firm becomes riskier without the cash
      Risk shifting: When a firm takes riskier projects than those agreed to at the outset, lenders are hurt. Lenders base interest rates on their perceptions of how risky a firm’s investments are. If stockholders then take on riskier investments, lenders will be hurt.
      Borrowing more on the same assets: If lenders do not protect themselves (loan covenants) a firm can borrow more money and make all existing lenders worse off.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Market Inefficiencies}

      In theory, financial markets are efficient and the stock price is a good indicator of a company’s performance. In practice, there are many holes in the market
      Information can be managed to show negative news in the best (perhaps manipulative) light
      Firms tend to release negative EPS and DPS data on Fridays before the market closes in hopes of investors forgetting about it over the weekend
      Outright fraud can be performed with the incentive of short term benefits 

      Investor irrationality: The base argument is that investors are irrational, and prices often move for no reason at all. As a consequence, prices are much more volatile than justified by the underlying fundamentals. Earnings and dividends are much less volatile than stock prices
      Overreaction or underreaction to news
      An insider conspiracy
      Short termism (investors are short-sighted)

      Are markets short term? Some counter (albeit not conclusive) evidence that they are not…
      Value of young firms
      Current earnings vs Future growth
      Positive market reaction to investments

    \end{core}

  \subsection{The End Game}

    \begin{core}{The Corporate Life Cycle}

      The best CEO of a company depends on the period within the corporate life cycle the firm is in. At first, it is a visionary, then a pragmatist, then a builder, then an opportunist, then a defender, then a liquidator at the end of a corporate life cycle.

      In the case of a tech company, its life cycle is characterized as a compressed version of a non-tech life cycle which are usually more elongated.
      The very thing that allows tech companies to scale up so quickly is the thing that allows competitors to overcome them in scale and success

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{The Financial Response}

      The Financial Market Response
      Companies can mislead investors for long periods, leading stock prices away from value and skewing capital allocation across firms. (Firms that mislead have access to more capital than they should...)
      Analysts, for the most part, seem to be ineffective at uncovering these “problems”, sometimes because of tunnel vision and sometimes because of biases.
      As investor access to information improves, it is becoming much more difficult for firms to control when and how information gets out to markets.
      If there are ways of trading on over valuation, the payoff to uncovering negative information about companies rises, and there will be an incentive on the part of investors to uncover the truth.
      No matter what, the truth eventually does come out, and when it does:
      The punishment is not only quick, but it is savage. Stock prices drop, as markets reset.
      The management of the company loses credibility making it difficult for the company to find its way back to health.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{The Societal Response}

      The SOCIETAL RESPONSE
      If firms consistently flout societal norms and create large social costs, the governmental response (especially in democracy) is for laws and regulations to be passed against such behavior (tobacco companies, marijuana companies, alcohol companies)
      Even if government and regulators do not act, a company that deliberately flouts societal norms and acquires a reputation as a bad company can pay a price

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Constrained Corporatism}

      Constrained Corporatism: The winning companies are the ones that find a way to maximize shareholder wealth, while being good corporate citizens, protecting employee interests and delivering good value to customers.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Modified Objective Functions}
      
      The Modified Objective Functions
      For publicly traded firms in reasonably efficient markets, where bondholders (lenders) are protected:
      Maximize Stock Price: This will also maximize firm value
      For publicly traded firms in inefficient markets, where bond holders are protected:
      Maximize stockholder wealth: This will maximize firm value, but might not maximize the stock price
      For publicly traded firms in inefficient markets, where bondholders are not fully protected
      Maximize firm value, though stockholder wealth and stock prices may not be maximized at the same point (ripping off bondholders cannot go long term)
      For private firms, maximize stockholder wealth (if lenders are protected) or firm value (if they are not)

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Risk and CAPM}
  
    \begin{core}{Hurdle Rate \& Risk Models}

      Since financial resources are finite, there is a hurdle that projects have to cross before being deemed acceptable. This hurdle should be higher for riskier projects than for safer projects.
      A simple representation of the hurdle rate is as follows:

      Hurdle Rate=Riskless Rate+Risk Premium

      How do you measure risk?
      How do you translate this risk measure into a risk premium?

      Risk is when danger and opportunity converge and is therefore neither good nor bad. It is just a fact of life. The question that businesses have to address is therefore not down to avoid risk but how to best incorporate it into their decision making.

      A good risk and return model should…
      measure the risk that applies to all assets
      delineate what types of risk are rewarded and what are not
      standardized risk measures
      translate the measure of risk into a rate of return
      work well 

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Capital Asset Pricing Model}

      The CAPM
      variance of actual returns around an expected return
      only the non-diversifiable portion that is rewarded
      non-diversifiable risk with beta
      beta into expected return
      Works as well as the next best alternative

      The Mean-Variance Framework
      The variance on any investment measures the disparity between actual and expected returns

      In risk and return models in finance, we start with the marginal investor (trades and owns a lot of stock) that is well diversified
      While most company’s marginal investors are institutional investors, some can have individual, small investors as the marginal investor
      If the marginal investor of your firm is diversified, you are on safer grounds

      The big assumption in a diversified market portfolio is that there are no marginal transaction costs for diversifying and all assets can be traded
      Individual investors will adjust for risk by adjusting their allocations to this market portfolio and a riskless asset (T-bill)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Limitations of CAPM}

      Limitation of the CAPM
      The model makes unrealistic assumptions
      The parameters of the model cannot be estimated precisely
      The market index used can be wrong
      the firm may have changed during the ‘estimation’ period
      The model does not work perfectly

      The CAPM, however, is the best we have in comparison to other models (Arbitrage Pricing Model, Multi-Factor Model, Proxy Models) in measuring a company’s risk because the other models are either the same with a higher degree of complexity or incorporates past data mining to generate conclusions (which uses hindsight bias) 

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Risk-Free Rates}

    \begin{core}{Risk-Free Rates}

      Risk-Free Rates
      The risk free rate is the rate on a zero coupon default-free bond matching the time horizon of the cash flow being analyzed
      Theoretically, this translates into using different risk free rates for each cash flow—the 1 year rate for the CF in year 1, the 2 year rate for the CF in year 2…
      Practically speaking, if there is substantial uncertainty about expected cash flows, the present value effect of using time varying risk free rates is small enough that it may not be worth it
      In corporate finance, almost everything we do is long term. So, using a long-term default free rate as the risk free rate makes sense.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Government Rates \& Default Risk}

      A risk-free rate is always matched to a currency.

      If the government is default free, using a long term government rate as the risk free rate on all the CFs in a long term analysis will be close to the true value. For short term analysis, it is entirely appropriate to use short term rates.
      When trying to find the risk free rate of, say, the euro, you would see the risk free rates of all the euro using countries in the world and just pick the lowest one because if it is the lowest, then it must be (with greater certainty) the risk free rates of all the other euro using countries

      If the government has default risk, adjust the local government borrowing rate by the sovereign default spread (from ratings agencies) for default risk to get a riskless local currency rate
      The bond yield of the currency already accounts for the default risk of the country because it is market traded, we need to subtract the additional yield due to default risk to get the riskless rate of the country
      When you don’t trust the currency of your firm’s country, (when the currency has no parameters either because it’s hyper inflation or nothing traded long term in that currency), then use a different currency (usually the US dollar)
      (The only difference between currencies is the inflation of each) Do your analysis in real terms, in which case the risk free rate has to be a real risk free rate. TIPS (Inflation protected bonds) is a measure of a real risk free rate

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Determining Default Spreads}

      Paths to get a currency’s default spread
      Sovereign dollar or euro denominated bonds: The difference between the rate on a sovereign US bond issued by the country and the US T-bond rate can be used as the default spread.
      For example if the Brazil sovereign US bond is 4.5% and the US T-bond rate is 2%, the default spread would be 2.5%
      CDS spreads: Obtain the default spreads for sovereigns in the credit default swap market.
      For example if the Brazil bond is 4.5% and the CDS insurance premium is 1.5%, the default spread would be 1.5% because then 3% is default free.
      Average spread: This is the spread generate by ratings agencies

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Equity Risk Premiums}

    \begin{core}{Equity Risk Premiums}

      Equity Risk Premium
      As a general proposition about ERP, this premium should be
      Greater than zero
      Increase with the risk aversion of the investors in that market
      Increase with the riskiness of the ‘average’ risk investment
      If so, it also follows that equity risk premiums should change over time, as economic circumstances change and investor composition also changes

      Risk aversion varies across people and time

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Estimating ERPs in Practice}

      Estimating Risk Premiums in Practice
      Survey Premium: Survey investors on their desired risk premiums and use the average premium from these surveys (nobody really uses this method in finance)
      there are no constraints on reasonability
      more reflective of the past
      tend to be short term
      Historical Premiums: Assume that the actual premium delivered over long time periods is equal to the expected premium - i.e., use historical data
      Assumes that the systematic view towards risk has not changed over time
      No matter which estimate you use, recognize that it is backward looking, is noisy and may reflect selection bias
      Implied Premiums: Estimate a forward-looking premium, based upon today’s asset prices
      If you know the price paid for an asset and have estimates of the expected cash flows on the asset, you can estimate the IRR of these cash flows. If you paid the price, this is your expected return

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{ERPs for Non-US Markets}

      If you can observe what investors are willing to pay for stocks, you can back out an expected return from that price and an implied equity risk premium

      An ERP and its resultant cost of equity is representative of a hurdle rate that you’re trying to beat as a firm with the IRR of a project that you are pursuing

      For bonds, you essential sum up the current US risk free rate with the country’s credit rating spread OR the CDS spread (ratings agencies then to be lagging to the CDS spreads however because the spreads are not chosen through market supply and demand) to get the cost of bond loans

      ERP for other markets
      Historical data for markets outside the US is available for much shorter time periods. The problem is even greater in emerging markets
      The historical premiums that emerge from this data reflects this data problem and there is much greater error associated with the estimates of the premiums
      For emerging markets, we will use the melded default spread approach (where default spreads are scaled up to reflect additional equity risk) to come up with the additional risk premium that we will add to the mature market premium

      Country Risk Premium=Country Default Spread(EquityCountry Risk)

      To get the ERP of a non-US market, you would multiply the country default spread by how much more equity volatility is above the country bond volatility (effectively scaling up the default spread of the country to account for equity)

      Step 1: Estimate an equity risk premium for a mature market. If your preference is for a forward looking, updated number, you can estimate an implied equity risk premium for the US (assuming that you buy into the contention that it is a mature market)
      Assume that the implied ERP for the US is 5.5%
      Step 2: Come up with a generic and measurable definition of a mature market
      Assume that all AAA rated countries are mature markets and have an ERP of 5.5%
      Step 3:Estimate the additional risk premium that you will charge for markets that are not mature. You have two choices
      The default spread for the country, estimated based either on sovereign ratings or the CDS market
      A scaled-up default spread, where you adjust the default spread upwards for the additional risk in equity markets

      The conventional practice on ERP is to calculate the ERP based on where the company is incorporated

      The more sensible practice is to calculate a weighted ERP based upon the geographic distribution of the firm’s revenues

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Beta and Performance}

    \begin{core}{Stock Return Regression}

      The standard procedure for estimating betas is to regress stock returns (Rj) against market return (Rm) and the slope of the regression corresponds to the beta of the stock.

      Rj=a+bRm

      The R squared of the regression provides an estimate of the proportion of the risk (variance) of a firm that can be attributed to market risk. The balance (1-R^2) can be attributed to firm specific risk
      REMINDER: R squared is a measure of how well a regression fits into the data set (1 means a perfect fit)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Jensen's Alpha}

      Estimating Performance
      The intercept of the regression provides a simple measure of performance during the period of the regression, relative to the CAPM (side note: R_f is the risk free rate)
      Rj=Rf+b(Rm-Rf)
         =Rf(1-b)+bRm                CAPM
      Rj=a+bRm                             Regression Equation
      If…
      a > R_f(1-b)  Stock did better than expected during regression period
      a = R_f(1-b)  Stock did as well as expected during regression period
      a < R_f(1-b)  Stock did worse than expected during regression period
      The difference between the intercept and Rf(1-b) is Jensen’s alpha. If it is positive, your stock did perform better than expected during the period of the regression
      The alpha is a risk adjusted, market adjusted measure of performance

      More on Jensen’s Alpha
      If you did the alpha analysis on every stock, the Jensen’s alpha should average out to zero because the standard is the stock market (market adjusted)
      To get a more accurate version of Jensen’s alpha for a company, you may want to find the Jensen’s alpha that is also sector adjusted to account for macroeconomic shifts that may affect only one sector particularly
      However, Jensen’s alpha is never a clear indicator to invest in a stock, thinking that it’ll produce money in the future

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Beta}

      Setting up for the Estimation
      Decide on an estimation period
      Services use periods ranging from 2 to 5 years for the regression
      Longer estimation period provides more data, but firms change
      Shorter periods can be affected more easily by significant firm-specific event that occurred during the period
      Decide on a return interval - daily, weekly, monthly
      Shorter intervals yield more observations, but suffer from more noise
      Noise is created by stocks not trading and biases all betas towards one
      Estimate returns (including dividends) on stock
      Return=(PriceEnd-PriceBeginning+DividendsPeriod)/PriceBeginning
      Included dividends only in ex-dividend month
      choose a market index, and estimate returns (inclusive of dividends) on the index for each interval for the period (check out MSCI for index services)

      Key points when analyzing a firm’s beta
      Always look at betas through a range generated from standard errors

\section{Investment Analysis}

  \subsection{Cash Flow Measurement}

    \begin{core}{Accrual Accounting \& Earnings Measurement}

      Under \textbf{accrual accounting}, revenues are recorded when products and services are sold or provided, not when payment is received. Expenses are matched to the revenues they help generate, rather than when cash is paid out. This creates a systematic gap between accounting earnings and actual cash flows, which must be reconciled when evaluating investments.

      % Principles Governing Accounting Earnings Measurement
      % Accrual Accounting: How revenues when products and services are sold or provided, not when they are paid for. Show expenses associated with these revenues rather than cash expenses

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Operating Expenses vs.\ Capital Expenditures}

      Not all costs are treated equally under accrual accounting. \textbf{Operating expenses} are costs associated with generating revenue in the current period and are fully expensed immediately. \textbf{Capital expenditures (CapEx)} create benefits over multiple periods and are therefore written off gradually over time through depreciation and amortization (D\&A), rather than expensed all at once.

      % Operating vs CapEx: Only expenses associated with creating revenues in the current period should be treated as operating expenses. Expenses that create benefits over several periods are written off over multiple periods (as D&A)
      % To get from earnings to FREE cash flows, you have to:
      % add back non cash expense (like D&A)
      % subtract out cash outflows which are not expensed (such as Capex)
      % consider changes in WC

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Depreciation \& Its Tax Effect}

      Because CapEx is capitalized rather than immediately expensed, the cost is recovered over time through \textbf{depreciation}. Depreciation is a non-cash expense that reduces taxable income, generating a tax shield each period. The magnitude of this benefit depends on the depreciation method used:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Straight-line depreciation:} spreads the cost evenly over the asset's useful life

        \item \textbf{Accelerated depreciation:} front-loads the expense, generating larger tax benefits earlier

      \end{itemize}

      While the total tax benefit is identical under both methods, the time value of money makes accelerated depreciation preferable — earlier benefits are worth more than later ones.

      % Depreciation gives a tax benefit because it reduces taxable income and is a non-cash expense
      % the magnitude of tax benefit differs depending on the method of depreciation carried out
      % Straight line depreciation
      % Accelerated depreciation

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{The CapEx Effect}

      CapEx is a cash outflow, but unlike operating expenses, it is \textit{not} treated as an accounting expense in the period it occurs. This means earnings overstate the true cash position of the firm whenever investment is taking place. CapEx falls into two categories:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{New CapEx:} investment in the creation of new assets

        \item \textbf{Maintenance CapEx:} investment to sustain existing assets

      \end{itemize}

      Maintenance CapEx grows as a project ages — a 25-year project requires substantially more maintenance investment than a 2-year one. This means every project eventually reaches a point where the marginal cost of maintenance makes continuation unviable.

      % Capex Effect
      % Capex are not treaded as accounting expenses but they are cash outflows
      % Categories
      % New capex for the creation of new assets
      % Maintenance capex for existing assets
      % The need for maintenance capital expenditures will increase with the life of the project. In other words, a 25-year project will require more maintenance capex than a 2-year project (thus a project will always have a point where it needs to end because the marginal capex to maintain a project gets too high)

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{The Working Capital Effect}

      \textbf{Working capital (WC)} — money tied up in inventory, accounts receivable, and similar items — cannot be deployed elsewhere. It therefore represents a drain on cash flows that does not show up as an accounting expense. Unless an interest return is earned on working capital, it functions as a \textit{totally wasting asset}: capital is consumed without generating a direct return.

      % The WC Effect
      %   Intuitively, money invested in inventory or in accounts receivable cannot be used elsewhere. It, thus represents a drain on cash flows
      %   Working capital (if you do not capture an interest revenue on it) represents a totally wasting asset

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:fcf]{From Earnings to Free Cash Flow}

      Reconciling accounting earnings to \textbf{free cash flow (FCF)} requires three adjustments to account for the distortions introduced by accrual accounting:

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item Add back non-cash expenses (D\&A)

        \item Subtract capital expenditures (CapEx)

        \item Account for changes in working capital ($\Delta$WC)

      \end{enumerate}

      \[
        \text{FCF} = \text{NOPAT} + \text{D\&A} - \text{CapEx} - \Delta\text{WC}
      \]

      Or equivalently at the firm level:
      \[
        \text{FCF} = \text{Operating Cash Flow} - \text{Capital Expenditures}
      \]

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Current Expense vs.\ Capital Expense}
      
      When a cost can be either immediately expensed or capitalized, the choice has meaningful implications for both reported earnings and cash flows. \textbf{Current expensing} is generally more favorable from a tax perspective: the full tax benefit is received upfront, whereas capitalizing a cost spreads the benefit over time through depreciation. Although the total tax saving is the same under both treatments, the time value of money means the immediate benefit of current expensing is worth more.

  \subsection{Time-Weighted Incremental Cash Flows}

    \begin{core}{The Return Mantra}

      The guiding principle of investment analysis rests on three requirements for measuring returns correctly:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item Use \textbf{cash flows} rather than earnings, especially when the two diverge significantly

        \item Use \textbf{incremental} cash flows — only those that occur as a direct consequence of the investment decision, not total firm cash flows

        \item Use \textbf{time-weighted} returns — cash flows that occur earlier are worth more than those that occur later

      \end{itemize}

      These three requirements are summarized in the return mantra: \textit{``Time-weighted, Incremental Cash Flow Return.''}

      % Use cash flows rather than earnings, especially when earnings differ from cash flows greatly
      % Use "incremental" cash flows relating to the investment decision, i.e., cashflows that occur as a consequence of the decision, rather than total cash flows
      % Use "time weighted" returns, i.e., value cash flows that occur earlier more than cash flows that occur later
      % The Return Mantra: "Time weighted, Incremental Cash Flow Return"

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{What Counts as an Investment?}

      An investment or project is broader than it may initially appear. The spectrum ranges from large strategic decisions to small operational ones:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item Major decisions to enter new markets or lines of business

        \item Acquisitions of other firms, which are projects despite often being treated separately

        \item Decisions on how best to deliver a service necessary for the business

      \end{itemize}

      Put broadly, every choice a firm makes can be framed as an investment, and should be evaluated as one.

      % Setting the table: What is an investment/project?
      % An investment/project can range the spectrum from big to small, money making to cost saving
      % Major strategic decisions to enter new areas of business or new markets
      % Acquisitions of other firms are projects as well, notwithstanding attempts to create separate sets of rule for them
      % Decisions on how best to deliver a service that is necessary for the business to run smoothly
      % Put in broader terms, every choice made by a firm can be framed as an investment

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:incremental]{Incremental Cash Flows}

      The test for whether a cash flow is \textbf{incremental} is simple: ask \textit{``what happens if I carry out the project?''} and \textit{``what happens if I don't?''} If the answer is the same in both cases, the item is \textit{not} incremental and must be excluded. When computing FCF, the following non-incremental items must be adjusted out:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item Pre-project investments (sunk costs)

        \item Pre-project depreciation $\times$ tax rate

        \item The non-incremental portion of allocated G\&A costs

      \end{itemize}

      Alternatively, one can arrive at incremental cash flows directly by simply beginning the calculation with only incremental items from the outset.

      % Incremental cash flows
      % By "incremental item", we ask "what happens if I carry out the project" and "what happens if I don't carry out the project". If the answer is the same, it is NOT an incremental item.
      % We need to adjust out FCF by subtracting out any non incremental tax items
      % Pre-project investment (sunk costs)
      % Pre-project depreciation * tax rate
      % Portion of G&A that is nonincremental
      % You can also just get incremental cash flows by simply starting with incremental items in the cash flow calculation

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Sunk Costs}

      A \textbf{sunk cost} is any expenditure that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered, regardless of whether a project is accepted or rejected. Classic examples include test marketing costs for a consumer product and R\&D expenses for a pharmaceutical drug. Because sunk costs do not change with the investment decision, they fail the incremental test from \autoref{core:incremental} and must be excluded from project analysis entirely.

      % Sunk Costs
      % What is a sunk cost? Any expenditure that has already been incurred and cannot be recovered (even if a project is rejected) is called a sunk cost.
      % A test market for a consumer product and R&D expenses for a drug (for a pharma company) would be good examples
      % Sunk cost rule: When analyzing a project, sunk costs should not be considered since they are not incremental

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Allocated Costs (G\&A)}

      Firms often allocate costs from a centralized pool — such as general and administrative (G\&A) expenses — to individual projects based on some project characteristic, typically sales. However, to the extent these costs would exist regardless of whether the project is undertaken, they are \textit{not} incremental. Including non-incremental allocated costs penalizes the project unfairly. Only the portion of allocated costs that genuinely increases as a result of the project should appear in the analysis.

      % Allocated Costs (G&A)
      % Firms allocate costs to individual projects from a centralized pool (like G&A) based upon some characteristic of the project (usually sales)
      % To the degree that these costs are not incremental (and would exist anyway), this makes the firm worse off
      % Thus, it is only the incremental component of allocated costs that should show up in project analysis

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Time-Weighting Cash Flows}

      Incremental cash flows occurring at different points in time cannot simply be summed — a dollar received today is worth more than a dollar received in the future. To account for this, cash flows must be moved through time using either:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Discounting:} bringing future cash flows back to their present value

        \item \textbf{Compounding:} projecting present cash flows forward to a future value

      \end{itemize}

      The result of discounting all incremental cash flows back to the present and summing them is the \textbf{Net Present Value (NPV)} of the investment, which forms the basis of the decision rules covered in \autoref{sub:decisionrules}.

      % Incremental cash flows across time cannot be added up simply; they must be brought to its respective net present value
      % This process of moving cash flows through time is
      % discounting, future to present
      % compounding, present to future

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Project Structure \& Assumptions}

    \begin{core}{Building a Project Model}

      A financial estimation of a project is built upon a structured set of assumptions. The typical modeling flow is as follows:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Initial CapEx:} an upfront investment in the construction or acquisition of a cash flow-generating asset

        \item \textbf{Revenue estimates:} based on comparable past projects with adjustments for specific conditions surrounding the current one. When no comparable exists — as is the case with genuinely novel endeavors like AI — estimation becomes extremely difficult

        \item \textbf{Expense assumptions:} modeled as a percentage of revenue, separated into operating expenses and G\&A. Associating G\&A to a specific project is particularly difficult as these costs are rarely traceable to a single cash-producing asset

        \item \textbf{Working capital:} assumed as a percentage of revenue, with investments made at the end of each year and not subject to depreciation

        \item \textbf{Marginal tax rate:} must be chosen carefully. In early construction years, negative earnings can generate tax losses that provide benefits against profitable years in the future

      \end{itemize}

      % At the end of the day, a financial estimation is made upon a projects specs
      % A initial capex investment for the construction or building of a cash flow inducing asset is made
      % The revenue estimates of the project is made based on similar projects done in the past (if the endeavor is the first ever in the world, it is extremely difficult to make estimates; for example, this is the case with AI) with some minor adjustments according to the specific conditions surrounding the project
      % The expense assumptions are assumed to be a percentage of total revenue projections in most projects and can be separated to operating and general administrative (G&A) expenses with percentages of revenue for each
      % Here associating a G&A cost to a project is difficult because the costs are usually difficult to trace back to the cash producing asset
      % Within the operation of the asset, there is working capital always circulating and is also assumed as a percentage of revenue, with the investments being made at the end of each year (this is not depreciated)
      % The marginal tax rate must also be chosen
      % at the earlier years when the asset is under construction, earnings will be negative, and can thus provide tax benefits for profitable years in the future

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Asset Book Value \& Invested Capital}

      Over the life of a project, the book value of the fixed asset changes through two forces: \textbf{depreciation}, which reduces it each period, and \textbf{capital maintenance expenditures}, which partially offset that decline. Depreciation is typically modeled as a percentage of total revenues, while maintenance CapEx is modeled as a percentage of depreciation. Maintenance CapEx is low in the early years when the asset is new, but grows steadily as the asset ages.

      Adding the depreciated value of pre-project investments, the fixed asset, and working capital together yields \textbf{total invested capital}. This figure can be taken at year-end or averaged across the year to smooth the assumption that investment occurs gradually throughout the period.

      % After adding the value of the preproject investments and the fixed asset (both depreciated accordingly) and working capital, you reach total capital
      % You can either use the total capital at the end of the year or an average of the capital to smooth out your assumption of investment throughout the course of a year in your return on capital calculation
      % This asset will change in book value over time through [1] depreciation and [2] capital maintenance expenditures (depreciation is based upon a percentage of total revenues and the additional capex is based upon a percentage of the depreciation)
      % The capital maintenance expenditures are low in the early years, when the asset is still new but increase as the asset ages

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:roc]{Return on Capital \& Return Spread}

      The \textbf{after-tax return on capital (ROC)} measures how efficiently invested capital generates operating profit:

      \[
        \text{After-tax ROC} = \frac{\text{EBIT}(1 - t)}{\text{(BV of Debt + BV of Equity - Cash)}_{\text{previous year}}}
      \]

      From this, the \textbf{return spread} is computed as:

      \[
        \text{Return Spread} = \text{After-tax ROC} - \text{Cost of Capital}
      \]

      A positive spread indicates value creation. When evaluating the spread, the key follow-up questions are: \textit{why} did the firm earn this spread, and \textit{will it persist} into the future?

      % With the outputted return rate (After tax ROC)
      % After tax ROC=EBIT(1-tax rate)/(BV of debt+BV of equity-cash)previous year
      % You compute the return spread earned by the firm which is the (After tax ROC - Cost of Capital)
      % follow up by examining why the company earned that spread and evaluate whether the company will continue to earn similar excess returns in the future

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Accounting Consistency \& Common Pitfalls}

      Maintaining consistent accounting assumptions throughout a project model is critical. Two common pitfalls to avoid:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Depreciation timing:} depreciation on a CapEx investment begins in the year \textit{after} the investment is made. There is no depreciation charge in the same year as the CapEx outflow

        \item \textbf{Start vs.\ end of year:} if figures are assumed to occur at year-end, then any item stated as a start-of-year assumption effectively belongs to the prior year. This is a common error in working capital modeling, where investments are sometimes incorrectly stated as occurring at the start of the year

      \end{itemize}

      Additionally, when a project ends, any difference between the \textbf{salvage value} of assets and their \textbf{book value} triggers a tax consequence — a capital gain or loss — that must be explicitly accounted for in the final year cash flows.

      % If there is an undershoot or overshoot of project-end asset salvage value in comparison to the assets book value, taxes get involved which complicates the model more because you need to account for a tax cost or benefit in the case of a capital gain or loss.
      % Depreciation would be inserted into a year AFTER the corresponding capex investment is made, thus there would NOT be any depreciation for a given capex investment in the year that it was made.
      % Start of the year and the end of the year are two very different things. You have to keep accounting assumptions consistent; if the assumption is figures are accounted for at the end of a year. A start of the year assumption for the same year would mean that figure would belong to the end of the year before. This is a mistake made in the Working capital section which stated that the working capital investment is made at the start of the year.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Assumptions for an Infinite Project Life}

      When a project is modeled beyond its assumed finite life, additional assumptions are required. The extended life need not go into true perpetuity — it may simply extend 20 or 30 years further — but several adjustments must be made:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item Depreciation periods for CapEx investments must be recalibrated for the longer life

        \item Capital maintenance expenditures must be inflation-adjusted

        \item General costs such as advertising and working capital growth persist and must be extended accordingly

        \item In a perpetual project, CapEx must \textit{at minimum} equal depreciation — if depreciation exceeds CapEx indefinitely, the asset's book value eventually reaches zero, leaving nothing left to depreciate

      \end{itemize}

      The \textbf{terminal value} in this context can be estimated as the cash flow in the final explicit year divided by the spread between the cost of capital and the inflation rate. Extending project life involves a trade-off: earlier period cash flows are reduced by higher maintenance CapEx, in exchange for a larger terminal value.

      % Other general costs and changes like advertising expense extension and working capital growth will also persist with the extended life.

    \begin{core}{Cross-Border Projects: Exchange Rate \& Political Risk}

      When a project is located in a foreign country, exchange rate and political risk are often cited as reasons to demand a higher return. However, these risks should be treated as \textbf{diversifiable} — and therefore not warrant a premium — when either of the following conditions holds:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item The firm has projects across many countries, diversifying country-specific risk at the portfolio level

        \item The firm's investors are globally diversified, meaning they can diversify away country risk themselves

      \end{itemize}

      Furthermore, country-specific risk is already embedded in the \textbf{equity risk premium (ERP)} used to compute the cost of equity for foreign investments, so adding an additional spread would double-count it. On the debt side, \textbf{no spread is added} to the cost of debt for foreign projects because debt is issued against the firm as a whole from its home country — it is not project-specific.

    \begin{core}{Salvage Value \& Terminal Value}

      At the close of a project, the final cash flows must account for the residual value of assets:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Salvage value:} the proceeds from selling off all assets at the end of the project's life. It is commonly set equal to the book value of fixed assets and working capital

        \item \textbf{Terminal value:} used when a project is assumed to have an infinite life, producing cash flows indefinitely. It is computed as:
        \[
          \text{Terminal Value} = \frac{\text{CF}_{\text{final year}}}{\text{Cost of Capital} - g}
        \]

      \end{itemize}

      Terminal value warrants close scrutiny — it can inadvertently incorporate intangible assets such as intellectual property and patent rights that are not actually part of the fixed asset base being modeled.

    \begin{core}[label=core:npvirr]{NPV and IRR: Core Decision Rules}

      The two primary DCF-based decision rules are:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Net Present Value (NPV):} the sum of the present values of all project cash flows, including the initial investment. Represents value in absolute terms.
        \[
          \text{Accept if NPV} > 0
        \]

        \item \textbf{Internal Rate of Return (IRR):} the discount rate at which NPV equals zero. Represents value in comparable, percentage terms.
        \[
          \text{Accept if IRR} > \text{hurdle rate}
        \]

      \end{itemize}

      In theory, a firm should always accept all projects with a positive NPV, and the firm's total market capitalization would equal the sum of the NPVs of all its individual projects.

    \begin{core}{NPV vs.\ IRR: Reinvestment Rate \& Sensitivity}

      Although NPV and IRR usually agree, they differ in one critical assumption: what happens to intermediate cash flows.

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{NPV} assumes intermediate cash flows are reinvested at the \textit{hurdle rate} — the return available on comparable-risk investments

        \item \textbf{IRR} assumes intermediate cash flows are reinvested at the \textit{IRR itself} — implicitly assuming an infinite stream of equally profitable projects

      \end{itemize}

      When the IRR is high and the project life is long, the IRR will overstate the true return because the reinvestment assumption becomes increasingly unrealistic. NPV is also more sensitive to rate changes for longer-duration projects: a rise in the risk-free rate increases the cost of capital on both the debt and equity side, causing later cash flows to be more heavily discounted relative to earlier ones.

    \begin{core}{Comparing Decision Rules}

      Several decision rules exist for evaluating investments, each with different strengths:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{ROE / ROC:} accounting-based returns; simple but can be distorted by book value changes over time

        \item \textbf{Payback / Discounted Payback:} measures how quickly invested capital is recovered; useful as a risk screen but ignores cash flows beyond the payback period

        \item \textbf{NPV:} theoretically superior; measures absolute value creation but requires a reliable hurdle rate

        \item \textbf{IRR:} expresses returns in percentage terms, making comparison intuitive, but can be misleading due to reinvestment assumptions (see \autoref{core:npvirr})

        \item \textbf{Profitability Index (PI):} $\text{PI} = \text{NPV} / \text{Initial Investment}$; converts NPV into a relative measure, useful under capital rationing

      \end{itemize}

      In practice, accounting returns such as ROE and ROC have trended toward greater prominence in corporate decision-making, though NPV and IRR remain theoretically superior in absolute terms.

        \item Less is more: not every variable warrants sensitivity testing

        \item The firm faces \textbf{capital rationing constraints} that prevent allocating capital to multiple projects simultaneously

    \begin{core}[label=core:npvirrconflict]{When NPV and IRR Conflict}

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Case 1 — Multiple IRRs:} when a project's cash flows change sign more than once, multiple IRRs are mathematically possible. Since there is always one unique NPV, defer to the higher NPV

          \item Firms with \textit{limited capital access} (e.g., emerging market firms where cost of capital is high and equity issuance is costly) may prefer the higher IRR project
          \item Firms with \textit{little capital restraint} should always prefer the higher NPV. Note that the binding constraint need not be financial — talent capital can equally prevent pursuit of a larger project
        \end{itemize}

    \begin{core}{Capital Rationing \& the Profitability Index}

      Capital rationing arises from several sources:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item Debt limits imposed by outside agreements (covenants)

        \item Debt limits placed by external lenders or rating agencies

        \item Internal management caps on borrowing

        \item Restrictive policies on retained earnings

        \item Maintenance of a target EPS or P/E ratio

      \end{itemize}

      When capital is constrained, the NPV rule has a shortcoming: it measures value in absolute dollar terms, inherently favouring larger projects. To compare projects on a level footing under rationing, NPV is converted into a relative measure — the \textbf{Profitability Index (PI)}:

      \[
        \text{PI} = \frac{\text{NPV}}{\text{Initial Investment}}
      \]

      A higher PI indicates more value created per dollar of capital deployed, making it the appropriate ranking tool when capital is scarce.

    \begin{core}{The Reinvestment Rate Problem \& Adjusted IRR}

      The reinvestment rate assumption is the root cause of most NPV--IRR conflicts (see \autoref{core:npvirrconflict}):

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{NPV} assumes intermediate cash flows are reinvested at the \textit{hurdle rate} — the return available on comparable-risk investments

        \item \textbf{IRR} assumes intermediate cash flows are reinvested at the \textit{IRR itself}, implicitly requiring an infinite stream of equally profitable projects

      \end{itemize}

      When the IRR is high and the project life is long, the IRR will materially overstate the true return. The fix is to compute an \textbf{adjusted IRR}:

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item Compound all intermediate cash flows forward to the end of the project at the hurdle rate

        \item Calculate the IRR using only the initial outflow and this compounded terminal sum

      \end{enumerate}

    \begin{core}{Opportunity Costs}

      An \textbf{opportunity cost} arises when a project uses a resource that the firm already owns. Because the resource has alternative uses, it must be priced at its \textit{next best alternative}, even if no cash was spent to acquire it. The three most common forms are:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Sale of the asset:} the opportunity cost is the expected after-tax proceeds from selling it, net of any capital gains taxes

        \item \textbf{Renting or leasing:} the opportunity cost is the present value of the after-tax rental or lease revenues foregone

        \item \textbf{Use elsewhere in the business:} the opportunity cost is the cost of replacing the resource for its current use

      \end{itemize}

      The opportunity cost must always be subtracted from the project's cash flows to account for the loss of potential gains. Three illustrative cases:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Case 1 (Disney):} land already owned by the firm that could be sold — the foregone sale proceeds are the opportunity cost of using it for a new project

        \item \textbf{Case 2 (Bookscape):} a general manager reassigned to a new project triggers a salary rise for a replacement, and storage leased for financial data represents a real incremental cost

        \item \textbf{Case 3 (Excess capacity):} using spare capacity for a new project is only truly free if that capacity usage does not grow over time and does not accelerate the need to invest in additional capacity

      \end{itemize}

    \begin{core}{Hedging: When Does It Make Sense?}

      Whether a firm should hedge depends critically on its ownership structure, debt load, and the nature of the risk being hedged.

      For \textbf{private companies}, hedging output prices — especially for commodities — is generally beneficial. It reduces revenue volatility and provides greater cash flow predictability, which matters when the firm cannot offload risk to diversified public market investors.

      For \textbf{publicly traded companies}, hedging commodity prices is typically value-destroying. The marginal investor is already diversified across many holdings, so commodity price risk is diversifiable and commands no premium. Hedging via futures contracts also eliminates the upside from favourable price moves, which diversified investors would otherwise benefit from. The same logic applies to exchange rate hedging for stable currency pairs.

      The exception for public firms is when the firm carries \textbf{high debt levels}. Here, a sudden adverse move in output prices or costs could trigger financial distress or bankruptcy — a scenario where hedging is justified even for public firms because the downside is no longer merely a return reduction but an existential threat.

      For \textbf{emerging market currencies} with potential for extreme, unprecedented fluctuations, the standard logic breaks down — the magnitude of possible moves justifies hedging regardless of investor diversification.

      When a project's time horizon exceeds the availability of publicly traded hedging instruments, firms can arrange a \textbf{synthetic hedge} through a financial institution, constructed to order for the specific exposure. This comes at a higher cost than exchange-traded derivatives but may be the only viable option for long-duration projects.

      The Costco case study surfaces several practical modelling considerations that apply broadly to real project analysis:

        \item \textbf{Segmented betas for opportunity costs:} when computing the opportunity cost of displaced revenues, the appropriate beta is that of the business segment generating those revenues — not the beta of the new project. The displaced revenue beta reflects the risk of what is being given up, not what is being taken on

        \item \textbf{G\&A allocation is subjective:} the portion of general and administrative costs allocated to a project is a judgement call, yet it can dominate the cost structure — in this case accounting for more than half of total operating costs across most years. Small changes in allocation assumptions can swing the project's profitability materially

        \item \textbf{Displaced future investments:} when an early investment accelerates or replaces a later planned investment, both effects must be captured. The logistics expansion in year 4 generates not only its own costs but also eliminates the need for a separate expansion in year 11 — the time-weighted benefit of avoiding that future outflow must be credited back to the project

      \end{itemize}

  \subsection{Real Options}

    \begin{core}{Limitations of Traditional Investment Analysis}

      Traditional investment analysis is inherently \textbf{static} — it evaluates a project as it stands today and does not capture the strategic flexibility embedded within it. In reality, firms hold options within their investments that can add substantial value, even turning a conventionally ``bad'' project into a worthwhile one. These are called \textbf{real options}, and they take three primary forms:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item The option to \textbf{delay} a project when the firm holds exclusive rights to it

        \item The option to \textbf{expand} into further opportunities that a current project unlocks

        \item The option to \textbf{abandon} a project if cash flows fail to meet expectations

      \end{itemize}

      The cost of ignoring real options is most visible during economic downturns — the most capital-intensive firms suffer the steepest stock price declines precisely because their heavy asset base leaves them with little flexibility to abandon or restructure projects, locking them into loss-making positions.

      % One of the limitations of traditional investment analysis is that it is static and does not do a good job of capturing the options embedded in investment
      % The first of these options is the option to delay taking a project, when a firm has exclusive rights to it, until a later date
      % The second of these options is taking one project may allow us to take advantage of other opportunities (projects) in the future
      % The last option that is embedded in projects is the option to abandon a project, if the cash flows do not measure up
      % These options all add value to projects and may make a "bad" project (from traditional analysis) into a good one; these are called REAL OPTIONS
      % Especially during a time of crisis and economic downturn, the most capital intensive in comparison to sales firms would have the highest drop in stock price because they were more locked in on their trades and their projects because there was less of an opportunity to abandon any projects

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{The Option to Delay}

      When a firm holds \textbf{exclusive rights} to a project or product for a defined period, it is not compelled to act immediately. Traditional analysis asks only whether the project is viable \textit{today} — but the rights to a currently unviable project can still carry significant value if conditions are expected to improve.

      A pharmaceutical firm acquiring a patent for a drug it does not yet intend to develop is a clear example: the patent blocks competitors from exploiting the opportunity while the firm waits until it has the capability or market conditions to pursue a superior version. The AI industry presents a similar dynamic, where firms secure positions in nascent technologies well before commercial viability is established.

      Three principles govern the value of the option to delay:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item Exclusive rights to a project are valuable \textit{even if the project is not viable today}

        \item The value of these rights \textbf{increases with the volatility} of the underlying business — greater uncertainty widens the range of future outcomes, making the option more valuable

        \item The cost of acquiring these rights must always be weighed against the option value they confer

      \end{itemize}

      % For example, big pharmaceutical firms can buy the patent right for a drug and not pursue it in case other competitors try to take advantage of the drug, effectively blocking the drug from competitor use until the patent owning firm has made advancements enough to pursue a better version of the drug project

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{The Option to Expand}

      Taking a project today may unlock the right to pursue larger, more valuable projects in the future. A project with a \textbf{negative NPV} in isolation may therefore be worth undertaking if the expansion option it creates more than compensates for that initial loss. Entering a new geographic market at a loss, for example, may establish the foothold needed to scale into a much larger and profitable operation once economies of scale are achieved.

      However, this option only holds value under one critical condition: \textbf{exclusivity}. If the firm does not have exclusive access to the follow-on opportunity, a competitor can observe the firm's initial success and move first on the larger project — eroding the margins and sales the firm was counting on. Without exclusivity, the expansion option is worth considerably less.

      % For example, expanding into a new geographical market with a negative NPV for the initial project may lead to the rights to expand more into the new market with a larger project that has a positive NPV due to economies of scale
      % NOTE: Keep in mind that this only works when you have exclusivity in a project because a competitor may preemptively take that larger scale project after the initial one based on your success with the smaller one
      % If there is no exclusivity, then the second, larger scale project will have its sales and margins pushed down because of more entering competitors

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{The Option to Abandon}

      The option to \textbf{abandon} a project — to exit and cut losses when cash flows fall short of expectations — adds value by truncating the downside of an investment. A firm that can walk away from a failing project before incurring further losses is in a fundamentally better position than one that is locked in.

      This option is most valuable in industries with \textbf{low capital intensity}, where assets are not deeply sunk and exit is relatively costless. Technology and software firms are prime examples — their limited physical asset base means they can wind down projects without bearing the heavy disposal costs that would face a manufacturing or infrastructure firm. Conversely, highly capital-intensive firms have the least abandonment flexibility, which is why they are disproportionately punished during economic downturns when projects turn sour.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Acquisitions as Projects}

    \begin{core}{Valuing an Acquisition}

      An acquisition is, at its core, simply a project — and should be evaluated as one. The analysis proceeds in two stages:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Standalone valuation:} the starting point is the FCF of the target firm on its own, discounted to a NPV. If this NPV exceeds the acquisition price, the deal clears the basic investment hurdle

        \item \textbf{Synergy adjustment:} beyond the standalone value, the potential \textbf{synergies} between the acquirer and the target must be assessed. Synergies can either create or destroy value — cost savings, revenue uplifts, and capability transfers add to NPV, while integration costs, cultural friction, and operational disruption can erode it

      \end{itemize}

      The combined NPV — standalone value adjusted for synergies — determines whether the acquisition is ultimately worth pursuing. As with project synergies discussed in the prior subsection, synergies in M\&A are frequently overstated and rarely materialise to the degree claimed at the time of the deal.

      % Acquisitions
      % In essence, acquisitions are merely projects for a company, and the returns from the project will be first calculated through looking at the FCF for the standalone acquiree. If the NPV of the project is positive in relation to the price of the firm, then it has a greater chance to be a good investment; additional factors to consider is the "synergy" between the two potentially merged firms which can add or destroy value between the merger which can shift the NPV and be another area of consideration before going forward with the (essentially) project

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Post-Mortem \& Ongoing Investment Review}

    \begin{core}{Applying Investment Analysis to Existing Projects}

      The tools and principles of investment analysis are not limited to evaluating new projects — they apply equally to investments already underway or completed. For existing investments, the analysis addresses one of two questions:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Post-mortem:} looking backward, did the investment create value? How do actual cash flows compare to what was originally forecast?

        \item \textbf{What next?} looking forward, should the investment be continued, expanded, or divested? Expected cash flows are reassessed in light of what has been learned, and the decision is re-evaluated using the same NPV and IRR framework applied to new projects

      \end{itemize}

    \begin{core}{Post-Mortem Analysis: Chance vs.\ Bias}

      When actual cash flows deviate from forecasts, the cause can always be attributed to one of two sources:

      \begin{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Chance:} random variation that is simply the nature of risk — outcomes differing from expectations without any systematic pattern

        \item \textbf{Bias:} systematic over-optimism or over-pessimism in the original forecasts, causing errors to consistently skew in one direction

      \end{itemize}

      On any individual project, it is impossible to cleanly separate chance from bias. However, the distinction becomes visible \textit{across projects and over time}: if chance is the culprit, forecast errors should be roughly symmetric — actuals beating forecasts about as often as they fall short. If bias is the cause, errors will persistently lean in one direction, signalling a structural flaw in how the firm builds its projections.

      Based on the updated picture of actual versus expected cash flows, the firm reaches a threshold at which it decides to liquidate, terminate, divest, or continue the investment.

  \subsection{Finding the Optimal Debt Ratio}

    \begin{core}{Approaches to the Optimal}

      Pathways to the Optimal
      The Cost of Capital Approach: The optimal debt ratio is the one that minimizes the cost of capital for a firm
      The Enhanced Cost of Capital Approach: The optimal debt ratio is the one that generates the best combination of (low) cost of capital and (high) operating income

    \begin{core}{The Cost of Capital Approach}

      [1] The Cost of Capital Approach
      Value of a Firm = PV of CFs to the Firm, discounted at CoC
      If the CFs to the firm are constant, and CoC is minimized, the value of the firm will be maximized
      CoC = CoE (% equity) + Pre Tax CoD (1-t)(% debt)
      The question then becomes a simple one. As the debt ratio changes, how does the CoC change?
      BOTH CoE and CoD increase because as a firm takes on more debt, it becomes more risky
      decreases credit rating, increasing default spread, increasing CoD
      increases levered beta, increasing CoE
      ALSO, when interest expenses rise above operating income, the tax benefit dissipates, so it is definitely not worth it to take debt to a point where the interest expenses outweigh the operating income

      Cost of Equity
      We first get the unlevered beta of the company through either the top-down or bottom-up approach (preferably the bottom-up because it has a smaller margin of error)
      Then we create a table of the cost of equity at various levels of D/E ratios by levering the unlevered beta generated

      Cost of Debt
      ASSUMPTIONS: EBITDA, Depreciation, and EBIT stay constant; the only thing we are changing here is the capital structure of the firm and holding everything else constant
      For each level of D/E ratio, we calculate the portion of the total firm value that will be attributed to each level of debt, if debt to capital is 0.10, $ Debt is EV*0.10
      We calculate the interest by multiplying the $ Debt and with the pre-tax cost of debt (this is the CoD based on the likely rating of the firm at 0% debt, this is only for the first iteration of 10%); you would use then the CoD at 10% for the 20% debt calculation
      Throughout each iteration, you would base the ratings off of the Interest coverage ratio of the company at x% of debt
      You should get a table at the end of the CoD at different levels of D/E

      This entire approach is must be always looked in the context of the company; it must be based off of “realistic” standards of debt levels
      For example, if the optimal level of debt for NVDA is 50%, it would not be likely to have 1.54T of debt (based on 24.06.12 Market Cap)
      The debt ratio should also represent interest payments levels below the operating margin because there is a limit to the tax benefit from the interest payments (up to the EBIT); once EBIT is surpassed, the effective tax rate goes down

      Implications of finding the optimal level of debt
      If a company has a lower level of debt in comparison to its optimal, you should theoretically borrow to that optimal level and buyback stock with it to reach the target financing ratio
      Given the magnitude of this decision however, you should expect to answer three questions
      Why should we do it? Depending on where the company is moving in the future with new business segments, we need to consider whether, perhaps, the optimal DE ratio is going to shift in the coming years.
      What if something goes wrong? What if a macroeconomic crisis hits? Taking on more debt will dissipate the cushion the excess capacity can provide financially.
      What if we don’t want to (or cannot) buy back stock and want to make investments with the additional debt capacity?

      It is static
      The most critical number in the entire analysis is the operating income. If that changes the optimal debt ratio will change

      It ignores indirect bankruptcy costs
      The operating income is assumed to stay fixed as the debt ratio and the rating changes

      Beta and Ratings
      It is based upon rigid assumptions of how market risk and default risk get borne as the firm borrows more money and the resulting costs

      Pre-tax Cash Flow Return: Higher cash flows, as a percent of value, give you a higher debt capacity, though less so in emerging markets with substantial country risk (this the reality check)
      Operating Risk: Firms that face more risk or uncertainty in their operations (and more variable operating income as a consequence) will have lower optimal debt ratios than firms that have more predictable operations
      Unlevered beta
      Bond ratings
      Equity vs Debt Risk Premiums: When debt premiums are lower relative to other years ratio of debt to equity, then debt is more attractive

    \begin{core}{The APV Approach}

      The APV Approach to Optimal Capital Structure
      In the APV approach, the value of the firm is written as the sum of the value of the firm without debt (the unlevered firm) and the effect of debt on firm value

      Firm Value=Unlevered Firm Value+(Tax Benefits of Debt-Expected Bankruptcy Cost from the Debt)

      The optimal dollar debt level is the one that maximizes firm value

      Step 1: Estimate the unlevered firm value. This can be done in one of two ways:
      Estimating the unlevered beta, the cost of equity based upon this unlevered beta and then valuing the firm using this cost of equity as the CoC (this is already possible with the content we’ve learned so far)
      Unlevered Firm Value = Current Market Cap - Tax Benefits of Debt (Current) + Expected Bankruptcy cost from Debt

    \begin{core}{Sensitivity Analysis in Debt Design}

      How sensitive is the firm’s value and operating income to changes in the level of interest rates?

      provides insight into whether the firm should be using fixed or floating rate debt

      Firms with stable CFs might prefer fixed rate debt to ensure their interest expenses are also predictable
      If a firm’s income moves in the opposite direction as interest rates, then the firm may also want fixed rate in the possible event of decreasing revenues and increasing interest rates

      How sensitive is the firm’s value and operating income to changes in the GNP/GDP?

      This is the same logic as interest rates

      If there are no tax disadvantages associated with dividends & companies can issue stock, at no issuance cost, to raise equity, whenever needed

  \subsection{Good \& Bad Reasons for Paying Dividends}

    \begin{core}{Bad Reason 1: The Bird in the Hand Fallacy}

      Bad reasons for paying dividends
      The bird in the hand fallacy
      Argument: Dividends now are more certain than capital gains later. Hence dividends are more valuable than capital gains. Stocks that pay dividends will therefore be more highly valued than stocks that do not.
      Counter: The appropriate comparison should be between dividends today and price appreciation today. The stock price drops on the ex-dividend day

      We have excess cash this year…
      Argument: The firm has excess cash on its hands this year, no investment projects this year and wants to give the money back to stockholder
      Counter: So why not just repurchase stock? If this is a one-time phenomenon, the firm has to consider future financing needs. The cost of raising new financing in future years, especially by issuing new equity, can be staggering

    \begin{core}{Good Reason 1: The Clientele Effect}

      There have been several firms throughout history that have shown that cash dividend shares are bought at a premium in comparison to the capital gains only counterpart

      Evidence: A study of 914 investors’ portfolios was carried out to see if their portfolio positions were affected by their tax brackets. The study found that older and poorer investors tended to hold high dividend stocks

      But higher or new dividends may signal bad news because it may portray that the company is no longer a growth company

The effect of dividends have been diluted
