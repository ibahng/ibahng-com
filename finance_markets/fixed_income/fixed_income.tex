% The Roots of the Bond Market
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% The fixed-income market is the largest market in the world. At the end of 2016, there were over $101T worth of traded bonds in existence worldwide.
% GDP was $79T at the time.
% World stock market was $65T.

% Current Bond Market Size
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% There are 2.1M government and corporate bonds in existence (adds up to $101T).

% Corporate bonds compose 1.6M of the 2.1M bonds outstanding.
% There is a greater diversity in corporate bonds, and like the US government, a single corporation can also issue a vast array of bonds.

% The US treasury had 1086 actively traded bonds and bills outstanding (accounting for $14T).
% The US bond market plays a crucial role in global finance.
% US corporate bonds account for $8T of the market (the 2nd largest segment).
% The US bond market’s relative size is one of the main reasons for dollar centricity in the currency market.

% The bond market is relatively recent like the FX market, born out of:
% * IR liberalization
% * Removal of capital controls after WWII, making it easier for governments to borrow

% Governments used to raise money for a few cases such as war and justice. But with the advent of the welfare state and social safety net, government spending as a proportion of GDP has risen steeply as advanced nations now pay more for healthcare, education, and other public services.

% US Debt for Foreign Nations
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Since currencies floated freely since 1971, many countries, have been forced to accumulate war chests of FX reserves to defend their pegs.
% Other countries sell their own currencies to buy US government bonds in order to weaken their currency.

% US treasuries are used as a safe and easily accessible place to park their money:
% * Strong reputation for creditworthiness
% * Right to tax citizens and corporations of the wealthiest country on earth
% * Privilege of being able to print the world’s reserve currency
% * US bonds are the most liquid tradable assets on earth
% * The FX market is US dollar centric

% Investors also use US treasury bonds as a safe haven. During times of financial turmoil and crisis, investors bought US bonds, bringing bond yields down.
% **Bond price and yields hold an inverse relationship**

% Why do companies borrow in the corporate bond market?
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% In most countries, a discrepancy in tax treatment means that debt repayments lower a company’s tax bill by reducing pre-tax profits through interest payments.
% Companies can borrow money for longer terms from the bond market than from banks, which are not typically keen on making long term loans to corporations.

% Bond Structure
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Bonds are IOU notes promising to pay regular fixed amount payments (coupons) and a large payment at maturation (principal); this is fixed income.

% Bond Yields are similar to APR on a credit card.
% The main input to the calculation of yield is the price of the bond (which fluctuates).
% Fixed income ≠ fixed yield.
% The remaining repayments and the floating bond price are used to calculate the yield.
% Yields make the diversity of the 2.1 million bonds in the market.

% Generally speaking, the lower the agreed purchase price for a bond, the greater the APR, or yield, as a smaller up-front sum results in the same fixed amount repayments down the road.
% EX. Perpetual bond is a bond whose principal is never repaid → yield and price are a perfect mirror image.

% New Lenders   | New Borrowers
% --------------|--------------
% High Yields   | Good / Bad
% Low Yields    | Bad / Good

% *Servicing a debt refers to sustaining one’s existing amount of borrowing by paying coupons.

% Summary: The Roots of the Bond Market
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * Fixed income is another word for the bond market, where loan agreements are bought and sold.
% * The term fixed income stems from the fixed nature of bond repayments.
% * The growth of governments has been the main factor giving rise to the $101T world bond market.
% * Investors view US government bonds as the safest, most liquid financial assets in the world.
% * There are millions of bonds outstanding and investors use yields to compare one bond to another.

% Bond Valuation
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% A bond yield is simply the equivalent interest rate on a bank account that would provide an identical end amount given that the coupon payments are deposited into this bank account upon receiving.

% Bond yields are nothing more than advertised deposit rates; the difference is that bond yields fluctuate as the price of the bond moves.

% Bond Valuation Drivers
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * Credit Risk
%   * Credit Risk Factors
%   * Credit Risk Indicators
% * Macroeconomics
%   * Short-Term Interest Rates
%   * Inflation

% Credit Risk Factors
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Bond prices today reflect beliefs on repayment likelihood tomorrow. The assumption when calculating yield, however, is that the income is truly fixed, that the full repayment amounts promised will materialize. Therefore, notwithstanding your fears about the solvency of the borrower, when it comes to calculating the yield, you assume that you will get the same cash for a lower price.

% When bond investors (lenders) begin to doubt the creditworthiness of a borrower, yields move sharply upwards.

% If the market believes that the government may have trouble paying money back, the price of the bond will be lower and, therefore, the yield higher. This makes future borrowing more expensive for this government. In this way, the bond market instills fiscal discipline in governments.

% To keep tabs on a government’s creditworthiness, investors commonly look at three metrics:
% 1. Debt/GDP
% 2. Deficit/GDP
% 3. Repayment Schedule

% Debt/GDP
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% As a rule of thumb, the higher a government’s debt burden in proportion to its GDP, the riskier its bonds. For higher risk, investors will demand a higher return in the form of an elevated yield. There is, however, no general rule on when bond yields will spike. Japan is one of the most heavily indebted nations in the world, yet it has one of the lowest government bond yields.

% When debt is repaid, this payment does not include itself in GDP; the original spending of money brought from this debt is when GDP increases.

% Deficit/GDP
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Negative deficits means that the governments are spending more money than they are bringing in through taxation, relying on the bond market to finance their deficit.
% The higher the deficit, the higher the interest payments go up as a proportion of the government budget.
% Again, there is no hard/fast rule on the % deficit that will make investors bail.

% Repayment Schedule (Rolling over Debt)
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Borrowing over the short term attracts a lower annual interest rate because it lowers risk for the lender. This is tempting because it lowers interest payments.
% EX. governments may theoretically repay debt by issuing new debt (rolling over debt).
% The downside of taking advantage of low interest short-term debt is that lenders can pull the rug and suddenly demand very high yields.
% By borrowing in the short-term, and refinancing the debt on a regular basis, the US government takes advantage of the lower interest rates available for short-term borrowers. US isn’t afraid of investors pulling the rug because of its strong creditworthiness.

% Credit Risk Indicators
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Credit Ratings
% Ratings agencies give bonds a rating ranging from AAA to D.

% With corporate bonds, ratings higher than BBB- or at BBB- are called “investment grade”; BB+ and below are called “non-investment grade” AKA “junk bonds”.

% Keep in mind that the track record for credit rating agencies is decidedly patchier for corporate bonds that it is for government bonds.

% Credit Default Swaps (CDSs)
% CDSs are like insurance on bonds going bust.

% CDSs often provide a more timely warning of impending default than ratings. This is b/c they are “real-time readings based on traded instruments. The higher the CDS spread, the higher the risk.

% The other major driver of bond valuation is the macroeconomic environment:
% * Short-Term Interest Rate
% * Inflation

% Short-Term Interest Rates
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Short-term interest rates directly affect the yields of the bond market.

% Non-US Government treasury bonds must always check their prices/yield with US treasury bonds; The US is the most creditworthy, thus all other bonds w/ the same details as US bonds must always be cheaper (thus higher yield) b/c of the added risk.

% This yield competition phenomenon is simply price competition masked by the jargon of the bond market.

% Inflation
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Inflation will erode the purchasing power of a bond’s coupon and principal payments, thus with higher inflation, bond yields increase as people demand lower prices for the same nominal returns.
% Bond yields are called nominal and not real. You do not adjust the yield calculation for inflation.

% Summary: Bond Valuation
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * A bond yield is just the IR on an equivalent bank account for the duration of the bond.
% * The three biggest factors driving bond yields are the creditworthiness of the borrower, inflation, and short-term interest rates.
% * Short-term borrowing is cheaper but riskier for borrowers than long-term borrowing as it relies on the ongoing appetite of lenders.
% * As repayments to bondholders are fixed, inflation will corrode the purchasing power of fixed bond repayments, sending yields up.
% * As US government bond yields serve as benchmarks for all investments, yields on other bonds tend to move with them.

% Central Bankers & Interest Rates
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% How do governments set short-term bond yields, that is, how they attempt to nudge the economy towards slow, steady growth?

% Central Bank Mandates (Inflation/Deflation)
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Inflation is psychological and is most prevalent during wartime when governments print large amounts of money to fund the war.
% Delation is also psychological and bad for the economy because consumers keep delaying purchase in response to constant price decreases.

% Bond markets are ravaged by inflation which became a major issue for economies in the 1970s.

% *High inflation will erode the price of bonds and send yields up.

% Central Bank Decision Making (Inflation Measures)
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Central Banks use three main statistics to gauge the economies’ inflation:
% * GDP deflator
% * CPI stat
% * PCE stat (PCE strips out volatile costs of food and energy, allowing for a more trustworthy representation of consumer consumption)

% However since these statistics only look at the past and CPI forecasts are only so accurate, investors usually look at TIPS to gauge inflation.

% Treasury Inflation Protected Securities (TIPS)
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Instead of having “fixed-income” payments to the lender, TIPS compensate the lender in the event of inflation, using CPI as a guide.
% By comparing the price of TIPS and non-inflation protected bonds, investors can glean inflation expectations. Higher inflation → higher demand for TIPS relative to normal bonds → greater difference in yields.

% Central Bank Decision Making (Output Gap)
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% The output gap is the difference between an economy’s potential output and its actual output.
% When the economy is tight—the actual output is higher than the potential—it is a frequent sign of inflation, while slackness frequently coincides with deflation.

% Output Gap% = (actual output $B - potential output $B) / potential output $B

% Once the central bankers have pored over all manner of inflation statistics, examined the output gap, and studied other data sources, they decide whether to cool the porridge, heat the porridge, or do nothing.

% Central Bank Toolkit (Short-Term Interest Rates)
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% When short-term interest rates go up, it becomes more attractive to deposit cash, dissuading consumption/investment, lowering the actual output, and bring inflation down (which coincides w/ tight output).
% The short term interest rate decision set by the Federal Open Market Committee 8 times a year are tremendous news as the US bond yields are influenced by it.

% Central Bank Toolkit (Statements)
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Small difference in past and recent FOMC statements can hint at a shift in bond yield indirectly.

% Short-term interest rate estimates:
% The futures and options market allows us to determine the probability of certain future FOMC decisions.

% Summary: Central Bankers & Interest Rates
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * Most central banks have a mandate to prevent runaway inflation and deflation.
% * As inflation is corrosive to bonds, fixed-income investors watch hawkishly for any signs of inflation.
% * Central banks closely monitor inflation expectations and the output gap when making rate decisions.
% * Central Banks contain inflation and deflation by directly changing interest rates or by altering interest rate expectations.
% * Over the past few decades, short-term interest rates have been the tool of choice to steer economics.

% The Yield Curve & Why it Matters
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Defining the Yield Curve
% The yield curve for most countries show a positive sloping curve with higher yields given the longer term bonds due to the higher risk added with greater chance of inaccuracy in inflation predictions.

% The difference between the yield on the longer maturity bonds and shorter-maturity bonds is called the “term premium”.

% The yield curve represents the cost of borrowing and, therefore, it reverberates throughout the markets and the world economy.
% When the Fed alters the left-hand end of the yield curve—short-term interest rate—it affects prices and yields of the whole $101T bond market.

% Corporate Impact
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% Almost all corporate investments are multiyear, thus medium or long-term borrowing is used to match the project’s duration.
% Facing the same risks of short term interest rate hikes and inflation (both of which will dent bond prices for the lender), corporations must also face the fact that they are typically less creditworthy than governments, thus they must typically provide higher yields for the bonds of the same maturity.
% This difference in yield between the treasury bonds and corporate bonds is called “the spread”.
% Corporate yield curves typically move in cohesion with government yield curve increases and decreases. Thus the spread stays relatively the same although overall yields either spiked or hiked (governments use this as a regulator for economic growth and inflation).
% The gap between all US government 10-year bond yield and all corporate investment-grade 10 year bond yield is called the “corporate spread”.
% * When the corporate spread increases (widening, cheapening, steepening)
% * When the corporate spread decreases (tightening, richening, flattening)

% Consumer Impact
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% US 30-year mortgage rates are priced off of government 10 year bond yields as the spread between these figures are typically consistent.
% Government bond yields therefore influence the affordability of homes and activity in the housing market (a large GDP portion).

% Global Impact
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% 10 year bond yields of several advanced economies are strongly correlated, thus they are stimulated/depressed in sync.

% Summary: The Yield Curve & Why it Matters
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * The yield curve represents the cost of borrowing for various loan lengths.
% * Yield curves are naturally upward sloping due to elevated risk of long-term lending.
% * Corporate bonds are priced using a spread off the government yield curve so the yield curve indirectly regulates company funding.
% * Consumer borrowing for big ticket items is priced off the yield curve.
% * Yields of developed economics are correlated, which means that the overall movement in yields has a global impact.

% Movements in the Yield Curve
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% The Yield Curve in Motion
% US government bond yields and, therefore, prices are principally driven by short-term interest rates and inflation. Creditworthiness is a non-issue, as we saw. Investors can therefore make profitable trades by correctly predicting the Fed’s next move and inflation.
% The left hand end of the yield curve is set by the Fed’s short-term interest rate decisions. It moves when the Fed changes the rate at the FOMC meeting.

% The Fed funds target rate moves in tandem with the left-hand end of the yield curve.
% The left hand end of the yield curve is simply the Fed Funds Target Rate.

% The right hand end of the yield curve is influenced majorly by ingrained inflation expectations.

% When inflation rises, the right hand side of the yield curve is free to move through the natural supply and demand of bond investors while the left hand side is tethered to the Fed Fund Target Rate.

% 2003 Dotcom bust recovery
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% The Fed might need to rein in inflation and preemptively lift short-term interest rates. With expected hikes, bond investors did not want to lose money by holding bonds (raised long-term yields resulted). Steep yield curves are a sign of an accelerating economy.
% Short-term interest rates finally rose and investors aggressively bought long-term bonds in expectation of a slowing economy and currently high rates.
% This drove long-term bond yields down resulting in an inverted yield curve a common precursor to an economic downturn.

% Summary: Movements in the Yield Curve
% ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
% * The far left of the yield curve is the overnight interest rate set by the central bank.
% * The far right of the yield curve is driven primarily by inflation expectations.
% * While the left-hand end of the yield curve is locked, the right hand end float freely.
% * A steep yield curve signals improving times.
% * A flat yield curve signals worsening times.
% * An inverted yield curve often precedes a recession.
