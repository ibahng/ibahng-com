\section{Light}

  \subsection{Waves}

    \begin{core}{Light}

      \textbf{Light} consists of oscillations of electric and magnetic fields --- or \textbf{electromagnatic waves} --- that travel together at the speed of light ($c \approx 3.0 \times 10^8\,m\,s^{-1}$)

      \myfig{0.75}{quantization_figures/light.png}{The respective electric and magnetic field oscillations of an electromagnetic wave}{fig:placeholder1}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Frequency, Wavelength, and the Electromagnetic Spectrum}

      While \textbf{frequency (ν)} quantifies the number of oscillations that occur per second, \textbf{wavelength (λ)} represents the physical distance spanned by a single cycle. Thus,
      \[
        c = λν
      \]

      The electromagnetic spectrum is the complete range of all electromagnetic radiation, organized by frequency or wavelength.

      \myfig{0.55}{quantization_figures/em_spectrum.png}{The electromagnetic spectrum (\textit{top}) and the visible specturm (\textit{bottom})}{fig:placeholder2}

      It describes every form of light in the universe, from the vast radio waves used for broadcasting to the tiny, high-energy gamma rays emitted by nuclear reactions.

      The \textbf{visible spectrum} is the portion of the EM spectrum that corresponds to EM waves with wavelengths between 400nm and 700nm.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Wave Power \& Energy}

      A light wave's power and energy are two distinct characteristics.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item \textbf{Power} is determined by the \textbf{amplitude} of the wave and represents the rate at which the light's energy is being delivered or transferred.

        \myfig{0.55}{quantization_figures/amplitude.png}{Wave amplitude}{fig:placeholder3}

        To understand why, it's helpful to understand the additive properties of waves or \textbf{superposition}. Waves do not "collide" and bounce off each other like billiard balls. Instead, they pass through one another, and their amplitudes add together algebraically at every point in space and time.

        \myfig{0.4}{quantization_figures/superposition.png}{Constructive and destructive interference as a result of wave superposition}{fig:placeholder4}

        This means when we see an amplitude increase, it's not a single wave getting stronger, but rather many waves of the same frequency being delivered simultaneously, thus increasing the rate at which energy is delivered (\textit{i.e., power}).

        \item \textbf{Energy} is the total amount of work a light wave can perform and is determined by the frequency of the wave. Higher frequency (\textit{more frequent oscillations}) means higher energy, and lower frequency (\textit{less frequent oscillations}) means lower energy. Thus
        \[
          E_{gamma} > E_{Xrays} > E_{ultraviolet}
        \]

      \end{enumerate}
      
      \textit{To understand the distinction between wave power and energy, imagine a stream of marbles moving through space at constant speed. Increasing each marble's mass will increase its kinetic energy on impact, thus increasing the work it can perform when colliding with another system. The marble's mass is analogous to the frequency of EM waves. Now imagine adding a second stream so the rate of marble delivery doubles. This is analogous to the power of EM waves.}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Photons}

    \begin{core}{Photons}

      Light energy is quantized into "packets" called \textbf{photons}, meaning that the energy of light is not a continuous, smooth flow, but is instead delivered in \textbf{discrete, indivisible "packets"}---\textit{just as a stream of water may look continuous on the macroscale but consists of discrete, indivisible \ce{H2O} molecules on the microscale.} The energy of an individual photon is determined by the frequency of the electromagnetic wave it is part of (\textit{as discussed above in the context of the general wave}). 
      \[
        E_{photon} = \frac{hc}{λ} = hν
      \]
      where $h = 6.626 \times 10^{-34}\,J\,s$ is \textbf{Planck's constant}. It represents a conversion factor that relates a photon's frequency to its energy.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Photoelectric Effect}

      Prior to the discovery of photons, the leading theory was the \textbf{Classical Wave Theory}, which viewed light as a continuous wave carrying energy proportional to its intensity (\textit{brightness}). This assumed that if you shine light on an atom, the \ce{e-}s should eventually "soak up" enough energy to break free regardless of the wavelength of the light.

      The existence of photons was proven via the \textbf{photoelectric effect}, where the kinetic energy of an \ce{e^-} begins to rise only after a certain threshold frequency, revealing that light interacts with matter in discrete, one-to-one events.

      This gave rise to the \textbf{"all-or-nothing" rule}, which states that an \ce{e^-} can only absorb one photon at a time and thus cannot accumulate energy from multiple low-energy photons.

      As shown in the graph above, the point where the line intersects the x-axis represents the threshold frequency at which an \ce{e^-} will be ejected from the atom. Anything below this frequency will have no effect, whereas anything above will cause the \ce{e^-} to be ejected with greater kinetic energy.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Calculating Photon Energy}

      Let's calculate the energy of a photon of blue light ($450nm$).
      \[
        E_{photon} = \frac{(6.626 \times 10^{-34}\,J\,s)(3.0 \times 10^8\,m\,s^{-1})}{450nm} = 4.417 \times 10^{-19}\,J
      \]
      However, instead of joules, the energy of a photon can also be measured in \textbf{eV} (electron volts), a much smaller and more convenient unit than the joule for describing energy levels of individual particles like \ce{e^-}s and \ce{p^+}s. It represents the amount of energy gained or lost by a single \ce{e^-} as it accelerates through an electric potential difference of 1 V in a vacuum.
      \[
        eV = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,J
      \]
      Thus the energy of a photon of blue light ($450nm$) would be
      \[
        E_{photon} = \frac{4.417 \times 10^{-19}\,J}{1.602 \times 10^{-19}\,J\,eV^{-1}} = 2.757\,eV
      \]
      
    \end{core}

\section{Electron Energy}

  \subsection{Wave-Particle Duality of Electrons}

    So far in our study of general chemistry, we have exclusively treated electrons as discrete particles. This is because, for the purposes of "chemical bookkeeping," the particle model is incredibly efficient (\textit{such as in \ce{e^-} transfer within redox reactions}). However, as you move deeper into physical chemistry, the model shifts.

    Here we also introduce the \textbf{wave-like nature of electrons}. Physicists have proven this wave-like state, namely through the double-slit experiment.

    \begin{core}{Double Slit Experiment}

      The \textbf{double-slit experiment} was a setup where a single \ce{e^-} was shot at a double slit in a wall and would leave a mark indicating where it hit on the screen on the other side of the slits.

      \myfig{0.35}{quantization_figures/double_slit.png}{Double slit experiment wave interference pattern}{fig:placeholder6}

      The results of the experiment produced a diffraction pattern that could only be explained by assuming that an \ce{e^-} behaves like a wave. The regions where marks were left represent regions hit by constructively interfering portions of the waves that originated from the slits. The regions with no marks represent regions hit by destructively interfering portions of the waves that originated from the slits.

      If \ce{e^-}s only behaved like particles, as physicists had assumed prior to this experiment, each would have passed through only one slit or the other at any given time. Over time, as you fire thousands of \ce{e^-}s, you would see only two distinct clusters or "piles" of hits on the screen, directly behind each slit.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Single Slit Experiment}

      We see a similar diffraction pattern in the \textbf{single-slit experiment} as well.

      \myfig{0.55}{quantization_figures/single_slit.png}{Single slit experiment diffraction pattern}{fig:placeholder7}

      The reason we see a diffraction pattern is the \textbf{Huygens-Fresnel principle}, which states that we don't treat a slit as just a "hole" that light passes through. Instead, every point along the width of the slit acts as a \textbf{secondary source} of spherical wavelets. These wavelets spread out in all directions from their respective points of origin. Thus the diffraction pattern we see on the screen is the interference pattern created by the superposition of all these secondary wavelets.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Electron Orbital Basics}

    \begin{core}{Standing Waves}

      A \textbf{standing wave} is a wave pattern that oscillates in time but does not move in space (\textit{confined to a restricted region}), formed by the interference of two waves of the \textbf{same frequency and amplitude} traveling \textbf{in opposite directions}.

      \myfig{0.5}{quantization_figures/standing_wave.png}{One-dimensional standing wave harmonics}{fig:placeholder8}

      Recognize that there are points along the standing waves shown above where the medium remains completely still and experiences zero amplitude and displacement due to destructive interference. These points are called \textbf{nodes} of the wave. Now let's take a look at a 2D standing wave.

      \myfig{0.7}{quantization_figures/2d_standing_wave.png}{Two-dimensional standing wave pattern}{fig:placeholder9}

      It's harder to visualize, but just like the 1D standing wave displayed above, 2D standing waves also have nodes where the wave function sums to zero. In 2D, these nodes can be either radial or angular, as shown below.

      \myfig{0.4}{quantization_figures/nodes.png}{Radial and angular nodes of two-dimensional standing waves}{fig:placeholder10}

      It's also important to keep in mind that the energy of these standing waves increases as the node count increases because the frequency of the standing wave increases.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Electron Orbitals}

      Now we introduce \textbf{electron orbitals}, which are \textbf{3-dimensional standing waves} that represent the \textbf{likelihood} of where \ce{e^-}s are located in space in the context of their wave-like characteristics rather than as particles.

      \myfig{0.5}{quantization_figures/e_orbitals.png}{Shapes of s, p, d, and f orbitals}{fig:placeholder11}

      Just as a standing wave exists only within a confined region, electron orbitals are also \textbf{confined in a space determined by the Coulomb attractions} between them and the nucleus of the atom.

    \end{core}
  
    \begin{core}{Quantum State of Electrons}

      Electron orbitals are categorized by their energy levels, identified with a \textbf{principal quantum number ($n$)} and an \textbf{angular quantum number ($l$)}. Electron orbitals within the same energy level are further distinguished by their \textbf{magnetic quantum number ($m_l$)}. Each electron within each orbital is again distinguished by its \textbf{spin quantum number ($m_s$)}.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item The \textbf{principal quantum number} $n$ defines the electron's main energy level (shell), its average distance from the nucleus, and its size. The value $n - 1$ also represents the \textbf{total number of nodes} within the electron orbital. Generally, the higher $n$ is, the higher the energy level of the orbital; however, there are some nuances to this based on the angular quantum number.

        \item The \textbf{angular quantum number} $l$ defines the subshell of the electron orbital and, more importantly, the shape of the orbital by representing the \textbf{number of angular nodes}. We use letters $s, p, d, f$ corresponding to $0, 1, 2, 3$, respectively, to represent the angular quantum numbers.

        Here is a schematic showing the different electron orbitals with their principal and angular quantum numbers. The arrow indicates the order from lowest (1s) to highest (7p) orbital energy levels. 

        \myfig{0.5}{quantization_figures/orbital_table.png}{Electron orbital energy level ordering schematic}{fig:placeholder12}

        Let's look at an example of the 3p orbital and its corresponding principal quantum number (3) and angular quantum number (1 or p).

        \myfig{0.35}{quantization_figures/3p_example.png}{3p electron orbital}{fig:placeholder13}

        As you can see, there are a total of $n-1 = 3-1 = 2$ nodes, of which there are $l = 1$ angular nodes.

        \item The \textbf{magnetic quantum number} $m_l$ simply tells us the spatial orientation of the electron orbital. The values for $m_l$, however, depend on the angular quantum number $l$. $m_l$ can only be integers from $-l$ to $l$, inclusive.

        \begin{table}[H]
          \centering
          \begin{tabular}{cc}
            \hline
            $l$ & Possible $m_l$ \\ \hline
            0 (s) & 0 \\
            1 (p) & $-1, 0, +1$ \\
            2 (d) & $-2, -1, 0, +1, +2$ \\
            3 (f) & $-3, -2, -1, 0, +1, +2, +3$ \\ \hline
          \end{tabular}
          \caption{Magnetic quantum numbers ($m_l$) for each angular quantum number ($l$)}
          \label{tab:quantum}
        \end{table}

        We revisit this figure, which illustrates the various orientations across the four angular quantum numbers.

        \myfig{0.6}{quantization_figures/e_orbitals.png}{Shapes of s, p, d, and f orbitals}{fig:placeholder15}

        As shown, s and p orbitals possess relatively straightforward orientations---either none (\textit{for the s orbital}) or along the x, y, or z axis (\textit{for the p orbitals}). However, the d and f orbitals require additional orientations beyond these simple Cartesian axes. The nature of these additional orientations will not be examined in these notes.

        It is sufficient to understand that the orientations of each electron orbital (\textit{with unique principal, angular, and magnetic quantum number combinations}) formed within an atom are directed such that they remain \textbf{orthogonal} to one another. While \textbf{orthogonality} in mathematics denotes independence or perpendicularity, in the context of electron orbitals, it signifies that each orbital is \textbf{statistically non-overlapping} with all others, despite potentially occupying the same general spatial region. Consequently, the \ce{e^-}s within the $2p_{-1}$ orbital will not occupy the $2p_{0}$ or $2p_{+1}$ orbitals, nor any other orbitals with differing angular or principal quantum numbers.

        \item Finally, we address the \textbf{spin quantum number}. The spin quantum number describes the orientation of the electron's spin within the orbital. It should be noted that nothing physically "spins" in the classical sense; rather, spin represents the orientation of the electron's intrinsic magnetic moment. This magnetic moment is quantized such that measurement of its magnitude along any given direction yields only two possible values: spin-up ($+\nicefrac{1}{2}$) or spin-down ($-\nicefrac{1}{2}$), which are orthogonal to each other.

      \end{enumerate}

    \end{core}
  
    \begin{core}{Pauli Exclusion Principle}
    
      Each \ce{e^-} within an atom possesses a unique set of quantum numbers ($n$, $l$, $m_l$, and $m_s$) that fully characterize the electron's spatial and angular orientation, as well as its spin state. This is formalized by the \textbf{Pauli Exclusion Principle}, which states that \textbf{no two \ce{e^-}s can occupy the same orbital with the same spin orientation}.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:hydrogenic]{Orbital Energy Discrepancies \& Hydrogenic Atomic Orbitals}

      You may be wondering why the \textbf{order of orbital energy levels is not entirely determined by the total number of nodes}. After all, we have established that the energy of a wave depends on its frequency---and by extension, its number of nodes. As shown in \autoref{fig:placeholder16}, the 2s and 2p orbitals illustrate this pattern well because both contain the same number of nodes (1), yet the 2s orbital sits at a lower energy than the 2p.

      \myfig{0.55}{quantization_figures/orbital_table.png}{Electron orbital energy level ordering schematic}{fig:placeholder16}

      To understand why, we first examine \textbf{hydrogenic atom orbitals}---those of atoms stripped of all but one \ce{e^-}, such as \ce{H}, \ce{He+}, and \ce{Li^{2+}}. In these simplified systems, the energy of the electron depends \textit{only} on the total number of nodes, or equivalently, on the principal quantum number $n$, regardless of the angular quantum number $l$. This is fully consistent with our earlier observation that more nodes means higher frequency, which means higher energy. The energy of a hydrogenic orbital is given quantitatively by:
      \[
        E = -\frac{hcRZ^2}{n^2}
      \]

      where $h$ is Planck's constant ($6.626 \times 10^{-34}\,\text{J\,s}$), $c$ is the speed of light ($2.998 \times 10^8\,\text{m\,s}^{-1}$), $R$ is the Rydberg constant ($1.097 \times 10^7\,\text{m}^{-1}$), $Z$ is the atomic number, and $n$ is the principal quantum number. The negative sign reflects the fact that the electron sits in an energy well relative to a free electron---consistent with our earlier discussion of potential energy and bond energies in EXTERNAL REFERENCE.

      Two trends follow directly from this expression. First, \textbf{energy decreases as $Z$ increases}, reflecting the stronger attractive pull of a more positively charged nucleus on the single \ce{e^-}. Second, energy increases with $n$, consistent with the wave-like nature of the electron and the role of nodes in determining frequency. \autoref{fig:placeholder17} below illustrates these energy levels across different principal quantum numbers for a hydrogenic atom.

      \myfig{0.4}{quantization_figures/hydrogenic.png}{Hydrogen-like atom orbital energies across different principal quantum numbers}{fig:placeholder17}

      The energy change when an \ce{e^-} transitions between orbitals is given by the following equation
      \[
        \Delta E = -hcRZ^2\left(\frac{1}{n^2_f}-\frac{1}{n^2_i}\right)
      \]

      With this foundation in place, we can now return to the original question. In a hydrogenic atom, orbital energy depends solely on $n$. However, when additional electrons are introduced, \textbf{\ce{e^-} \ce{e^-} repulsion} disturbs this clean dependence. These repulsion interactions alter the \textbf{radial} character of orbitals---while the \textbf{angular} character and total node count \textbf{remain unchanged} from the hydrogenic case---causing orbitals with the same $n$ but different $l$ to diverge in energy. The result is that orbital energy in multi-electron atoms depends on \textit{both} $n$ and $l$, producing the ordering we observe in practice.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}[label=core:aufbau]{Aufbau Principle}

      According to the \textbf{Aufbau principle}, \ce{e^-} fill orbitals in order from \textbf{lowest to highest energy} as an atom gains more \ce{e^-}s. The diagonal arrow schematic in \autoref{fig:placeholder16} is a useful mnemonic for remembering this filling order across subshells.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Simplifying Electron Configuration Notation}

      The Aufbau principle is best understood through electron configuration diagrams, where orbitals are filled sequentially from lowest to highest energy. \autoref{fig:placeholder19} illustrates this clearly for Li, Ne, and Al.

      It is also convenient to use \textbf{noble gas notation} to simplify electron configurations. By placing a noble gas symbol in brackets---for example, \ce{[Ne]} as shown in the Al electron configuration---you represent all core electrons corresponding to that noble gas's stable configuration, allowing you to focus exclusively on the valence electrons responsible for chemical bonding.

      \myfig{0.55}{quantization_figures/example_configurations.png}{Electron configuration notation for Li, Ne, and Al}{fig:placeholder19}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Hund's Rule}

      \textbf{Hund's Rule} states that within the highest energy subshell, \ce{e^-}s occupy orbitals singly and with the same spin before any pairing occurs, maximizing the number of unpaired \ce{e^-}s.

      \myfig{0.55}{quantization_figures/hunds_rule.png}{Hund's rule electron configurations}{fig:placeholder20}

      The underlying reason stems from the \ce{e^-} \ce{e^-} repulsion interactions briefly introduced earlier, the full treatment of which is beyond the scope of these notes.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Electron Energy Emissions \& Absorptions}

    \begin{core}{Electron Energy Emissions \& Absorptions}

      Returning to the electron energies of hydrogenic ion orbitals, the magnitude of the potential energy well of an \ce{e^-} is given by the Rydberg formula.
      \[
        E = -\frac{hcRZ^2}{n^2}
      \]

      As a side note, multiplying by Avogadro's number gives the same quantity per mole of electrons.
      \[
        E = -\frac{hcRN_AZ^2}{n^2}
      \]

      Because electron bonding follows the same energetic logic as any bond, energy is absorbed when an \ce{e^-} is promoted to a higher shell (\textit{greater $n$}) and emitted when it relaxes to a lower shell (\textit{lower $n$}).
      \[
        \Delta E_{electron} > 0 \rightarrow \text{absorption} \qquad \Delta E_{electron} < 0 \rightarrow \text{emission}
      \]

      Since the energy gaps between adjacent shells narrow at higher $n$, electrons in higher principal quantum number orbitals are \textbf{more susceptible to thermal excitation}---this is especially relevant for elements in the lower rows of the periodic table.

      \textbf{Flames} provide a practical illustration of this phenomenon. At flame temperatures, intramolecular bonds are broken and \textbf{electrons are promoted to higher orbitals}. As they relax back down, the energy difference is \textbf{released as photons}. Because the Rydberg formula ties electron energy differences directly to photon wavelength, different compounds produce distinct emission wavelengths---and when those wavelengths fall within the \textbf{visible spectrum}, they appear as characteristic colors.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Crystal Field Theory}

      The emission of photons within the visible spectrum is particularly well associated with \textbf{transition metals}, whose valence electrons occupy d orbitals. To understand why, we must first introduce Crystal Field Theory.

      \myfig{0.8}{quantization_figures/d_orbitals.png}{d orbital shapes}{fig:placeholder22}

      \textbf{Crystal Field Theory} is a model that explains the splitting of d orbital energy levels in \textbf{coordination compounds}---structures consisting of a central transition metal ion surrounded by bound molecules or ions called ligands, as shown in \autoref{fig:placeholder23}.

      \myfig{0.25}{quantization_figures/coordination_compound.png}{Coordination compound example}{fig:placeholder23}

      In the above mole, \ce{NH3} and \ce{NO2} act as ligands donating \ce{e^-} density into the d orbitals of \ce{Co}. This incoming \ce{e^-} density repels the existing \ce{e^-} density on the metal ion---the same \ce{e^-} \ce{e^-} repulsion discussed earlier in EXTERNAL REFERENCE---and just as that repulsion caused orbital energy levels to depend on both $n$ and $l$ rather than $n$ alone (\autoref{core:hydrogenic}), here it \textbf{unevenly distributes energy across the five d orbitals}, pushing some to higher energy levels than others.

      \myfig{0.6}{quantization_figures/cft.png}{Crystal Field Theory d orbital energy level splitting}{fig:placeholder24}

      Crucially, the magnitude of this d orbital energy splitting in transition metals generally falls \textbf{within the energy range corresponding to visible light wavelengths}. This is not the case for non-transition metals, where electronic transitions span distinct orbital shells and subshells, producing energy differences far too large to fall within the visible spectrum and corresponding instead to much shorter wavelengths.

      Underlying all of these interactions is the concept of \textbf{effective nuclear charge} ($Z_{eff}$), introduced briefly in EXTERNAL REFERENCE in the context of electronegativity. It also governs the shielding effect, whereby inner electrons occupy the space between the nucleus and outer electrons, repelling the outer ones and reducing the net nuclear attraction they experience---contributing directly to the skewing of orbital energy levels shown in \autoref{fig:placeholder24}.

      It should be noted that the treatment above is a simplified overview of Crystal Field Theory. A more complete picture accounts for the nature of the specific ligands present and the geometry of the coordination compound, both of which influence the magnitude and ordering of d orbital splitting.

    \end{core}

\section{Orbital Bonding}
  
  \subsection{Sigma/Pi \& Bonding/Antibonding Orbitals}

    \begin{core}{Orbital Wave Overlap}

      Bonding is favorable because it results in a lower energy state, as discussed in EXTERNAL REFERENCE. Now that we have established that orbitals represent electron density, we can explain the physical origin of this energy drop from both an electrostatic and a wave mechanical perspective.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item From an \textbf{electrostatic standpoint}, when two atoms bond, electron density accumulates directly between two positive nuclei. Rather than being attracted to a single nucleus, each \ce{e^-} is now simultaneously attracted to two — this bridge of \textbf{negative charge pulling on both positive centers lowers the overall PE of the system}.

        \item From a \textbf{wave mechanical standpoint}, bonding can be understood through the \textbf{de Broglie relationship}, which is represented by the following equation.
        \[
          λ = \frac{h}{mv}
        \]

        This equation links the wavelength of an \ce{e^-} to its momentum and thus its KE. When two atomic wave functions overlap during bond formation, they undergo interference---\textbf{constructive interference} in the bonding region increases the combined wavelength of the resulting wave function, and since a longer wavelength corresponds to lower frequency and slower effective motion, the KE of the \ce{e^-} is reduced.

        \myfig{0.45}{quantization_figures/superposition.png}{Constructive and destructive interference as a result of wave superposition}{fig:placeholder25}

        This is equivalent to enlarging the "box" that the \ce{e^-} standing waves inhabit---a larger confining region permits a longer standing wave, recalling that the fundamental standing wave wavelength is twice the length of its confinement ($λ = 2L$).

        \myfig{0.5}{quantization_figures/wave_overlap.png}{Molecular orbital formation wave overlap}{fig:placeholder26}

      \end{enumerate}

      Together, the reduction in both PE and KE is sufficient to overcome the nuclear-nuclear repulsion at the equilibrium bond length, making bond formation energetically favorable overall.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{σ and π Bonds}

      Electron orbital overlap gives rise to two types of bonds, distinguished by the axis along which the overlap occurs: σ bonds (\textit{head-on overlap}) and π bonds (\textit{side-to-side overlap}).

      \myfig{0.45}{quantization_figures/sigma_pi.png}{σ and π bonding interactions}{fig:placeholder27}

      It is important to note that σ and π do not refer to the s and p orbital types---they refer exclusively to \textbf{the geometry of the overlap}. A σ bond, for instance, can form between any combination of orbital types (s-s, s-p, p-p, etc.). For the scope of these notes, we will restrict our discussion to s and p orbital interactions, as d and f orbital bonding lies beyond the current scope.

      \begin{enumerate}

        \item A \textbf{σ bond} forms when two orbitals overlap directly along the internuclear axis---that is, head-on from the center of one atom to the center of the other. The various orbital combinations that can produce a σ bond are shown in \autoref{fig:placeholder28}.

        \myfig{0.6}{quantization_figures/sigma_bonds.png}{σ bond formation from various orbital overlap combinations}{fig:placeholder28}

        \item A \textbf{π bond}, by contrast, forms when two orbitals overlap perpendicular to the internuclear axis. While various orbital combinations can produce a π bond, the most common involves two p orbitals overlapping side-to-side, as shown in \autoref{fig:placeholder29}.

        \myfig{0.45}{quantization_figures/pi_bonds.png}{π bond formation from p orbital overlap}{fig:placeholder29}

      \end{enumerate}

      With this framework in place, single bonds consist of one σ interaction, while double and triple bonds layer one or two π bonds on top of the initial σ framework. This will become clearer in the context of \autoref{sec:diatomic}.

    \end{core}
 
    \begin{core}{Antibonding Orbitals \& Quantum Superposition}

      Every orbital overlap that produces a \textbf{bonding orbital} simultaneously produces a corresponding \textbf{anti-bonding orbital}, denoted with an asterisk (σ$^*$ or π$^*$). This is a consequence of \textbf{quantum superposition}---the overlapping wave functions do not simply choose between constructive and destructive interference; rather, the mathematical space defined by the two overlapping atoms permits both modes of vibration to exist \textbf{simultaneously} as distinct solutions.

      \myfig{0.4}{quantization_figures/anti_s.png}{σ bonding and antibonding orbitals}{fig:placeholder30}

      This is formally captured by the \textbf{Linear Combination of Atomic Orbitals (LCAO)} theory, which requires that the \textbf{total number of orbitals be conserved}, meaning that if you begin with two atomic orbitals, you must end with two molecular orbitals---one bonding and one anti-bonding. The anti-bonding orbital carries an additional node relative to its bonding counterpart, placing it at a higher energy level. As a result, electrons fill the bonding orbital preferentially before occupying the anti-bonding orbital, consistent with the \textbf{Aufbau principle} discussed in \autoref{core:aufbau}.

      The σ bonding and anti-bonding orbitals are shown in \autoref{fig:placeholder30}, and their π counterparts in \autoref{fig:placeholder31}.

      \myfig{0.65}{quantization_figures/anti_p.png}{π bonding and antibonding orbitals}{fig:placeholder31}

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Diatomic Orbital Bonding}
  \label{sec:diatomic}

    When two atoms are bonded, the orbitals that primarily engage in this interaction are the valence orbitals, which are the highest energy occupied orbitals with unpaired \ce{e^-}s.
    
    \begin{core}{Homogenous Diatomic Molecular Orbital Bonding (\textit{Single Bonds})}

      Let us look at the simplest example of an orbital overlap resulting in a single bond across \ce{H2}:

      \myfig{0.35}{quantization_figures/h2_mod.png}{Molecular orbital diagram for \ce{H2}}{fig:placeholder32}

      Here the highest energy occupied orbital is simply the 1s orbital in each H atom, which combine to form a low energy bonding orbital $\sigma$ and a high energy antibonding orbital $\sigma^*$. The two \ce{e^-}s of the two H atoms will then go onto occupy the $\sigma$ orbital and the antibonding orbital will remain empty.

      We now introduce the calculation of \textbf{bond order}. \textbf{Bond order} is a numerical value that represents the stability and strength of a chemical bond by calculating the "net" number of bonds between two atoms.

      \[
        \text{Bond Order} = \frac{\text{Number of bonding \ce{e^-}} - \text{Number of antibonding \ce{e^-}}}{2}
      \]

      For \ce{H2}, the bond order would be 1, representing a single bond. A 0 bond order would indcate that the bond is unstable and would thus not form. Take a look at the molecular orbital diagram for \ce{He2}.

      \myfig{0.35}{quantization_figures/he2_mod.png}{Molecular orbital diagram for He2}{fig:placeholder33}

      The bond order comes out to be 0. And conceptually the presence of \ce{e^-} occupation within the antibonding orbital will cancel out the bonding orbital, thus resulting in no bond. This aligns with reality as \ce{He2} does not exist naturally.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Homogenous Diatomic Molecular Orbital Bonding (\textit{Double Bonds})}

      Now, let us look at an example of the orbital overlap of the double bond across \ce{O2}:

      \myfig{0.6}{quantization_figures/o2_mod.png}{Molecular orbital diagram for O2}{fig:placeholder34}

      Lower level 1s and 2s orbitals will overlap to form fully occupied bonding and antibonding orbitals $\sigma$ and $\sigma^*$, thus canceling out and not forming a bond. This makes sense because these are not the valence orbitals (2p). The 2p orbitals exist in 3 different orientations, and the overlap of those that exist along the bonding axis will be responsible for the formation of the $\sigma$ and $\sigma^*$ orbitals. Those that exist along the axis perpendicular to that of the bonding will be responsible for $\pi$ and $\pi^*$ orbitals. As shown in the diagram, when we fill in the overlapped orbitals according to the Aufbau principle, all 3 bonding orbitals are filled and one \ce{e^-} is place in the $\pi^*$ orbitals each, resulting in a bond order of 2.
      \[
        \text{Bond Order} = \frac{6-2}{2} = 2
      \]

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Homogenous Diatomic Molecular Orbital Bonding (\textit{Triple Bonds})}

      Lastly, let us look at the molecular orbital diagram of the triple bond across \ce{N2}:

      \myfig{0.6}{quantization_figures/n2_mod.png}{Molecular orbital diagram for N2}{fig:placeholder35}

      Here, the molecular orbital structure works in exactly the same manner as it did in \ce{O2}, except that no \ce{e^-}s are alotted to the antibonding orbitals. This results in a bond order of 3, hence the triple bond.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Heterogenous Diatomic Molecular Orbital Bonding}

      So far, we have only seen examples of homogenous diatomic molecular orbital bonding. Let us now take a look at a heterogeneous diatomic interation: HF

      \myfig{0.5}{quantization_figures/hf_mod.png}{Molecular orbital diagram for HF}{fig:placeholder36}

      Here, the only bond that is able to be made is a $\sigma$ orbital between the 1s of the H and the 2p of the F that exists along the bonding axis. This is a very simple heterogenous bonding interaction. How about something like FCl? which has several lower level core orbitals present?

      \myfig{0.5}{quantization_figures/clf_mod.png}{Molecular orbital diagram for ClF}{fig:placeholder37}

      Still, in this case, the \textbf{bonding interactions only occur between the incompletely occupied valence orbitals}, which is a 2p orbital of F and 3p orbital of Cl. Although lower level orbitals are presently forming bonding and cooresponding antibonding molecular orbitals (most definetely $\sigma$ orbitals because the only lower level orbitals present in F are 1s and 2s), they do not effect the molecular orbital structure of the valence \ce{e^-} because they have a net 0 bond order. Likewise, although core shells do overlap to some infinitesimal degree when any two atoms are close, this interaction is so tiny for core \ce{e^-}s that we treat them as if they don't interact at all.

    \end{core} 

    \begin{core}{S-P Mixing (\textit{Periodic Table 2nd Row})}

      Now let us discuss the unique phenomenon of \textbf{s-p mixing} along the diatomic molecules of the second row of the periodic table. This phenomenon is based on the delicate balance between \ce{e^-} \ce{e^-} and \ce{e^-} nuclear attraction that occur for atoms at this nuclear range and results in an anomaly for smaller atoms in regards to the $\sigma_{2p}$/$\pi_{2p}$ energy level orderings.

      For lighter diatomic molecules \ce{B2}, \ce{C2}, and \ce{N2}, since the atom has less effective nuclear charge due to less protons, the energy gap between the 2s and 2p orbital is small enough that mixing is significant and results in the $\sigma_{2p}$ being pushed higher in the energy hierarchy compared to the $\pi_{2p}$. The previously shown molecular orbital diagram of \ce{N2} does not account for this phenomenon. A more accurate representation of the molecular orbital diagram of \ce{N2} is shown below.
      \myfig{0.5}{quantization_figures/n2_mod_correct.png}{Corrected molecular orbital diagram for N2}{fig:placeholder38}

      In larger diatomic molecules \ce{O2} and \ce{F2}, this anomaly does not occur due to the sufficient effective nuclear charge strong enough to prevent the 2s and 2p orbitals from mixing. Therefore, the $\pi_{2p}$ energy level remains higher than that of $\sigma_{2p}$. \textit{The previously shown molecular orbital diagram of \ce{O2} is correct.}

      So, why is this important? There are actually two large practical consequences of this s-p mixing interaction.

      First is \textbf{the stability of \ce{N2}}. Because nitrogen's 2s and 2p orbitals are close in energy, s-p mixing is very strong. This mixing pushes the $\sigma_{2s}$ orbital lower in energy and the $\sigma_{2p}$ higher. By lowering the energy of the $\sigma_{2s}$ bonding orbital, the entire molecule becomes significantly more stable. This is why \ce{N2} is an exceptionally unreactive gas that composes the majority of the earth's atmosphere. Without s-p mixing, \ce{N2} may be more reactive, potentially making life as we know it impossible.

      Second is regarding magnetism prediction (specifically regarding \ce{B2}). 

    \end{core}
    
    \begin{core}{Diamagnetism \& Paramagnetism}

      Here we introduce a two new concepts: \textbf{diamagnetism} and \textbf{paramagnetism}, which refers to not being and being attracted to a magnetic field, respectively. Whether a molecule is diamagnetic or paramagnetic is dependent on the \textbf{presence} (paramagnetic) or \textbf{absence} (diamagnetic) of unpaired \ce{e^-}s in \ce{e^-} orbitals. 
      \myfig{0.8}{quantization_figures/magnetism.png}{Molecular orbital theory and magnetism}{fig:placeholder39}

      To understand why unpaired \ce{e^-} in orbitals lead to magnetism, we must understand \textbf{spin}. Mentioned briefly through the spin quantum number ($m_s$) in \textit{4.2.2 Electron Orbital Basics}, spin refers to an intrinsic property called spin angular momentum that creates a tiny magnetic field around the \ce{e^-} in one direction (\textit{up or down}). In the presence of a partner \ce{e^-} with the opposite spin, these magnetic fields cancel out, resulting in a net magnetic field of zero. In the case of an unpaired \ce{e^-}, there is no opposing spin to cancel its magnetic field, thus the magnetic moment is maintained.

      Now let's take a look at the molecular orbital diagram of \ce{B2}.
      \myfig{0.5}{quantization_figures/b2_mod.png}{Molecular orbital diagram for B2}{fig:placeholder40}

      As we can see, the paramagnetism of \ce{B2} is a direct consequence of s-p mixing. If s-p mixing was absent, the two valence \ce{e^-}s would be fully occupying the $\sigma_{2p}$ orbital in opposite spin states, resulting in diamagnetism. However, since the $\pi_{2p}$ orbitals stand at a lower energy level, these valence \ce{e^-}s are instead forced to occupy these two molecular orbitals in the same spin state, resulting in paramagnetism.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Bonding \& Antibonding Orbital Energy Split}

      Moving on to another topic, you may be wondering what determines the \textbf{SIZE OF THE ENERGY SPLIT} between bonding and antibonding molecular orbitals. This is factor is determined by the \textbf{overlap between the two relevant orbitals}. A stronger overlap means larger splitting, and several factors contribute to stronger overlaps. 

      (1) $\sigma$ bonds have a larger overlap relative to $\pi$ bonds because they interact along the bond axis.

      (2) Smaller orbitals ($n=2$) overlaps more intensely compared to larger orbitals ($n=5$) due to \ce{e^-} density, nuclei proximity, and distance differences between orbital sizes.

      For example, the first excitation energy (the energy it takes to excite an \ce{e^-} from its highest occupied molecular orbital to its lowest unoccupied molecular orbital \textit{or HOMO to LUMO}) among homogenous diatomic halogens (\ce{F2}, \ce{Cl2}, \ce{Br2}, \ce{I2}) increases as you go up the periodic table. In other words,

      \[
        E_{\ce{F2}} > E_{\ce{Cl2}} > E_{\ce{Br2}} > E_{\ce{I2}}
      \]

      (3) Smaller differences in energy between atomic orbitals lead to greater overlap whereas larger differences lead to less because the EN differences cause a more one-sided skew in \ce{e^-} density compared to a more uniform delocalization. 

      As a result, homonuclear bonds tend to be more stable than heteronuclear bonds and hence the ionization energy (IE) of heteronuclear bonds is generally lower than homonuclear bonds.

      For instance, the IE of \ce{CO} and \ce{N2} are roughly 14eV and 15.6eV, respectively, despite their molecular orbital diagrams being virtually identical.
      \myfig{0.5}{quantization_figures/n2_mod_correct.png}{Corrected molecular orbital diagram for N2}{fig:placeholder41}

      In another instance, the IE of \ce{O2} and \ce{NO} are roughly 12eV and 9.2eV, respectively, despite their HOMO both being $\pi^*_{2p}$.

    \end{core}

  \subsection{Orbital Hybridization}
    
    \begin{core}{Orbital Hydridization}

      \textbf{Orbital hybridization} is the mixing of orbitals to create a new set of orbtials. The resulting count of orbitals is unchanged and are still orthogonal to one another. This is the "correction factor" for the mismatch between how atomic orbitals looked in isolation (as we have covered so far) and how they actually behave when interacting with multiple bonding orbitals simultaneously.

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{$sp$ Hybridization}

      \myfig{0.75}{quantization_figures/sp.png}{sp hybridization showing orbital mixing}{fig:placeholder42}

      Above is the simplest example of orbital hybridization, $sp$, is made from the mixing of an s and p orbital, which is used to make two $\sigma$ bonds and/or lone pairs at a 180$^\circ$ angle from each other. A good representative example of $sp$ hybridization is \ce{HCN}.

      \myfig{0.6}{quantization_figures/HCN.png}{HCN molecule showing sp hybridization}{fig:placeholder43}

      Result: $(1s)^4(\sigma_{CH})^2(\sigma_{CN})^2(\pi_{CN})^4(n_{N})^2$

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{$sp^2$ Hybridization}

      $sp^2$ hybridization results from the mixing of one s and two p orbitals. Each $sp^2$ orbital points at one vertex of an equilateral triangle and used to make 3 $\sigma$ bonds and/or lone pairs at roughly 120$^\circ$ angles from each other (the leftover p orbital can be used for a $\pi$ bond). A good representative example of $sp^2$ hybridization is Ethylene \ce{CH4}.

      \myfig{0.65}{quantization_figures/ethylene.png}{Ethylene molecule showing sp2 hybridization}{fig:placeholder44}

      Result: $(1s)^4(\sigma_{CH})^8(\sigma_{CC})^2(\pi_{CC})^2$

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{$sp^3$ Hybridization}

      $sp^3$ hybridization results from the mixing of one s and three p orbitals, forming a tetrahedron that is used to make four $\sigma$ bonds and/or lone pairs in a tetrahedral arrangement. 

      \myfig{0.55}{quantization_figures/ch3f.png}{CH3F molecule orbital diagram}{fig:placeholder45}

      Result: $(1s)^4(\sigma_{CH})^6(\sigma_{CF})^2(n_{F})^6$

    \end{core}

    \textit{It's also important to ponder why orbital hybridization occurs.} Like we have discussed throughout this entire course, bonding interactions will occur in a manner that \textbf{minimizes the PE of a system}. How orbital hybridization follows this tendency comes back down to the greater energy differences between the bonding and antibonding molecular orbitals as greater overlap occurs. Hybrid orbitals are "lopsided" --- they have one very large lobe and one tiny lobe. This \textbf{large lobe extends much further into space} than a standard s or p orbital ever could, \textbf{leading to more overlap} when interacting with other atomic orbitals and thus minimizing the PE of the bonding system.

    \begin{core}{Light \& HOMO LUMO}
      
      Let's now revisit a topic briefly mentioned in \textit{4.3.3 Orbital Hybridization}: the \textbf{HOMO LUMO gap}.

      HOMO and LUMO stands for \textbf{highest occupied molecular orbital} and \textbf{lowest unoccupied molecular orbital}. Let's take a look at a previously shown molecular orbital diagram for \ce{O2}.

      \myfig{0.7}{quantization_figures/o2_mod.png}{Molecular orbital diagram for O2}{fig:placeholder49}

      In this scenario, the HOMO and LUMO would be the $\pi^*_{2p}$ and $\sigma^*_{2p}$ molecular orbitals, respectively. The HOMO LUMO gap represents the energy it takes to excite one \ce{e^-} in the HOMO to the LUMO. Therefore, the size of this gap determines what wavelength of light it would absorb. Take a look at this diagram representing halogen (\textit{column 17 of the periodic table}) HOMO LUMO transitions that we have discussed previously.

      \myfig{0.8}{quantization_figures/halogen_homo_lumo.png}{HOMO-LUMO orbitals in halogens}{fig:placeholder50}

      As the HOMO LUMO gap increases, the energy required to excite the HOMO \ce{e^-} increases, thus the wavelength of light it absorbs decreases. \textit{The light source we shine in this model refers to white light (such as sunlight or LEDs), which is a mixture of different wavelengths spanning the visible spectrum.} Therefore, what we visually see are the rest of the wavelengths of light that the halogens didn't absorb and instead reflects, summing up the complementary color. For instance, the HOMO LUMO gap for \ce{I2} cooresponds to the wavelength around green and yellow, thus the complementary color we see is purple. This same process can be seen for \ce{Cl2} and \ce{Br2} as well. In the case of \ce{F2}, the wavelength lands in the UV spectrum, thus all of the wavelengths are reflected and we see a relatively more transparent color (but still pale yellow because it slightly absorbs a portion of violet wavelengths). Here is a real visual of these gases in the order \ce{F2}, \ce{Cl2}, \ce{Br2}, and \ce{I2}.

      \myfig{0.6}{quantization_figures/halogen_gases.png}{Halogen molecular orbital diagrams}{fig:placeholder51}

      Combining our lessons on delocalization and the HOMO LUMO gap, we observe that the HOMO LUMO gap decreases under greater delocalization. This feels counterintuitive based on our understanding that delocalization stablizes the system (\textit{more overlap}). However, the overall energy difference intervals between molecular orbital levels of the conjugated $\pi$-system get smaller despite the difference between the top and bottom levels getting larger as delocalization increases. 

      This is nicely represented in the figure below showing the molecular orbital diagrams for the conjugated $\pi$-systems of ethene (\textit{two p orbitals}) and butadiene (\textit{four p orbitals}).

      \myfig{0.6}{quantization_figures/homo_lumo_delocalization.png}{HOMO-LUMO delocalization in molecules}{fig:placeholder52}

    \end{core}

    \begin{core}{Proton Nuclear Magnetic Resonance}
      
      Okay, last topic. Just as \ce{e^-}s, \ce{p^+}s also have a spin. In the absence of an external magnetic field, the direction of these spins are random. However, in the presence of a magnetic field, the direction of these spins orient with the field (parallel, lower energy) or against it (anti-parallel, higher energy), creating an energy gap between the two states that increases in magnitude as the external magnetic field strength increases.
